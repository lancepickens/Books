Chapter I

Geometry as the Language of Physics

Modern physics rests on two geometric pictures that almost — but not quite — fit together. String theory's opening move is to take geometry one step further: replace the moving point with a moving line.

§1Two geometric languages

Twentieth-century physics produced two extraordinary theories, each unmistakably geometric in form.

General relativity says that spacetime is a four-dimensional smooth manifold $M$ equipped with a Lorentzian metric $g_{\mu\nu}$. Gravity is the curvature of that metric. A freely falling particle does the laziest possible thing in this geometry: it follows a geodesic. Matter and energy bend the manifold through Einstein's equation,

$$ R_{\mu\nu} - \tfrac{1}{2} R\, g_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda\, g_{\mu\nu} \;=\; \frac{8\pi G}{c^4}\, T_{\mu\nu}. $$

Where

$R_{\mu\nu}$
Ricci curvature tensor — a trace of the full Riemann curvature; tells you how a small ball of test particles changes shape under tidal forces.
$R$
Ricci scalar, $R = g^{\mu\nu} R_{\mu\nu}$ — the trace of the Ricci tensor.
$g_{\mu\nu}$
Spacetime metric, signature $(-,+,+,+)$.
$\Lambda$
Cosmological constant.
$T_{\mu\nu}$
Stress–energy tensor — how matter and energy are distributed and flowing.
$G,\,c$
Newton's constant; speed of light.

Geometry here is not a description added on top of gravity. Geometry is the gravitational field.

Quantum field theory looks less geometric at first glance, but the structures underneath are also geometric. Matter fields are sections of vector bundles over spacetime. Gauge fields are connections on principal bundles. The Yang–Mills field strength

$$ F_{\mu\nu}^a \;=\; \partial_\mu A_\nu^a - \partial_\nu A_\mu^a + g\, f^{abc}\, A_\mu^b A_\nu^c $$

is the curvature 2-form of the connection $A_\mu^a$. Anomalies — obstructions to quantizing classical symmetries — are characteristic classes. The Standard Model is, in effect, fiber-bundle geometry over flat Minkowski spacetime.

Two languages. Both are geometry. Both work spectacularly — general relativity has passed every test from gravitational waves to event-horizon imaging, and the Standard Model agrees with experiment to ten or more decimal places. Each works in a domain the other can ignore.

§2Where the geometries clash

Trouble appears when both are needed at once: at very high energies, or equivalently at very short distances, gravity itself must be quantum.

The fundamental length scale of quantum gravity is the Planck length,

$$ \ell_P \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{\hbar\, G}{c^3}} \;\approx\; 1.616 \times 10^{-35}\ \mathrm{m}. $$

Where

$\hbar$
Reduced Planck constant $h/(2\pi)$.
$G$
Newton's gravitational constant.
$c$
Speed of light in vacuum.

Three constants, one length. It's the unique scale you can build from $\hbar$ (quantum), $G$ (gravity), and $c$ (relativity), so it's a natural guess for where all three matter at once. At scales near $\ell_P$ the metric is no longer a fixed smooth field — its quantum fluctuations are themselves of order one.

Treating gravitons (the hypothetical quanta of $g_{\mu\nu}$) as point-particle excitations and computing their scattering the usual way produces divergences that no finite list of counterterms can absorb. We say gravity is perturbatively non-renormalizable. Geometrically, the Feynman-diagram vertex — a single spacetime point where lines meet — is too sharp: it concentrates an unbounded amount of curvature at one point.

Renormalization, in one paragraph

A quantum field theory is renormalizable if its short-distance infinities can be absorbed by adjusting a finite number of parameters in the Lagrangian. The Standard Model is renormalizable. Gravity, treated as a QFT of point gravitons, is not — at every order in perturbation theory new infinities appear, and absorbing them would require infinitely many parameters, which is no theory at all.

This isn't a proof that gravity cannot be a quantum field theory. It's a strong hint that the point-particle picture must break down somewhere on the way to the Planck scale.

§3The bold geometric move

String theory's foundational move is a single sentence:

Replace point particles, which sweep out 1-dimensional worldlines, with strings, which sweep out 2-dimensional worldsheets.

A point particle has no intrinsic length. A string has a length scale $\ell_s = \sqrt{\alpha'}$ (the string length), and that length scale smears out the Feynman vertex: interactions happen along a smooth 2-surface, not at a point. Whatever was diverging at a point now has a finite size to spread over.

Several remarkable consequences follow from this small change, none of which were anticipated when string theory was first written down as a model of hadrons in the late 1960s.

A massless spin-2 mode appears. Among the quantized vibrations of a closed string sits a state of spin 2 and zero mass. Weinberg showed in 1965 that any consistent theory interacting via a massless spin-2 particle must reduce, at low energies, to general relativity. So the graviton is not put in by hand; it falls out, and with it, Einstein's equations as a low-energy approximation.

The dimension of spacetime is fixed. Consistency of the quantum theory only works in particular target-space dimensions: 26 for bosonic strings, 10 for superstrings. We are not free to choose. The number comes out of demanding that a certain anomaly on the worldsheet cancels — a beautiful geometric story we will reach in Chapter VIII.

Spacetime geometry itself loosens. A closed string wrapped around a circle of radius $R$ is physically indistinguishable from one wrapped around a circle of radius $\alpha'/R$ — this is T-duality. Large and small geometries are interchangeable. There is no "shortest" length you can probe with a string; below $\ell_s$, geometry stops being what we thought it was.

§4What we'll mean by "geometric"

Throughout this book we'll keep four geometric pictures in mind. Each is a different manifold (or family of manifolds), and almost everything we encounter will be a statement about one of them.

  1. Target-space geometry — the spacetime $M$ in which the string moves. Initially flat Minkowski; later compactified, with six dimensions curled up into a small internal manifold.
  2. Worldsheet geometry — the 2-dimensional surface $\Sigma$ traced out by the string, together with its intrinsic metric $h_{ab}$ and its conformal structure.
  3. Moduli space geometry — the space of allowed shapes and sizes of compactification manifolds. Coupling constants are coordinates on this space.
  4. Bundle and brane geometry — gauge fields as connections on bundles over $M$, and the submanifolds (D-branes) on which open strings end.

Wherever we meet an equation we will read it geometrically first: is this an area, a curvature, a Laplacian, a parallel-transport equation? Symbol-pushing comes second.

§5A glimpse of the central equation

So you can see where we are heading, here is the simplest form of the bosonic string action — the Polyakov action (in natural units $\hbar = c = 1$, which we will use throughout):

$$ S_P[X,h] \;=\; -\frac{1}{4\pi\alpha'} \int_{\Sigma} \mathrm{d}^2\sigma\, \sqrt{-h}\, h^{ab}\, \partial_a X^\mu\, \partial_b X^\nu\, \eta_{\mu\nu}. $$

Where

$\Sigma$
The worldsheet — a 2-dim surface with local coordinates $\sigma^a = (\tau, \sigma)$, $a = 0, 1$.
$X^\mu(\sigma^a)$
The embedding map: it says where each point of $\Sigma$ sits in target spacetime. $\mu = 0,1,\dots,D-1$.
$h_{ab}(\sigma^a)$
Intrinsic Lorentzian metric on the worldsheet; $h = \det h_{ab}$.
$\eta_{\mu\nu}$
Flat Minkowski metric on target spacetime, signature $(-,+,+,\dots)$.
$\alpha'$
The Regge slope — sets the string length, $\ell_s = \sqrt{\alpha'}$. The string tension is $T = 1/(2\pi\alpha')$.

Don't try to parse all of it now. Notice instead the shape of it. It is an integral over a 2-dimensional surface $\Sigma$ of a quantity built from a worldsheet metric $h_{ab}$ and the embedding maps $X^\mu$. It is to a string what the geodesic action

$$ S_{\text{particle}} \;=\; -m \int \mathrm{d}s \;=\; -m \int \mathrm{d}\tau\, \sqrt{-\dot X^\mu \dot X^\nu \eta_{\mu\nu}} $$

is to a particle: a geometric measure of the swept-out region, weighted by a tension or mass. Everything that follows — quantization, the critical dimension, the graviton, dualities, branes, black-hole entropy — is built on top of this single integral. By the end of Chapter IV every symbol above will be familiar.

§7The road ahead

Fifteen chapters in total. After this one we will:

  1. Set up the geometric toolkit — manifolds, metrics, geodesics, the action principle (Ch. II).
  2. Solve the relativistic point particle as a warm-up (Ch. III).
  3. Write down the string action and read it geometrically (Ch. IV).
  4. Understand its symmetries — diffeomorphism and Weyl invariance — and gauge-fix them (Ch. V).
  5. Solve the classical equations of motion and find that boundary conditions give us D-branes for free (Ch. VI).
  6. Quantize, meet the Virasoro algebra, and see the conformal anomaly (Ch. VII).
  7. Derive the critical dimension from the anomaly (Ch. VIII).
  8. Put the string on a curved spacetime and watch Einstein's equations re-emerge as a consistency condition (Ch. IX).
  9. Add fermions — superstrings, the GSO projection, $D=10$ (Ch. X).
  10. Compactify, meeting Calabi–Yau manifolds (Ch. XI), dualities (Ch. XII), and D-branes (Ch. XIII).
  11. Recover the Bekenstein–Hawking entropy from brane microstate counting (Ch. XIV).
  12. End at the AdS/CFT correspondence — the deepest geometric statement string theory has so far produced (Ch. XV).

None of this is a claim that string theory is empirically correct. It is a working theory, internally consistent over remarkable distances, and rich enough to have changed how we think about geometry itself. That is enough reason to take the climb seriously.
