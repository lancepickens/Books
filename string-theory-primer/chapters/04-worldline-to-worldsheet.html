Chapter IV

From Worldline to Worldsheet

Promote the point particle's 1-dimensional worldline to a string's 2-dimensional worldsheet. The action becomes an area; the einbein becomes a worldsheet metric. The geometry is the only thing that changes.

§1A string sweeps out a surface

A point particle is a 0-dimensional object; through time it traces a 1-dimensional worldline. A string is a 1-dimensional object — a small curve — that, through time, sweeps out a 2-dimensional surface called the worldsheet, denoted $\Sigma$.

We describe the worldsheet by an embedding map

$$ X^\mu : \Sigma \to M, \qquad (\tau, \sigma) \mapsto X^\mu(\tau, \sigma), $$

where $(\tau, \sigma)$ are coordinates on $\Sigma$ and $M$ is target spacetime. $\tau$ is the worldsheet "time" direction, $\sigma$ the "spatial" coordinate along the string. From $\Sigma$'s point of view, $X^\mu$ is a collection of $D$ scalar fields (one per target dimension) defined on a 2-manifold.

§2Open and closed strings

The string itself can be one of two topologically distinct objects:

  • Closed — a loop. The worldsheet coordinate $\sigma$ runs over $[0, 2\pi)$ with the periodicity $X^\mu(\tau, \sigma + 2\pi) = X^\mu(\tau, \sigma)$. The worldsheet $\Sigma$ is a cylinder.
  • Open — a segment with two free ends. $\sigma$ runs over $[0, \pi]$, and the boundary conditions at $\sigma = 0, \pi$ (Neumann or Dirichlet) will become important in Chapter VI. The worldsheet $\Sigma$ is a strip.

The two topologies host genuinely different physics. Closed strings give the graviton; open strings give gauge fields. In the modern picture, open and closed strings coexist, with open-string endpoints anchored on D-branes (Chapter XIII).

§3The induced metric

Target spacetime $M$ carries a metric $\eta_{\mu\nu}$ (flat Minkowski for now; curved later). This metric pulls back to the worldsheet through the embedding $X^\mu$, producing the induced metric

$$ \gamma_{ab}(\tau, \sigma) \;=\; \partial_a X^\mu\, \partial_b X^\nu\, \eta_{\mu\nu}, \qquad a, b \in \{0, 1\} \equiv \{\tau, \sigma\}. $$

Where

$\partial_a$
Derivative with respect to worldsheet coordinate $\sigma^a$; $\partial_0 = \partial/\partial\tau$, $\partial_1 = \partial/\partial\sigma$.
$\gamma_{ab}$
The induced metric — a 2$\times$2 symmetric matrix at each point of $\Sigma$, with Lorentzian signature $(-,+)$ if the worldsheet is timelike (which we will always assume).

Geometrically, $\gamma_{ab}$ measures lengths and angles on the worldsheet as inherited from the way $\Sigma$ sits inside spacetime. A small displacement $(d\tau, d\sigma)$ on $\Sigma$ corresponds to a spacetime displacement $dX^\mu = \partial_a X^\mu\, d\sigma^a$, whose proper length squared is $\eta_{\mu\nu} dX^\mu dX^\nu = \gamma_{ab}\, d\sigma^a\, d\sigma^b$.

§4The Nambu–Goto action

The natural geometric quantity attached to a 2-surface is its area. For a worldsheet with induced metric $\gamma_{ab}$, the proper area is

$$ A[\Sigma] \;=\; \int_\Sigma \mathrm{d}^2\sigma\, \sqrt{-\det \gamma_{ab}}. $$

The minus sign sits there for the same reason as in proper time: with Lorentzian signature, $\det \gamma < 0$ for a timelike worldsheet, so $-\det \gamma > 0$. This is the worldsheet generalization of the proper-time integral.

The Nambu–Goto action is $-T$ times this area:

$$ \boxed{\; S_{\mathrm{NG}}[X] \;=\; -T \int_\Sigma \mathrm{d}^2\sigma\, \sqrt{-\det\!\left(\partial_a X^\mu\, \partial_b X^\nu\, \eta_{\mu\nu}\right)}. \;} $$

Where

$T$
String tension — energy per unit length. Conventionally $T = 1/(2\pi\alpha')$, with $\alpha'$ the Regge slope and $\ell_s = \sqrt{\alpha'}$ the string length.

This is the exact analogue of $S_{\mathrm{pp}} = -m \int \mathrm{d}s$ for the particle, one dimension up. Mass becomes tension; proper length becomes proper area. The geometry is what counts.

Nambu wrote this action down in 1970; Goto fixed conventions and extended it shortly afterward. It is geometrically pristine, but it has the same drawback as the particle's square-root action: a square root is awkward to quantize.

§5The Polyakov form

We apply the same trick as for the einbein. Introduce an independent dynamical field $h_{ab}(\tau, \sigma)$ on $\Sigma$ — a worldsheet metric, the 2-dim analogue of the einbein — and consider the alternative action

$$ \boxed{\; S_P[X, h] \;=\; -\frac{T}{2} \int_\Sigma \mathrm{d}^2\sigma\, \sqrt{-h}\, h^{ab}\, \partial_a X^\mu\, \partial_b X^\nu\, \eta_{\mu\nu}. \;} $$

This is the Polyakov action, written down in this form by Brink, Di Vecchia, Howe, and (independently) Deser & Zumino in 1976, and put to spectacular use by Polyakov in 1981 to compute the string path integral. With $T = 1/(2\pi\alpha')$, the prefactor is the $-1/(4\pi\alpha')$ that appears in every textbook.

Where

$h_{ab}$
An independent symmetric, Lorentzian-signature metric on the worldsheet. Three independent components: $h_{00}, h_{01}, h_{11}$.
$h$
Determinant of $h_{ab}$.
$h^{ab}$
Matrix inverse of $h_{ab}$.

$S_P$ is the worldsheet version of the einbein action — quadratic in the embedding fields $X^\mu$, so well-suited to canonical quantization and path integrals. The worldsheet metric $h_{ab}$ is the worldsheet analogue of the einbein $e$.

§6Classical equivalence to Nambu–Goto

Vary $S_P$ with respect to $h_{ab}$. The equation of motion reads

$$ T_{ab} \;\equiv\; \partial_a X^\mu\, \partial_b X_\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\, h_{ab}\, h^{cd}\, \partial_c X^\mu\, \partial_d X_\mu \;=\; 0, $$

where $T_{ab}$ is the (worldsheet) stress-energy tensor. This says: the induced metric $\gamma_{ab} = \partial_a X^\mu \partial_b X_\mu$ is proportional to $h_{ab}$ via the equation

$$ \gamma_{ab} \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\, h_{ab}\, h^{cd}\, \gamma_{cd}. $$

Tracing both sides with $h^{ab}$ shows the proportionality factor is itself $h^{cd}\gamma_{cd}$. Substituting $h_{ab} = (\text{positive function}) \cdot \gamma_{ab}$ back into $S_P$ cancels the Weyl factor (because $\sqrt{-h}\, h^{ab}$ is Weyl-invariant in 2-d) and recovers $S_{\mathrm{NG}}$:

$$ S_P\big[X, h_{\star}(X)\big] \;=\; S_{\mathrm{NG}}[X]. $$

So Polyakov and Nambu–Goto are classically equivalent: every solution of one is a solution of the other. Quantum-mechanically the Polyakov form is the gateway: it linearizes in $X^\mu$ and makes the worldsheet symmetries manifest, both of which the square root hides.

The 2-dimensional miracle

That $\sqrt{-h}\, h^{ab}$ is Weyl-invariant — $h_{ab} \to e^{2\omega(\sigma)} h_{ab}$ leaves it unchanged — is a special feature of two-dimensional worldsheets. In any other dimension, $\sqrt{-h}\, h^{ab}$ picks up a Weyl-factor power, and the equivalence between the metric and induced-metric forms of the action fails. This is one of several reasons string theory (rather than membrane theory) is much more tractable.

§7Equation of motion for $X^\mu$

Vary $S_P$ with respect to $X^\mu$. The result is a curved-2-d wave equation,

$$ \frac{1}{\sqrt{-h}}\, \partial_a\!\left( \sqrt{-h}\, h^{ab}\, \partial_b X^\mu \right) \;=\; 0. $$

This is the statement that $X^\mu$ is a harmonic function on the worldsheet — its Laplace–Beltrami operator vanishes. In the conformal gauge $h_{ab} = \eta_{ab}$ (Chapter V), it reduces to the ordinary 2-d wave equation

$$ \big(\partial_\tau^2 - \partial_\sigma^2\big)\, X^\mu \;=\; 0, $$

whose solutions are arbitrary functions of $\tau \pm \sigma$ — left- and right-moving waves on the string. Solving this is the work of Chapter VI.

§8The picture so far

Two boxed actions are the central output of this chapter. Both describe the same classical physics; the Polyakov form is the one we will quantize.

§10What we carry forward

  1. A string sweeps out a 2-dimensional worldsheet $\Sigma$ in spacetime; closed strings give cylinders, open strings give strips.
  2. The induced metric $\gamma_{ab} = \partial_a X^\mu \partial_b X^\nu \eta_{\mu\nu}$ is the pull-back of the spacetime metric to $\Sigma$.
  3. The Nambu–Goto action $S = -T \int \mathrm{d}^2\sigma \sqrt{-\det \gamma}$ is the worldsheet area times the tension — the direct geometric analogue of $-m$ times proper time.
  4. The Polyakov action introduces an independent worldsheet metric $h_{ab}$ and is quadratic in $X^\mu$; it is classically equivalent to NG but quantum-mechanically far more tractable.
  5. The 2-dimensional Weyl invariance of $\sqrt{-h}\, h^{ab}$ is the special feature that makes this equivalence work — and the same feature that will, in Chapter V, give us a powerful gauge symmetry to fix.
