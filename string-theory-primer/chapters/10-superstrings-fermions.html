Chapter X

Superstrings and Fermions

Add a worldsheet fermion for each worldsheet boson and demand the two transform into each other. The tachyon dies; the critical dimension drops from twenty-six to ten; spacetime fermions emerge for free.

§1What needs fixing

The bosonic string is geometrically clean but physically incomplete. Two problems weigh against it:

  • The tachyon. The lowest state has $\alpha' M^2 = -1$ (open) or $-4$ (closed). A negative mass-squared signals vacuum instability.
  • No spacetime fermions. Every state in the bosonic spectrum is a tensor — never a spinor. The Standard Model needs quarks and leptons, which are fermions. The bosonic string has nowhere to put them.

Both problems are cured by promoting the worldsheet theory from purely bosonic to supersymmetric. The trick is to add, for each worldsheet scalar $X^\mu(\tau, \sigma)$, a worldsheet fermion $\psi^\mu(\tau, \sigma)$ in the same spacetime representation.

§2The Ramond–Neveu–Schwarz action

The simplest supersymmetric extension of the Polyakov action — the Ramond–Neveu–Schwarz (RNS) action in flat target spacetime and conformal worldsheet gauge — is

$$ S_{\mathrm{RNS}} \;=\; -\frac{1}{4\pi\alpha'} \int \mathrm{d}^2\sigma\, \big( \partial_a X^\mu\, \partial^a X_\mu \;-\; i\, \bar\psi^\mu\, \rho^a\, \partial_a \psi_\mu \big). $$

Where

$\psi^\mu(\tau, \sigma)$
Worldsheet Majorana fermion — a 2-component spinor in worldsheet spinor indices, carrying one target-spacetime index $\mu$.
$\bar\psi^\mu$
Dirac conjugate, $\bar\psi = \psi^\dagger \rho^0$.
$\rho^a$
2-d gamma matrices satisfying $\{\rho^a, \rho^b\} = 2 \eta^{ab}$.

This action is invariant under a global supersymmetry transformation that mixes $X^\mu$ and $\psi^\mu$:

$$ \delta X^\mu \;=\; \bar\varepsilon\, \psi^\mu, \qquad \delta \psi^\mu \;=\; -i\, \rho^a\, \partial_a X^\mu\, \varepsilon, $$

with $\varepsilon$ a constant 2-component Grassmann (anticommuting) parameter. Crucially, this is worldsheet supersymmetry — symmetry of the 2-d theory living on $\Sigma$. Spacetime supersymmetry is a derived consequence (after GSO; see §5).

The fermion equation of motion is the 2-d Dirac equation $\rho^a \partial_a \psi^\mu = 0$. Like the bosons, $\psi^\mu$ decomposes into left- and right-moving Weyl components $\psi^\mu_\pm$, each obeying $\partial_\mp \psi^\mu_\pm = 0$ — so the worldsheet fermions are massless and chiral on $\Sigma$.

§3Boundary conditions: NS and R sectors

For a closed string, the boson $X^\mu(\tau, \sigma)$ is strictly periodic in $\sigma$. The fermion $\psi^\mu(\tau, \sigma)$, however, can be either periodic or antiperiodic without violating any physical principle (fermion bilinears, the physical observables, are periodic in both cases). This gives two independent sectors:

  • Neveu–Schwarz (NS) sector: $\psi^\mu(\tau, \sigma + 2\pi) = -\psi^\mu(\tau, \sigma)$. Antiperiodic. The mode expansion uses half-integer frequencies: $\psi^\mu \sim \sum_{r \in \mathbb{Z} + 1/2} b_r^\mu e^{-ir\sigma^\mp}$.
  • Ramond (R) sector: $\psi^\mu(\tau, \sigma + 2\pi) = +\psi^\mu(\tau, \sigma)$. Periodic. The mode expansion uses integer frequencies: $\psi^\mu \sim \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} d_n^\mu e^{-in\sigma^\mp}$.

For closed strings there are independent NS / R choices on the left- and right-moving fermions, giving four sectors: NS-NS, NS-R, R-NS, R-R. For open strings the boundary couples left and right movers, leaving two sectors (NS and R).

The two sectors give physically distinct content. Geometrically:

  • NS states are spacetime bosons — built by acting with half-integer-moded $b_{-r}^\mu$ on an NS vacuum.
  • R states are spacetime fermions — built on a Ramond vacuum that is itself a target-space spinor (the zero modes $d_0^\mu$ obey a Clifford algebra in target space).

The boundary condition you put on a worldsheet fermion thus controls whether the resulting spacetime particle is a boson or a fermion. This is one of the most striking geometric facts in string theory.

§4The critical dimension drops to ten

Repeating the central-charge accounting of Chapter VIII with the added fermions:

  • $D$ free bosons contribute $c_X = D$.
  • $D$ free Majorana fermions contribute $c_\psi = D/2$.
  • The reparametrization ghosts $b, c$ contribute $c_{bc} = -26$.
  • The local-worldsheet-supersymmetry ghosts $\beta, \gamma$ contribute $c_{\beta\gamma} = +11$.

Cancellation of the total central charge demands

$$ D + \frac{D}{2} - 26 + 11 \;=\; 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \frac{3D}{2} \;=\; 15 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad D \;=\; 10. $$

The superstring lives in ten spacetime dimensions. The normal-ordering shifts likewise change: $a_{\mathrm{NS}} = 1/2$, $a_{\mathrm{R}} = 0$.

§5The GSO projection

Even with $D = 10$, the NS sector starts off with a tachyon — the NS vacuum has $\alpha' M^2 = -1/2$. The cure is the Gliozzi–Scherk–Olive (GSO) projection: keep only states whose worldsheet-fermion-number parity satisfies a chosen sign condition.

In the NS sector, define $(-1)^F$ to act as $+1$ on states with an even number of $b_{-r}$ excitations and $-1$ on states with an odd number. The NS vacuum has $(-1)^F = -1$; the first excited state $b_{-1/2}^\mu |0\rangle_{\mathrm{NS}}$ has $(-1)^F = +1$. The GSO projection keeps states with $(-1)^F = +1$ — the tachyon is projected out, and the massless NS gauge boson survives.

In the R sector, the projection selects one chirality of the spacetime spinor, giving a chiral fermion. The choice of GSO sign in the R sector (and the relative sign between NS and R) distinguishes the various superstring theories.

After GSO the spectra of the bosonic and fermionic sectors agree level-by-level — this is exactly what spacetime supersymmetry would require. The worldsheet GSO projection has manufactured a target-spacetime SUSY for free.

§8What we carry forward

  1. Adding worldsheet fermions $\psi^\mu$ — one for each boson $X^\mu$ — and demanding worldsheet supersymmetry gives the RNS action.
  2. The two fermion boundary conditions split the spectrum: NS (antiperiodic) → spacetime bosons, R (periodic) → spacetime fermions.
  3. Anomaly cancellation now demands $D = 10$ — the critical dimension of the superstring.
  4. The GSO projection removes the NS tachyon and produces a spectrum with level-by-level matching between bosons and fermions: spacetime supersymmetry emerges as a derived consequence of the worldsheet construction.
  5. Five distinct consistent superstring theories exist in 10D; they are all dual to each other and to 11-dim M-theory (Chapter XII).
