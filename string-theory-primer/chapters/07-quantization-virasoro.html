Chapter VII

Quantization and the Conformal Algebra

Promote the oscillator coefficients to operators, work out their algebra, and the string spectrum tumbles out: a tachyon at the bottom, a massless gauge field for the open string, a graviton for the closed.

§1From oscillators to operators

In conformal gauge the worldsheet action is $D$ free 2-d scalar fields. Promoting the classical mode coefficients $\alpha_n^\mu, \tilde\alpha_n^\mu$ to quantum operators, the canonical commutation relations $[X^\mu(\tau, \sigma), \Pi^\nu(\tau, \sigma')] = i \eta^{\mu\nu} \delta(\sigma - \sigma')$ translate to

$$ [\alpha_m^\mu, \alpha_n^\nu] \;=\; m\, \delta_{m+n, 0}\, \eta^{\mu\nu}, \qquad [\tilde\alpha_m^\mu, \tilde\alpha_n^\nu] \;=\; m\, \delta_{m+n,0}\, \eta^{\mu\nu}, \qquad [\alpha_m^\mu, \tilde\alpha_n^\nu] \;=\; 0, $$

together with $[x^\mu, p^\nu] = i \eta^{\mu\nu}$ for the zero modes. The reality conditions $\alpha_{-n}^\mu = (\alpha_n^\mu)^\dagger$ identify positive-$n$ modes with annihilation operators and negative-$n$ with creation. Up to a factor of $\sqrt{m}$, $\alpha_m$ and $\alpha_{-m}$ are the ladder operators of a harmonic oscillator — one for each spacetime direction and each integer mode number.

Where

$[A, B]$
Commutator $AB - BA$.
$\delta_{m+n,0}$
Kronecker delta — equals $1$ if $m = -n$, else $0$.
$\Pi^\nu$
Canonical momentum conjugate to $X^\nu$; equals $\dot X^\nu / (2\pi\alpha')$ in our normalization.

One peculiarity: the timelike oscillators $\alpha_n^0$ commute to $-m$, not $+m$, because of $\eta^{00} = -1$. This means $\alpha_{-n}^0 |0\rangle$ has negative norm — a ghost. The Virasoro constraints will end up projecting these unphysical states out, but only when the spacetime dimension takes a very specific value. That story is Chapter VIII.

§2The Fock space

The Hilbert space is built on a momentum eigenstate ground state $|0; p\rangle$ satisfying

$$ \alpha_n^\mu\, |0; p\rangle \;=\; 0 \quad (n > 0), \qquad \hat p^\mu\, |0; p\rangle \;=\; p^\mu\, |0; p\rangle. $$

General states are built by applying creation operators $\alpha_{-n}^\mu$ with $n > 0$:

$$ |\psi\rangle \;=\; \prod_{n, \mu} \big(\alpha_{-n}^\mu\big)^{N_{n,\mu}}\, |0; p\rangle. $$

The total level of a state is $N = \sum_n n\, N_{n,\mu}$ — the energy carried by oscillator excitations, in units of $1/\alpha'$. For closed strings we have two independent Fock spaces (left and right movers), and the total Hilbert space is a tensor product.

§3The Virasoro algebra and the central charge

The classical Virasoro generators $L_n = \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_m \alpha_{n-m} \cdot \alpha_m$ become operators after quantization. The product $\alpha_{n-m} \cdot \alpha_m$ is ill-defined when the two factors don't commute (which happens for $L_0$, where $m = 0$ involves $\alpha_0$, and for negative $m$). Standard practice is normal ordering: put all creation operators to the left of all annihilation operators. The price of ordering is a constant shift in $L_0$ only:

$$ L_0 \;=\; \frac{1}{2} \alpha_0^2 + \sum_{m \geq 1} \alpha_{-m} \cdot \alpha_m \;+\; \text{(c-number constant)}. $$

The crucial fact is that the commutator of two normal-ordered $L_n$'s does not close on the classical Witt algebra. It picks up an extra term — the central extension — giving the Virasoro algebra:

$$ \boxed{\; [L_m, L_n] \;=\; (m - n)\, L_{m+n} \;+\; \frac{c}{12}\, (m^3 - m)\, \delta_{m+n, 0}. \;} $$

Where

$c$
Central charge — a c-number measuring the failure of the classical conformal symmetry to survive quantization. For $D$ free bosons, $c = D$.

The extra $(c/12)(m^3 - m)$ piece is the conformal anomaly. It is the algebraic shadow of the fact that there is no way to quantize the worldsheet stress tensor while strictly preserving its tracelessness — at the quantum level, $\langle T^a{}_a \rangle = -c R / 12$, where $R$ is the worldsheet Ricci scalar. The trace doesn't vanish; it equals an anomaly proportional to the central charge.

Anomaly

An anomaly is a classical symmetry that fails to survive quantization. The Virasoro central extension is a conformal anomaly; chiral and gauge anomalies appear elsewhere in physics. Geometrically, anomalies are characteristic classes of certain bundles — the central charge $c$ is one such number. Anomalies are not bugs to be papered over but features that constrain which theories are consistent.

§4Physical states

In the classical theory we required $L_n = 0$ for all $n$. We cannot impose this as an operator equation in the quantum theory (the algebra wouldn't close), so instead we demand it weakly:

$$ L_n\, |\psi\rangle \;=\; 0 \quad (n > 0), \qquad (L_0 - a)\, |\psi\rangle \;=\; 0. $$

The constant $a$ is the normal-ordering shift in $L_0$. For the bosonic string a careful computation (zeta-function regularization, Chapter VIII) gives $a = 1$ — the famous result $1 + 2 + 3 + \dots = -1/12$ is what makes this finite and well-defined.

For closed strings we impose $L_n |\psi\rangle = 0$ and $\tilde L_n |\psi\rangle = 0$ separately for $n > 0$, plus level-matching $L_0 = \tilde L_0$, $N = \tilde N$.

§5The mass formula

The $L_0 = a$ condition becomes the quantum mass-shell relation. For the open string,

$$ \alpha' M^2 \;=\; N - a. $$

For the closed string, using $\alpha_0^\mu = \tilde\alpha_0^\mu = \sqrt{\alpha'/2}\, p^\mu$,

$$ \frac{\alpha' M^2}{4} \;=\; N - a \;=\; \tilde N - a. $$

The string is an infinite tower of states, one for each $N$, with mass-squared spaced by $1/\alpha'$. Different levels carry different spins and field content. At low energies the heavy modes decouple and only the massless and lightest massive ones matter — which is why string theory looks like ordinary field theory in any low-energy limit.

§6Reading off the lowest states

Take $a = 1$ (anticipating Chapter VIII) and read off levels.

Open string, $N = 0$: $\alpha' M^2 = -1$. A tachyon — a state of negative mass-squared, instability. This is a defect of the purely bosonic theory and is removed by adding worldsheet supersymmetry (Chapter X).

Open string, $N = 1$: $\alpha' M^2 = 0$. The state $\alpha_{-1}^\mu |0; p\rangle$ is a massless vector — it transforms as a Lorentz vector and has $D - 2$ physical polarizations after constraints. This is the photon-like gauge mode of the open string. With $N$ coincident D-branes, the analogous construction gives a $U(N)$ gauge boson.

Closed string, $N = \tilde N = 1$: $\alpha' M^2 = 0$ again, but now built from a left- and right-moving oscillator: $\alpha_{-1}^\mu \tilde\alpha_{-1}^\nu |0; p\rangle$. This is a rank-2 tensor representation, which decomposes into

  • a symmetric traceless part — the graviton $h_{\mu\nu}$,
  • an antisymmetric part — the Kalb–Ramond 2-form $B_{\mu\nu}$, and
  • a trace part — the dilaton $\Phi$.

This is the most consequential result so far in the book. Quantizing the closed string automatically produces a massless spin-2 field with exactly the symmetries of a graviton. Weinberg's theorem (Chapter I §3) then tells us that, at low energy, the dynamics of this field must reduce to general relativity. String theory contains gravity, not by construction, but by consistency.

§8What we carry forward

  1. The classical oscillators $\alpha_n^\mu$ become quantum operators with $[\alpha_m, \alpha_n] = m \delta_{m+n} \eta^{\mu\nu}$ — ladder operators of independent harmonic oscillators.
  2. The Virasoro generators $L_n$ obey an algebra with a central extension proportional to $c$, the central charge. For $D$ free bosons, $c = D$.
  3. Physical states satisfy $L_{n>0}|\psi\rangle = 0$ and $(L_0 - a)|\psi\rangle = 0$ — a quantum version of the classical Virasoro constraints.
  4. The resulting mass formula $\alpha' M^2 = N - a$ gives an infinite tower of states, $1/\alpha'$ apart.
  5. At $N = 1$ the open string gives a massless vector; the closed string gives a massless symmetric tensor (the graviton), antisymmetric 2-form, and dilaton. String theory contains gravity for free.

One question remains. We have used $a = 1$ and a particular spacetime dimension $D$ without justifying either. Chapter VIII shows that consistency — the absence of negative-norm states from the spectrum — forces $a = 1$ and $D = 26$ simultaneously.
