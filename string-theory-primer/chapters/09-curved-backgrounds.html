Chapter IX

Strings on Curved Backgrounds

Put the string on a curved spacetime. Demand that its worldsheet remain a conformal field theory. Out fall Einstein's equations — with calculable, finite stringy corrections.

§1The non-linear sigma model

So far the target spacetime has been flat Minkowski with metric $\eta_{\mu\nu}$. To do interesting physics we have to allow it to curve. Replace $\eta_{\mu\nu}$ everywhere with a general metric $g_{\mu\nu}(X)$, and add two other massless background fields — the antisymmetric Kalb–Ramond field $B_{\mu\nu}(X)$ and the scalar dilaton $\Phi(X)$ — that we will see emerge naturally from the spectrum. The full worldsheet action is

$$ S \;=\; -\frac{1}{4\pi\alpha'} \int_\Sigma \mathrm{d}^2\sigma\, \sqrt{-h}\left[ h^{ab}\, g_{\mu\nu}(X) + \epsilon^{ab}\, B_{\mu\nu}(X) \right] \partial_a X^\mu\, \partial_b X^\nu \;+\; \frac{1}{4\pi} \int_\Sigma \mathrm{d}^2\sigma\, \sqrt{-h}\, R^{(2)}\, \Phi(X). $$

Where

$g_{\mu\nu}(X)$
Target spacetime metric. The dependence on $X$ makes this a non-linear sigma model on the worldsheet.
$B_{\mu\nu}(X)$
Antisymmetric 2-form field — the Kalb–Ramond gauge field that strings couple to electrically.
$\Phi(X)$
Dilaton — a scalar field whose expectation value sets the string coupling, $g_s = e^\Phi$.
$\epsilon^{ab}$
2-d Levi-Civita tensor, $\epsilon^{ab} = \tilde\epsilon^{ab}/\sqrt{-h}$ with $\tilde\epsilon^{ab}$ the antisymmetric symbol — so $\sqrt{-h}\,\epsilon^{ab}$ is metric-independent.
$R^{(2)}$
Worldsheet Ricci scalar — a curvature invariant of $h_{ab}$, not of target spacetime.

The three background fields are exactly the massless modes of the closed-string spectrum from Chapter VII §6: graviton, Kalb–Ramond, dilaton. We are now letting these massless modes acquire spacetime-dependent expectation values — a non-trivial "background" in which the string propagates.

Geometrically, the worldsheet theory has become a map $\Sigma \to (M, g, B, \Phi)$. It is the natural 2-dimensional analogue of a particle worldline in a curved metric, but with two additional gauge backgrounds.

§2Conformal invariance as a consistency condition

In flat target space, the worldsheet theory was free and Weyl invariance was automatic. With curved $g, B, \Phi$, the theory is interacting and Weyl invariance becomes a non-trivial demand. The trace of the worldsheet stress tensor picks up contributions from each background field:

$$ \langle T^a{}_a \rangle \;=\; -\frac{1}{2\alpha'}\, \beta^g_{\mu\nu}\, h^{ab}\, \partial_a X^\mu\, \partial_b X^\nu \;-\; \frac{1}{2\alpha'}\, \beta^B_{\mu\nu}\, \epsilon^{ab}\, \partial_a X^\mu\, \partial_b X^\nu \;-\; \frac{1}{2}\, \beta^\Phi\, R^{(2)}. $$

The coefficients $\beta^g, \beta^B, \beta^\Phi$ are the renormalization-group beta functions of the sigma-model couplings. For Weyl invariance to hold, every one must vanish:

$$ \beta^g_{\mu\nu} \;=\; 0, \qquad \beta^B_{\mu\nu} \;=\; 0, \qquad \beta^\Phi \;=\; 0. $$

These are equations on the background fields $g, B, \Phi$, derived purely from worldsheet consistency. They are not put in by hand. The point of this chapter is to read them out.

§3The leading $\alpha'$ result

Computing the beta functions perturbatively in $\alpha'$ (small worldsheet curvature) is a careful exercise; the result to leading order is

$$ \beta^g_{\mu\nu} \;=\; \alpha'\left( R_{\mu\nu} + 2\nabla_\mu \nabla_\nu \Phi - \tfrac{1}{4} H_{\mu\rho\sigma}\, H_\nu{}^{\rho\sigma} \right) + O(\alpha'^2), $$
$$ \beta^B_{\mu\nu} \;=\; \alpha'\left( -\tfrac{1}{2} \nabla^\rho H_{\rho\mu\nu} + (\nabla^\rho \Phi)\, H_{\rho\mu\nu} \right) + O(\alpha'^2), $$
$$ \beta^\Phi \;=\; \alpha'\left( -\tfrac{1}{2} \nabla^2 \Phi + (\nabla \Phi)^2 - \tfrac{1}{24} H_{\mu\nu\rho} H^{\mu\nu\rho} \right) + \frac{D - 26}{6} + O(\alpha'^2), $$

Where

$R_{\mu\nu}$
Ricci tensor of the target spacetime metric $g_{\mu\nu}$.
$H_{\mu\nu\rho}$
Field strength of $B$: $H = \mathrm{d}B$, with components $H_{\mu\nu\rho} = \partial_\mu B_{\nu\rho} + \partial_\nu B_{\rho\mu} + \partial_\rho B_{\mu\nu}$.
$\nabla_\mu$
Covariant derivative built from the Levi-Civita connection of $g_{\mu\nu}$.

The dilaton beta function carries an explicit $(D - 26)/6$ piece — the bosonic critical-dimension anomaly from Chapter VIII reappearing here as a contribution to the worldsheet conformal anomaly. Setting $D = 26$ removes it; this is the same constraint as before, in a different language.

§4Einstein's equations from worldsheet consistency

Look at $\beta^g_{\mu\nu} = 0$ with $B = 0$ and constant $\Phi$. It reduces to

$$ R_{\mu\nu} \;=\; 0. $$

This is exactly Einstein's vacuum equation. A consistent string background must be Ricci-flat at leading order in $\alpha'$ — and Einstein gravity is the unique low-energy effective theory of a massless spin-2 field. Quantum-mechanical consistency of the worldsheet has derived (the vacuum form of) general relativity from scratch.

Including matter — say, a non-trivial $H$-flux — gives Einstein's equations sourced by the stress-energy of the Kalb–Ramond field:

$$ R_{\mu\nu} \;=\; \tfrac{1}{4} H_{\mu\rho\sigma}\, H_\nu{}^{\rho\sigma} \quad (\Phi \text{ constant}). $$

Including the dilaton produces additional terms, all of which assemble into the Einstein equations of a particular low-energy effective action — the type-II / heterotic low-energy supergravity in the superstring case, or its bosonic ancestor in 26 dimensions. The effective action is

$$ S_{\text{eff}} \;=\; \frac{1}{2\kappa^2} \int \mathrm{d}^D X\, \sqrt{-g}\, e^{-2\Phi}\left( R + 4(\nabla\Phi)^2 - \tfrac{1}{12} H_{\mu\nu\rho} H^{\mu\nu\rho} \right) + O(\alpha'). $$

This is gravity coupled to a 2-form field and a scalar, in the so-called string frame. A field redefinition (the Weyl rescaling $g_{\mu\nu} \to e^{4\Phi/(D-2)} g_{\mu\nu}$) puts it into the more familiar Einstein frame form.

§5The stringy corrections

The $O(\alpha'^2)$ terms in the beta functions yield curvature-squared and higher corrections to Einstein's equations. Schematically,

$$ \beta^g_{\mu\nu} \;\sim\; \alpha' R_{\mu\nu} + \alpha'^2 R_{\mu\rho\sigma\tau}\, R_\nu{}^{\rho\sigma\tau} + \alpha'^3\, R^3 + \cdots, $$

so the full equation of motion is an infinite series in $\alpha'$:

$$ R_{\mu\nu} + \alpha'\, R^2\text{-corrections} + \alpha'^2\, R^3\text{-corrections} + \cdots \;=\; 0. $$

At length scales much larger than $\ell_s = \sqrt{\alpha'}$, the higher terms are tiny — string theory looks like Einstein gravity. At length scales comparable to $\ell_s$, the corrections become important and the geometry departs from the smooth-manifold picture of GR. Geometrically, the string has a finite size and cannot resolve features smaller than itself; the corrections to Einstein encode this.

This is a fundamentally different prediction from generic theories of quantum gravity. The corrections are computable, finite, and ordered by a single small parameter $\alpha'$.

§7What we carry forward

  1. The string couples to its three massless modes $g_{\mu\nu}, B_{\mu\nu}, \Phi$ as background fields in a 2-d non-linear sigma model.
  2. Worldsheet conformal invariance — Weyl invariance of the quantum theory — is a constraint on these backgrounds, expressed as the vanishing of the sigma-model beta functions.
  3. At leading order in $\alpha'$, $\beta^g_{\mu\nu} = 0$ is Einstein's equation (sourced by $H$ and $\Phi$). General relativity is the low-energy approximation of string consistency, not an input.
  4. Higher orders in $\alpha'$ produce calculable curvature-squared and higher corrections — predictions of string theory beyond GR.
  5. The dilaton beta function carries the critical-dimension anomaly $(D - 26)/6$, recovering $D = 26$ from a different route than Chapter VIII.

With curved backgrounds in hand we are equipped to handle compactification — making most of spacetime small enough to hide. Chapter X first finishes the matter sector by adding fermions (the superstring), then Chapter XI compactifies and we meet Calabi–Yau manifolds.
