Chapter VIII

The Critical Dimension

Quantizing the string honestly demands an anomaly cancellation. There is only one spacetime dimension in which the cancellation works. The number isn't four. It's twenty-six.

§1The anomaly we cannot ignore

In Chapter VII we found that the quantum Virasoro algebra acquires a central extension,

$$ [L_m, L_n] \;=\; (m - n)\, L_{m+n} \;+\; \frac{c}{12}\, (m^3 - m)\, \delta_{m+n,0}. $$

The coefficient $c$ measures the conformal anomaly: the failure of Weyl invariance to survive quantization. Classically, Weyl was a gauge symmetry of the Polyakov action; if it remains anomalous after quantization, the gauge symmetry is broken and the worldsheet metric — which we set to $\eta_{ab}$ — is no longer pure gauge. The theory becomes inconsistent.

For consistency we must arrange for the total central charge of the worldsheet theory to vanish:

$$ c_{\text{total}} \;=\; 0. $$

Each free boson contributes $c = 1$. With $D$ free bosons from $X^\mu$, the matter sector gives $c_X = D$. Something must cancel this off. That something is the reparametrization ghosts.

§2Ghosts from gauge-fixing

To gauge-fix $h_{ab} = \eta_{ab}$ rigorously in the path integral, one introduces Faddeev–Popov ghost fields $b_{ab}$ and $c^a$. These are anticommuting fields with a specific kinetic action,

$$ S_{\text{ghost}} \;=\; \frac{1}{2\pi} \int \mathrm{d}^2\sigma\, b_{ab}\, \partial^a c^b, $$

and they carry a contribution to the central charge that comes out, after careful computation, to

$$ c_{\text{ghost}} \;=\; -26. $$

The negative sign is the algebraic content of being anticommuting; the magnitude $26$ comes from the $b, c$ system having conformal weights $(2, -1)$ — these particular weights, dictated by the structure of the gauge symmetry being fixed, deliver this specific number. Total anomaly cancellation requires

$$ \boxed{\; c_X + c_{\text{ghost}} \;=\; D - 26 \;=\; 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad D \;=\; 26. \;} $$

The spacetime dimension is fixed. Bosonic string theory lives in exactly twenty-six dimensions or it lives nowhere consistently.

§3The same answer from zero-point energies

The same $D = 26$ can be read off without ever mentioning ghosts, by a route both elegant and (initially) alarming. Light-cone quantization fixes the gauge $X^+ = \tau / p^+$ explicitly. Only the $D - 2$ transverse oscillators remain physical, and there is no ghost sector. But now we have to evaluate the $L_0$ normal-ordering shift directly.

The Hamiltonian of a quantum harmonic oscillator with frequency $\omega$ has a zero-point energy $\omega / 2$. Each transverse oscillator of mode number $n$ contributes a zero-point energy $n/2$ to $L_0$, and we sum over all $n \geq 1$ and all $D - 2$ transverse directions:

$$ a \;=\; \frac{D - 2}{2} \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty} n. $$

The naive sum $\sum n$ diverges. But it is regularized by the Riemann zeta function: $\zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty n^{-s}$ is convergent for $\mathrm{Re}(s) > 1$ and admits a unique analytic continuation to the whole complex plane. The continued value at $s = -1$ is

$$ \zeta(-1) \;=\; -\frac{1}{12}, $$

and accordingly

$$ \sum_{n=1}^\infty n \;\;\longrightarrow\;\; -\frac{1}{12}. $$

What zeta regularization is doing

Zeta-function regularization replaces a divergent sum by the analytic continuation of a nearby convergent one. It is not a swindle: the same finite answer drops out of every careful regulator (heat-kernel cutoff, point-splitting, dimensional regularization) for this particular sum. Physically, the regulator removes a divergent self-energy attributable to having infinitely many modes, leaving the finite Casimir-type shift that actually depends on the geometry of the worldsheet.

Plugging in,

$$ a \;=\; \frac{D - 2}{2} \cdot \left( -\frac{1}{12} \right) \;=\; -\frac{D - 2}{24}. $$

Wait — but in Chapter VII we used $a = +1$ in the mass formula $\alpha' M^2 = N - a$. Why the sign disagreement?

The conventional sign is $a = (D-2)/24$, with the mass formula $\alpha' M^2 = N - a$. The light-cone derivation matches this once one is careful about signs in $L_0 = -p^- p^+ + $ (oscillator contributions). Setting $a = 1$ as the value that makes the level-$N=1$ state massless gives

$$ 1 \;=\; \frac{D - 2}{24} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad D \;=\; 26. $$

The same answer emerges. From the ghost picture: cancel the central charge. From the light-cone picture: the level-1 state had better be massless (otherwise we lose Lorentz invariance — see §4). Both routes converge on $D = 26$, no fudging.

§4The Lorentz-invariance route

There is a third way to derive $D = 26$, complementary to the two above. In light-cone gauge we have explicitly broken manifest Lorentz invariance by singling out the directions $X^\pm$. For Lorentz invariance to be restored at the quantum level, the algebra of Lorentz generators $J^{\mu\nu}$ must close. The commutator $[J^{i-}, J^{j-}]$ should vanish — and a direct computation shows it does, provided

$$ D = 26 \;\;\;\text{and}\;\;\; a = 1. $$

The fact that three independent demands — anomaly cancellation, zero-point arithmetic, and Lorentz closure — all agree on the same $(D, a)$ is the strongest possible evidence that string theory wants twenty-six dimensions.

§5The tachyon problem

With $a = 1$ confirmed, the bosonic string spectrum has a flaw: the open-string ground state $|0; p\rangle$ has

$$ \alpha' M^2 \;=\; 0 - 1 \;=\; -1. $$

It is a tachyon — a state of negative mass-squared. In quantum field theory, a tachyon is a sign that we are not at a stable vacuum; the potential $V(\phi) = \tfrac{1}{2} M^2 \phi^2 + \dots$ is upside down at our chosen point. For the closed string, the ground state is also tachyonic: $\alpha' M^2 / 4 = -1$.

The bosonic string is therefore unstable. Despite the elegance of $D = 26$ and the natural appearance of a massless graviton, the bosonic string cannot be a complete physical theory.

The cure is to add worldsheet supersymmetry (Chapter X). The resulting superstring has additional fermionic worldsheet fields whose ghost contributions shift the anomaly cancellation, leading to a different critical dimension — and a different tachyon situation. After a clever projection (the GSO projection), the superstring has no tachyon at all, and its critical dimension turns out to be

$$ D_{\text{super}} \;=\; 10. $$

The bosonic string of this chapter is therefore best thought of as a pedagogical warm-up; the physically interesting string lives in ten dimensions, not twenty-six. The geometric structure is the same.

§7What we carry forward

  1. Anomaly cancellation in the bosonic string requires $c_{\text{total}} = c_X + c_{\text{ghost}} = D - 26 = 0$, fixing $D = 26$.
  2. Zeta-function regularization of the zero-point sum $\sum n$ gives the finite value $-1/12$ and produces the same $D = 26$ from the requirement $(D-2)/24 = 1$.
  3. Lorentz invariance in light-cone quantization independently demands $D = 26$ and $a = 1$.
  4. The resulting spectrum contains a tachyon at level 0 — a sign of vacuum instability. The bosonic string is incomplete on its own.
  5. Adding worldsheet supersymmetry (Chapter X) removes the tachyon (via the GSO projection) and reduces the critical dimension to $D = 10$.
