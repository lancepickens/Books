Chapter III

The Relativistic Point Particle

Before we promote points to strings, let us understand a single relativistic particle from first principles. Its geometric action — mass times proper time — already contains every symmetry and structural move we will need for the string.

§1The worldline as a curve in spacetime

A relativistic point particle in spacetime $M$ traces out a one-dimensional submanifold: its worldline. We describe it by an embedding

$$ X^\mu : \mathbb{R} \to M, \qquad \lambda \mapsto X^\mu(\lambda), $$

where $\lambda$ is an arbitrary parameter along the curve. The tangent vector to the worldline at parameter $\lambda$ is $\dot X^\mu \equiv \mathrm{d}X^\mu / \mathrm{d}\lambda$.

For a massive particle, the worldline must be everywhere timelike:

$$ \eta_{\mu\nu}\, \dot X^\mu\, \dot X^\nu \;<\; 0. $$

This is the geometric statement that the particle moves slower than light: its tangent vector lies inside the local light cone. For massless particles the tangent is null, $\eta_{\mu\nu} \dot X^\mu \dot X^\nu = 0$. We will work with flat Minkowski spacetime and metric $\eta_{\mu\nu}$ in this chapter; replacing $\eta_{\mu\nu}$ with a general $g_{\mu\nu}(X)$ generalizes everything to curved backgrounds with no extra ideas.

The worldline is the geometric object

$\lambda$ is just a label — a coordinate on the worldline. The physically meaningful object is the curve in spacetime, not how fast we walk along it. Anything we write should ultimately depend only on the curve, never on the parameterization.

§2The Nambu-style action

The simplest reparametrization-invariant quantity attached to a worldline is its length — or, in Lorentzian spacetime, its proper time. For a timelike worldline,

$$ \tau \;=\; \int \mathrm{d}\lambda\, \sqrt{-\eta_{\mu\nu}\, \dot X^\mu\, \dot X^\nu}. $$

We define the action of a relativistic point particle of mass $m$ to be $-m$ times this:

$$ \boxed{\; S_{\mathrm{pp}}[X] \;=\; -m \int \mathrm{d}\lambda\, \sqrt{-\eta_{\mu\nu}\, \dot X^\mu\, \dot X^\nu}. \;} $$

Where

$m$
Rest mass of the particle. Positive for massive particles; we treat $m=0$ separately in §5.
$X^\mu(\lambda)$
Embedding of the worldline in target spacetime; $\mu = 0,1,\dots,D-1$.
$\dot X^\mu$
Tangent to the worldline, $\mathrm{d}X^\mu/\mathrm{d}\lambda$.
$\eta_{\mu\nu}$
Mostly-plus Minkowski metric, $\mathrm{diag}(-1,+1,+1,\dots)$.

The minus sign in front of $m$ exists precisely so that the principle of stationary action — which selects critical points of $S$ — picks out paths that maximize proper time (Chapter II §5). The free relativistic particle is exactly the geometric object whose action is its swept-out length.

§3Reparametrization invariance

The crucial property of $S_{\mathrm{pp}}$ is that it does not care how we parameterize the worldline. Replace $\lambda$ by any orientation-preserving smooth reparametrization $\lambda \mapsto \lambda'(\lambda)$, $\mathrm{d}\lambda'/\mathrm{d}\lambda > 0$. Under such a change,

$$ \dot X^\mu \;\equiv\; \frac{\mathrm{d}X^\mu}{\mathrm{d}\lambda} \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{d}X^\mu}{\mathrm{d}\lambda'}\,\frac{\mathrm{d}\lambda'}{\mathrm{d}\lambda}, $$

so the square-root piece transforms as $\sqrt{-\dot X^2}\,\mathrm{d}\lambda \;=\; \sqrt{-(\mathrm{d}X/\mathrm{d}\lambda')^2}\,\mathrm{d}\lambda'$, and the action is unchanged:

$$ S_{\mathrm{pp}}\big[X(\lambda)\big] \;=\; S_{\mathrm{pp}}\big[X(\lambda'(\lambda))\big]. $$

Geometrically: the action depends only on the image of the worldline in spacetime, not on the coordinate we drew on it. This is a redundancy in our description — a gauge symmetry of the worldline. We will eventually fix it (e.g., by setting $\lambda = \tau$, the proper time itself), but the underlying invariance is what makes the action manifestly geometric.

Gauge symmetry, in one sentence

A gauge symmetry is a transformation that changes how we describe a physical state but not the state itself. Worldline reparametrization is gauge: the same curve, written in different parameter coordinates, is one physical worldline. The corresponding symmetry on the string worldsheet — diffeomorphism + Weyl invariance — is the engine of every consistency condition we will meet, including the critical dimension.

This is the structural template we will reuse: parameter-redundancy of the embedded object. For a worldline, it's a 1-parameter freedom. For a worldsheet, it's 2-parameter — and the same proof, with one more index, will go through unchanged.

§4Equations of motion

Vary $S_{\mathrm{pp}}$ with respect to $X^\mu$, with endpoints held fixed. Writing $\mathcal{L} = -m\sqrt{-\dot X^2}$ (using $\dot X^2 \equiv \eta_{\mu\nu} \dot X^\mu \dot X^\nu$), the canonical momentum is

$$ p_\mu \;=\; \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot X^\mu} \;=\; \frac{m\, \eta_{\mu\nu}\, \dot X^\nu}{\sqrt{-\dot X^2}}. $$

Notice immediately that $p_\mu$ obeys a constraint:

$$ p^\mu p_\mu \;=\; -m^2. $$

This is the mass-shell condition. It is enforced automatically by the square root in the action — there is no choice of $\dot X$ for which $p^\mu p_\mu \neq -m^2$. It is the algebraic shadow of reparametrization invariance, and it will reappear as the Virasoro constraint for the string.

The Euler–Lagrange equation $\mathrm{d}p_\mu/\mathrm{d}\lambda = 0$, after choosing the proper-time gauge $\lambda = \tau$ so that $\sqrt{-\dot X^2} = 1$, simplifies to

$$ \ddot X^\mu \;=\; 0 \qquad (\text{flat spacetime}). $$

The particle moves in a straight line, at constant 4-velocity. On a curved background $g_{\mu\nu}(X)$, the same variation produces the geodesic equation of Chapter II §5,

$$ \ddot X^\mu + \Gamma^\mu{}_{\nu\rho}\, \dot X^\nu\, \dot X^\rho \;=\; 0, $$

with $\Gamma$ now built from $g_{\mu\nu}(X)$. Nothing changes structurally.

§5The square-root problem and the einbein

The Nambu-style action has two cosmetic awkwardnesses:

  1. The square root is hard to handle in a path integral.
  2. It is singular at $m = 0$: a massless particle has no action this way.

Both issues are cured by an equivalent action that introduces an auxiliary field $e(\lambda)$, called the einbein:

$$ \boxed{\; S_{\mathrm{pp}}'[X, e] \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2} \int \mathrm{d}\lambda\, \Big[ e^{-1}(\lambda)\, \eta_{\mu\nu}\, \dot X^\mu\, \dot X^\nu - e(\lambda)\, m^2 \Big]. \;} $$

Where

$e(\lambda)$
A positive auxiliary field on the worldline. Geometrically it is the "vielbein" of a 1-dim metric $g_{\lambda\lambda} = e^2$ on the worldline — the einbein gives the worldline its own length scale.

The einbein must transform under reparametrization $\lambda \to \lambda'(\lambda)$ as $e \to e\, \mathrm{d}\lambda / \mathrm{d}\lambda'$ — so that the combination $e\, \mathrm{d}\lambda$ (the worldline volume element) is invariant. With that rule, $S_{\mathrm{pp}}'[X, e]$ is manifestly reparametrization-invariant, even though the integrand is no longer a square root.

The equation of motion for $e$ comes from varying $\partial S_{\mathrm{pp}}'/\partial e = 0$:

$$ -\frac{1}{e^2}\, \eta_{\mu\nu}\, \dot X^\mu\, \dot X^\nu \;=\; m^2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad e \;=\; \frac{1}{m}\, \sqrt{-\eta_{\mu\nu}\, \dot X^\mu\, \dot X^\nu}. $$

Substituting this back into $S_{\mathrm{pp}}'$ gives exactly $-m \int \mathrm{d}\lambda\, \sqrt{-\dot X^2} = S_{\mathrm{pp}}$. So the two actions are classically equivalent — they agree on every physical solution.

But the einbein form has two advantages the Nambu form does not:

  • No square root. The Lagrangian is a polynomial in $\dot X^\mu$, ready for canonical quantization or path-integration.
  • Massless particles included. Setting $m = 0$ leaves a perfectly good action $\tfrac{1}{2} \int \mathrm{d}\lambda\, e^{-1} \dot X^2$. The $e$-EOM now reads $\dot X^2 = 0$ — the worldline is null, as it should be for a massless particle.

This is the key structural rehearsal. When we move to strings in the next chapter, we will write the Nambu–Goto action (worldsheet area) and then immediately introduce a worldsheet metric $h_{ab}$ — the 2-dimensional analogue of the einbein $e$ — to get the Polyakov action. The two will be classically equivalent for the same reason as here.

§7The geodesic maximizes proper time

The action $S_{\mathrm{pp}} = -m\, \tau$ is geometric: it is $-m$ times the proper-time length of the worldline. Stationary points are critical points of proper time. For nearby timelike paths between two events $A$ and $B$, the geodesic (straight line in flat space) is a maximum of $\tau$ — every wiggle decreases it. This is the geometric content of the classical twin paradox.

§8What we carry forward

  1. The action of a free relativistic particle is $S = -m \int \mathrm{d}s$ — mass times the proper length of its worldline. It is a geometric invariant of the curve.
  2. It is invariant under reparametrizations of the worldline. This redundancy is a gauge symmetry of the theory.
  3. Its equations of motion are the geodesic equation; for nearby timelike paths the geodesic maximizes proper time.
  4. An equivalent einbein action $S' = \tfrac{1}{2}\int \mathrm{d}\lambda\, (e^{-1}\dot X^2 - e m^2)$ replaces the square root with a polynomial Lagrangian and admits massless particles.
  5. The mass-shell condition $p^2 + m^2 = 0$ is the algebraic content of reparametrization invariance.

In the next chapter we promote $\lambda$ to $(\tau, \sigma)$, the worldline to a worldsheet, and the einbein $e$ to a worldsheet metric $h_{ab}$. Every move we just made will have a 2-dimensional analogue, and the action that comes out is the Polyakov action of the bosonic string.
