Chapter VI

Solving the Classical String

The wave equation is the same one a guitar string obeys; the difference is what we hang off the boundary. Free endpoints give an open string of vibrational modes; fixed endpoints reveal D-branes.

§1The wave equation, recapped

In conformal gauge $h_{ab} = \eta_{ab}$, the Polyakov equation of motion for the string's embedding reads

$$ \big( \partial_\tau^2 - \partial_\sigma^2 \big)\, X^\mu(\tau, \sigma) \;=\; 0, $$

or in light-cone form $\partial_+ \partial_- X^\mu = 0$ with $\sigma^\pm = \tau \pm \sigma$. The general solution is the sum of a left-moving and a right-moving wave,

$$ X^\mu(\tau, \sigma) \;=\; X_L^\mu(\sigma^+) + X_R^\mu(\sigma^-), $$

where $X_L^\mu, X_R^\mu$ are arbitrary smooth functions of one variable. The constraints come from two places: (i) the topology of the string sets a boundary or periodicity condition, and (ii) the Virasoro constraints from Chapter V §4 must be satisfied. We deal with topology first.

§2Closed string — periodic boundary conditions

For a closed string, $\sigma \sim \sigma + 2\pi$, and $X^\mu$ must be periodic:

$$ X^\mu(\tau, \sigma + 2\pi) \;=\; X^\mu(\tau, \sigma). $$

Periodicity is compatible with independent left- and right-mover expansions. The Fourier expansion of each is

$$ X_L^\mu(\sigma^+) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2} x^\mu + \tfrac{\alpha'}{2}\, p^\mu\, \sigma^+ + i\sqrt{\tfrac{\alpha'}{2}}\, \sum_{n \neq 0} \frac{\tilde\alpha_n^\mu}{n}\, e^{-in\sigma^+}, $$
$$ X_R^\mu(\sigma^-) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2} x^\mu + \tfrac{\alpha'}{2}\, p^\mu\, \sigma^- + i\sqrt{\tfrac{\alpha'}{2}}\, \sum_{n \neq 0} \frac{\alpha_n^\mu}{n}\, e^{-in\sigma^-}. $$

Where

$x^\mu$
Center-of-mass position of the string at $\tau = 0$.
$p^\mu$
Total spacetime momentum of the string (zero-mode of the velocity).
$\alpha_n^\mu$
Right-moving oscillator modes, labeled by $n \in \mathbb{Z}\setminus\{0\}$.
$\tilde\alpha_n^\mu$
Left-moving oscillator modes — independent of the right-movers.
$\alpha'$
Regge slope; sets the overall string-length scale.

Reality of $X^\mu$ requires $\alpha_{-n}^\mu = (\alpha_n^\mu)^*$ and similarly for $\tilde\alpha_n^\mu$. The factor of $i\sqrt{\alpha'/2}$ in front is a convention chosen so the commutation relations come out clean after quantization (Chapter VII).

Geometric reading: the string has a position, a momentum, and an infinite tower of oscillation modes labeled by integer $n$. Left- and right-moving modes are independent — physically, two trains of waves traveling around the loop in opposite directions, never interacting except through the Virasoro constraints.

§3Open string — Neumann boundary conditions

An open string has $\sigma \in [0, \pi]$ with boundaries at the two endpoints. Varying the Polyakov action with $\sigma$ ranging over an interval produces, in addition to the bulk equation of motion, a boundary term

$$ \frac{1}{2\pi\alpha'} \int \mathrm{d}\tau\, \big[ \partial_\sigma X^\mu\, \delta X_\mu \big]_{\sigma = 0}^{\sigma = \pi} \;=\; 0. $$

For each direction $\mu$, we must impose either $\partial_\sigma X^\mu = 0$ (Neumann — the endpoint is free) or $\delta X^\mu = 0$ (Dirichlet — the endpoint is fixed). We treat each case in turn.

With Neumann boundary conditions on all $\mu$, $\partial_\sigma X^\mu\big|_{\sigma = 0, \pi} = 0$, the boundary forces left- and right-movers to be reflections of each other,

$$ \tilde\alpha_n^\mu \;=\; \alpha_n^\mu. $$

Only one set of oscillators survives. The mode expansion combines them as cosines, which manifestly satisfy Neumann boundaries:

$$ X^\mu(\tau, \sigma) \;=\; x^\mu + 2\alpha'\, p^\mu\, \tau + i\sqrt{2\alpha'}\, \sum_{n \neq 0} \frac{\alpha_n^\mu}{n}\, e^{-in\tau}\, \cos(n\sigma). $$

Compared to the closed string, the open string has half as many oscillators and twice the prefactor on the momentum term — these conventions arise from carefully matching the worldsheet integration ranges.

§4Open string — Dirichlet boundary conditions and D-branes

Suppose instead we impose Dirichlet conditions on some directions, say $X^I(\tau, 0) = X^I(\tau, \pi) = c^I$ (a constant) for $I = p+1, \dots, D-1$, while keeping Neumann conditions for the remaining $\mu = 0, 1, \dots, p$ directions.

Geometrically, this says: the two ends of the string are constrained to lie on a fixed $(p+1)$-dimensional submanifold of spacetime — the hyperplane $X^I = c^I$. That submanifold is what we call a D$p$-brane.

D-brane

A D$p$-brane is a $(p+1)$-dimensional submanifold of spacetime on which open strings end. The "D" stands for Dirichlet — the boundary condition that nails the string endpoint to the brane. D-branes are not auxiliary: in the modern picture they are dynamical objects in their own right (with their own action, the Dirac–Born–Infeld action; Chapter XIII), carrying tension and Ramond–Ramond charge.

The mode expansion now mixes cosines (in Neumann directions) and sines (in Dirichlet directions):

$$ X^\mu(\tau, \sigma) \;=\; x^\mu + 2\alpha'\, p^\mu\, \tau + i\sqrt{2\alpha'}\, \sum_{n \neq 0} \frac{\alpha_n^\mu}{n}\, e^{-in\tau}\, \cos(n\sigma) \quad (\mu = 0,\dots,p), $$
$$ X^I(\tau, \sigma) \;=\; c^I + \sqrt{2\alpha'}\, \sum_{n \neq 0} \frac{\alpha_n^I}{n}\, e^{-in\tau}\, \sin(n\sigma) \quad (I = p+1,\dots,D-1). $$

Notice that in Dirichlet directions there is no $p^I$ — the brane has no momentum in the directions transverse to itself (it is pinned), and there is no $x^I$ either, only the constant $c^I$ giving the brane's position.

The geometric picture is striking. Where open strings are allowed to end is itself a geometric object in spacetime. Multiple branes mean multiple submanifolds; stacks of $N$ parallel branes give $N^2$ varieties of open strings (one for each pair of branes the endpoints attach to) — which, when quantized, are exactly the bosons of a $U(N)$ gauge theory living on the brane. This is the geometric origin of the gauge theories in string theory.

§5Virasoro constraints in mode form

The Virasoro constraints $T_{\pm\pm} = 0$ from Chapter V §5 translate into conditions on the modes. Define the Fourier coefficients of the stress tensor as

$$ L_n \;=\; \frac{1}{2} \sum_{m \in \mathbb{Z}} \alpha_{n-m}^\mu\, \alpha_m{}_\mu, \qquad \tilde L_n \;=\; \frac{1}{2} \sum_{m \in \mathbb{Z}} \tilde\alpha_{n-m}^\mu\, \tilde\alpha_m{}_\mu, $$

where we have introduced the convention $\alpha_0^\mu \equiv \sqrt{\alpha'/2}\, p^\mu$ (closed) or $\alpha_0^\mu \equiv \sqrt{2\alpha'}\, p^\mu$ (open). The classical Virasoro constraints are simply $L_n = 0$ for all $n$ (and $\tilde L_n = 0$ in the closed case).

The $L_0$ constraint deserves special attention. Evaluating gives

$$ L_0 \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\alpha_0^2 + \sum_{m \geq 1} \alpha_{-m}^\mu \alpha_m{}_\mu \;=\; \tfrac{\alpha'}{4}\, p^\mu p_\mu + N, $$

(in the closed string normalization), where $N = \sum_{m \geq 1} \alpha_{-m} \cdot \alpha_m$ is the right-moving level number. Setting $L_0 = 0$ and using $p^\mu p_\mu = -M^2$ gives the classical mass formula

$$ \alpha' M^2 \;=\; 4N \quad (\text{closed, classical}). $$

The string's mass-squared is its internal oscillation energy, in units of $1/\alpha'$. Quantum corrections (normal-ordering) will shift this by a constant; that shift, plus the requirement that the Virasoro algebra close consistently, will fix the spacetime dimension in Chapter VIII.

For closed strings there is an additional condition: $L_0 = \tilde L_0$, i.e. $N = \tilde N$ — the level-matching condition. Left- and right-moving oscillator energies must be equal. Geometrically: the closed string is a loop and has no special point, so we cannot tell left from right without a reference — the symmetry between them is enforced by the constraint.

§6The string as a tower of oscillators

Stripping the formalism away, the picture is this. A classical relativistic string is a set of harmonic oscillators — one $\alpha_n^\mu$ for each integer $n \neq 0$ and each spacetime direction $\mu$ — sharing a common center-of-mass position and momentum. The oscillators move as Fourier components of a vibrating string, exactly as on a guitar; spacetime tension is what couples them.

Different excitation patterns are different particles. Set every $\alpha_n^\mu = 0$ and you have a massless point — the lowest-mass state. Excite the $n=1$ mode and you get a heavier state; excite $n=2$ heavier still. The integer $n$ labels Fourier mode; $\mu$ labels spacetime direction; together they index the string's infinite-dimensional space of internal states.

§8What we carry forward

  1. The classical string solves $\partial_+\partial_- X^\mu = 0$, with left- and right-moving sectors mixed by boundary/periodicity conditions.
  2. Closed strings have independent left and right oscillators $\tilde\alpha_n^\mu, \alpha_n^\mu$; open strings with Neumann boundaries identify them.
  3. Dirichlet boundary conditions on a subset of directions force the open-string endpoints to lie on a submanifold — a D-brane. This is the geometric origin of D-branes from the worldsheet.
  4. The Virasoro constraints become $L_n = 0$ on the modes; the $L_0$ component gives the classical mass formula $\alpha' M^2 = 4N$ (closed) or $\alpha' M^2 = N$ (open).
  5. Level-matching $N = \tilde N$ holds for closed strings — a topological fact about the loop.

Chapter VII promotes these classical oscillators to quantum operators, computes their commutation relations, and reads off the lowest few states of the string spectrum — including the massless spin-2 mode that becomes the graviton.
