Chapter XI

Compactification and Calabi–Yau Manifolds

The superstring lives in ten dimensions. Hide six of them in a tiny manifold of just the right shape and you get a four-dimensional world with the right amount of supersymmetry and a particle spectrum determined by topology.

§1The compactification idea

The superstring is consistent only in $D = 10$. We observe four. The standard resolution, going back to Kaluza in 1921 and Klein in 1926, is to hypothesize that the extra dimensions form a small compact manifold $K$:

$$ M_{10} \;=\; M_{1,3} \times K. $$

If $K$ has typical size $R$, energies below $1/R$ cannot resolve it and the world looks four-dimensional. With $R$ near the string scale ($\sim 10^{-34}$ m), the extra dimensions are easy to miss.

This works pedagogically because of two structural facts:

  1. Fields on $M_{10}$ decompose into Fourier modes on $K$, called Kaluza–Klein modes.
  2. Each KK mode looks like a 4-d field with a mass set by the eigenvalue of the Laplacian on $K$.

For a circle of radius $R$, the KK eigenvalues are $(n/R)^2$ with $n \in \mathbb{Z}$, so the 4-d spectrum is

$$ M_n^2 \;=\; M_0^2 + \frac{n^2}{R^2}, \qquad n = 0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \dots. $$

The zero mode ($n=0$) is the visible 4-d field; the higher modes ($n \neq 0$) form a tower of progressively heavier copies, invisible at low energies.

§2What we need from the internal manifold

For string-theory compactification, $K$ is a 6-dimensional manifold. We want the resulting 4-d theory to have several desirable features:

  • Solve Einstein's equations — at leading order, $\beta^g = 0$ requires $K$ to be Ricci-flat.
  • Preserve some supersymmetry — generally we want $\mathcal{N} = 1$ supersymmetry in 4-d, which protects the theory from quantum instabilities and matches the structure of realistic phenomenology.
  • Have a sensible spectrum of fields — set by the topology of $K$.

Preserving $\mathcal{N} = 1$ supersymmetry in 4-d means we need exactly one covariantly constant spinor on $K$. Geometrically this is a strong demand: most 6-manifolds admit no such spinor at all.

§3From SUSY to special holonomy

Parallel-transport a spinor around a closed loop on $K$ and it generally comes back rotated. The set of all possible rotations forms the holonomy group $\mathrm{Hol}(K) \subseteq SO(6)$. A spinor is covariantly constant if and only if it is left invariant by every holonomy transformation — i.e., if the holonomy group has a fixed spinor.

For generic 6-manifolds, holonomy is all of $SO(6)$ and no spinor is fixed. For special manifolds, the holonomy is reduced to a subgroup, and the more it is reduced the more covariantly constant spinors (and hence supercharges) survive. For phenomenology — a 4-d theory close to the Standard Model — we want $\mathcal{N} = 1$, so the canonical choice is a 6-manifold with $SU(3)$ holonomy. Such manifolds are called Calabi–Yau threefolds.

§4Calabi–Yau threefolds, geometrically

A Calabi–Yau $n$-fold is a compact complex $n$-dimensional manifold (real dimension $2n$) that is:

  1. Kähler — its metric is Hermitian and the associated 2-form $\omega = i g_{i\bar j}\, dz^i \wedge d\bar z^j$ is closed, $d\omega = 0$. Kähler manifolds carry a compatible complex structure, symplectic structure, and metric.
  2. Ricci-flat — $R_{\mu\nu} = 0$, so the leading-order $\beta$-function condition is satisfied.
  3. Equivalently to (1)+(2): the holonomy group of the Levi-Civita connection sits inside $SU(n)$ rather than all of $U(n)$.

Yau's theorem (proved 1977, published 1978) — which solved the Calabi conjecture — guarantees that on a compact Kähler manifold with vanishing first Chern class $c_1 = 0$, a Ricci-flat metric exists in each Kähler class. CY manifolds therefore exist; they are not just hypothetical structures.

Calabi–Yau, in one image

Geometrically, a CY 3-fold is the higher-dimensional analogue of a 2-torus. It is compact, has no boundary, carries a complex structure (you can locally write down holomorphic coordinates), and admits a non-vanishing holomorphic volume form $\Omega = \Omega_{ijk}\, dz^i \wedge dz^j \wedge dz^k$ that pins down its $SU(3)$ holonomy. The simplest non-trivial example is the quintic: the hypersurface in $\mathbb{CP}^4$ cut out by a degree-5 polynomial like $z_1^5 + z_2^5 + z_3^5 + z_4^5 + z_5^5 = 0$.

§5Topology determines particle content

The 4-d effective field theory after CY compactification has a particle spectrum set by the topology of $K$ — specifically, by its Hodge numbers $h^{p,q}$, which count independent harmonic $(p,q)$-forms. For a CY 3-fold the only independent ones are $h^{1,1}$ and $h^{2,1}$, and they have a direct physical meaning:

  • $h^{1,1}$ counts independent Kähler moduli — sizes of the various 2-cycles in $K$. Each contributes a complex scalar field to the 4-d theory.
  • $h^{2,1}$ counts independent complex-structure moduli — shape parameters of $K$. Each also gives a 4-d complex scalar.

The number of chiral matter generations in the resulting 4-d theory is fixed by the Euler characteristic,

$$ \chi(K) \;=\; 2\big( h^{1,1} - h^{2,1} \big), $$

via the relation $N_{\text{gen}} = |\chi|/2$. To match the three generations of the Standard Model we want $|\chi| = 6$ — a topologically very specific demand on $K$. For instance, the quintic has $h^{1,1} = 1$ and $h^{2,1} = 101$, giving $|\chi| = 200$, so it doesn't directly produce three generations; one orbifolds or otherwise modifies it.

§6The landscape

How many CY 3-folds are there? Not classified, but estimates suggest at least $10^5$ topologically distinct CY 3-folds, and on each one a moduli space of metric choices. Add in flux and brane configurations and the count rises to estimates of $10^{500}$ distinct vacua — the so-called string landscape. Different compactifications give different 4-d effective theories with different particle content, coupling strengths, and cosmological constants.

This is both a feature (it makes string theory rich) and a serious problem (we do not yet have a principle that selects a unique vacuum). It is the central open issue in connecting string theory to observed Standard Model physics.

§8What we carry forward

  1. Compactification on a compact manifold $K$ reduces $D = 10$ to four observable dimensions; KK modes give a tower of heavy 4-d states, invisible at low energy.
  2. Preserving $\mathcal{N} = 1$ supersymmetry in 4-d requires $K$ to have $SU(3)$ holonomy — a Calabi–Yau 3-fold: Kähler, Ricci-flat, complex.
  3. Yau's theorem guarantees Calabi–Yau manifolds exist for compact Kähler manifolds with vanishing first Chern class.
  4. The particle content of the 4-d effective theory is fixed by the topology of $K$, in particular by its Hodge numbers $h^{1,1}, h^{2,1}$ and Euler characteristic.
  5. The number of consistent compactifications is enormous — the "landscape" — and selecting the one that matches our universe is an open problem.
