Chapter XII

Dualities as Geometry

A string of radius $R$ is indistinguishable from a string of radius $\alpha'/R$. A Calabi–Yau is indistinguishable from another with swapped Hodge numbers. Strong coupling is indistinguishable from weak. The five superstrings — five vantage points on one theory.

§1T-duality on a circle

Compactify one direction of a closed string on a circle of radius $R$: $X^{25} \sim X^{25} + 2\pi R$. Two new things happen to the spectrum.

Kaluza–Klein quantization. The momentum in the compact direction is quantized: $p_{25} = n/R$ for $n \in \mathbb{Z}$.

Winding. A closed string can wrap the circle $w$ times before closing, with $w \in \mathbb{Z}$. The winding contributes a length $2\pi R w$ to the string, giving a winding momentum $\propto wR/\alpha'$.

The mass formula combines both:

$$ \alpha' M^2 \;=\; \alpha' \left( \frac{n}{R} \right)^2 + \left( \frac{w R}{\sqrt{\alpha'}} \right)^2 + 2(N + \tilde N - 2). $$

This is invariant under the simultaneous swap

$$ \boxed{\; R \;\longleftrightarrow\; \frac{\alpha'}{R}, \qquad n \;\longleftrightarrow\; w. \;} $$

This is T-duality. A string on a circle of radius $R$ has exactly the same physics as a string on a circle of radius $\alpha'/R$, provided we exchange KK momentum modes with winding modes. There is no experiment (within the string theory) that can distinguish the two.

The self-dual radius

At $R = R_* = \sqrt{\alpha'} = \ell_s$, $R$ maps to itself under T-duality. This is the self-dual radius, and the smallest meaningful circle radius in string theory. Geometry below $\ell_s$ doesn't exist as a separate notion — it is identified with geometry above $\ell_s$. A particle theory probing arbitrarily short distances has no analogue of this.

The geometric punchline is that string theory does not have a notion of "ever smaller distance." The smallest length is $\ell_s$. Below it, T-duality recycles small geometries as big geometries.

§2T-duality and D-branes

Where Type IIA has a D$p$-brane wrapping a direction $X^{25}$ that is compactified at radius $R$, the T-dual Type IIB theory at radius $\alpha'/R$ has a D$(p-1)$-brane transverse to $X^{25}$ at a specific location. The Neumann boundary condition along the wrapped direction becomes a Dirichlet condition in the T-dual frame, lowering the brane dimension by one.

This is how T-duality intertwines the two type-II superstring theories: it is not just a geometric symmetry, it exchanges the brane content. Type IIA on a small circle is Type IIB on a large circle, with each theory's branes mapped to the other's.

§3Mirror symmetry

For a Calabi–Yau compactification, the analogue of T-duality is mirror symmetry. The claim is that every Calabi–Yau 3-fold $K$ has a "mirror partner" $\tilde K$ such that

$$ h^{1,1}(K) \;=\; h^{2,1}(\tilde K), \qquad h^{2,1}(K) \;=\; h^{1,1}(\tilde K). $$

Geometrically, mirror symmetry swaps the Kähler moduli with the complex-structure moduli — it exchanges "how big are the cycles" with "how is the shape of the manifold parameterized." Two manifolds that look topologically different are physically the same theory of strings.

Mirror symmetry has been spectacularly useful even outside string theory. It led to the resolution of long-standing problems in enumerative algebraic geometry: counting rational curves on the quintic, for instance, was solved by mapping it to the much simpler complex-structure computation on the mirror manifold (Candelas, de la Ossa, Green, Parkes, 1991).

§4S-duality

Strings have a coupling constant $g_s = e^\Phi$, the exponential of the dilaton expectation value. Perturbative string theory works at small $g_s$. S-duality claims that some string theories are invariant under

$$ g_s \;\longleftrightarrow\; \frac{1}{g_s}. $$

The strong-coupling regime of one theory is the weak-coupling regime of another (or, in the self-dual case, of the same one). Specifically:

  • Type IIB is S-self-dual.
  • Type I at coupling $g_s$ is the same as the heterotic SO(32) theory at coupling $1/g_s$.
  • The heterotic $E_8 \times E_8$ theory at large coupling is described by M-theory on a line segment (Hořava–Witten).
  • Type IIA at large coupling grows an eleventh dimension and becomes 11-dimensional M-theory.

S-duality has no analogue in ordinary QFT outside special cases (4-d $\mathcal{N}=4$ super-Yang–Mills with Montonen–Olive). For string theory it is a generic feature, evidence that what perturbation theory sees is only a tiny slice of the full picture.

§5The web of dualities and M-theory

The five 10-d superstring theories are connected by an intricate web:

Pair Duality
Type IIA $\leftrightarrow$ Type IIBT-duality on a circle
Type IIB $\leftrightarrow$ Type IIBS-duality (self-dual)
Type I $\leftrightarrow$ Heterotic $SO(32)$S-duality
Heterotic $SO(32)$ $\leftrightarrow$ Heterotic $E_8 \times E_8$T-duality on a circle
Type IIA at strong coupling11-d M-theory on $S^1$
Heterotic $E_8 \times E_8$ at strong couplingM-theory on $S^1/\mathbb{Z}_2$ (Hořava–Witten)

The duality web showed (1995, the "second superstring revolution") that the five theories are five corners of a single underlying structure. The 11-dimensional unifying theory is called M-theory. We know much of its physics — its low-energy limit is 11-d supergravity — but a full microscopic formulation remains the great unsolved problem of the field.

§6What dualities tell us about geometry

Dualities are not symmetries of a particular spacetime geometry. They are statements that different geometries describe the same physics. This is geometrically profound:

  • T-duality says geometry below $\ell_s$ is not an independent notion. There is no shortest distance.
  • Mirror symmetry says manifolds with different topology can carry the same quantum theory. Topology is not absolute.
  • S-duality says spacetime configurations look completely different at different couplings — even the natural perturbative objects (strings vs branes) can interchange.

String theory's geometry is therefore "quantum geometry": classical geometry is what you see in a particular regime, but the true theory has a richer, less rigid structure. Dualities are the most direct evidence that this is the case.

§8What we carry forward

  1. T-duality identifies a circle of radius $R$ with one of radius $\alpha'/R$, and KK momentum with winding charge. It implies $\ell_s$ is the minimum meaningful length.
  2. Mirror symmetry identifies pairs of Calabi–Yau manifolds with exchanged Hodge numbers $(h^{1,1}, h^{2,1})$.
  3. S-duality identifies weak and strong coupling regimes — often of different theories.
  4. The five 10-d superstring theories are connected by these dualities into a single picture, with an 11-d ancestor called M-theory.
  5. Geometry in string theory is not the rigid classical object of GR. T-, S-, and mirror dualities all assert that different geometries can describe the same physics — geometry is something the theory does, not something it is.
