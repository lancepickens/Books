A Primer

Computational Chemistry

From the Schrödinger equation to foundation models — a tour of how we compute, sample, and learn the potential energy surface, in ten chapters with something to play with in each.

10 chapters · in browser · self-contained

  1. I The Potential Energy Surface, and the Problem Schrödinger refresher, the Born–Oppenheimer split, and why the many-electron problem is exponentially hard.
  2. II Hartree–Fock: Mean-Field Electrons Slater determinants, the variational principle, the Fock operator, and the self-consistent field.
  3. III Basis Sets and Correlation Gaussian basis sets, what mean-field misses, and the ladder MP2 → CCSD → CCSD(T).
  4. IV Density Functional Theory Hohenberg–Kohn, the Kohn–Sham construction, and Jacob's ladder of functionals.
  5. V Periodic Systems and Plane Waves Bloch's theorem, k-point sampling, pseudopotentials — how materials science actually computes things.
  6. VI Dynamics on the Surface Symplectic integrators, thermostats, ab initio MD, and free-energy methods that bias the walk.
  7. VII Excited States and Photochemistry TDDFT, CASSCF, conical intersections, and surface hopping — where the ground state isn't enough.
  8. VIII Learning the Surface I: Descriptors and Kernels Behler–Parrinello, SOAP, GAP, and why permutation and rotation invariance are the right priors.
  9. IX Equivariant Neural Potentials and Foundation Models Message passing, SchNet → NequIP → MACE, and the first generation of universal NNPs.
  10. X Quantum Computing for Chemistry VQE, qubit encodings, the unitary coupled-cluster ansatz, and the route to fault tolerance.

Conventions used throughout: atomic units ($\hbar = m_e = e = 1/(4\pi\epsilon_0) = 1$), so energy is in Hartrees ($1\,\mathrm{Ha} = 27.2114\,\mathrm{eV}$) and length in Bohr radii ($1\,a_0 \approx 0.5292\,\text{Å}$). Electron coordinates use lowercase $\mathbf{r}$; nuclear coordinates use uppercase $\mathbf{R}$. Operators wear hats ($\hat H$); one-body integrals are written in chemists' notation $(ij|kl)$. Citations follow Szabo–Ostlund for wavefunction methods, Parr–Yang for DFT, and Frenkel–Smit for molecular dynamics unless noted.
