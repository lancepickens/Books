Chapter III

Basis Sets and Correlation

Hartree–Fock left two big problems: a discrete basis that limits how flexibly orbitals can deform, and the correlated motion of opposite-spin electrons. Solving the first is bookkeeping; solving the second is most of the rest of wavefunction theory.

§1Why Gaussians, not Slaters

Atomic orbitals decay exponentially: an electron bound to a nucleus dies off as $e^{-\zeta r}$. The natural Slater-type orbital (STO) gets that decay exactly:

$$ \phi^{\text{STO}}(\mathbf{r}) \;\propto\; r^{l}\, e^{-\zeta r}\, Y_l^m(\theta, \varphi). $$

STOs are physically right but computationally awful: the four-center two-electron integrals $(\mu\nu|\lambda\sigma)$ between Slater functions on different centers have no closed form. For all but the smallest molecules they must be computed numerically, and there are $O(K^4)$ of them.

Boys's 1950 insight was that Gaussian-type orbitals (GTOs) have the wrong shape — flat at the nucleus instead of cusped, faster decay than exponential — but they have a magic property:

$$ e^{-\alpha |\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{A}|^2}\, e^{-\beta |\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{B}|^2} \;=\; K_{AB}\, e^{-(\alpha + \beta)|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{P}|^2}, $$

where $\mathbf{P} = (\alpha \mathbf{A} + \beta \mathbf{B})/(\alpha + \beta)$. The product of two Gaussians on different centers is a single Gaussian on a third center, with a known prefactor $K_{AB}$. Four-center integrals reduce to two-center integrals, which reduce to known integrals over a single Gaussian. Everything has an analytic form. The price — wrong cusp, wrong tail — is paid by combining several Gaussians into a single basis function.

A contracted Gaussian basis function is a fixed linear combination of primitive Gaussians,

$$ \phi_\mu(\mathbf{r}) \;=\; \sum_{p=1}^{N_p} d_{\mu p}\, g_p(\mathbf{r}; \alpha_p, \mathbf{A}, l, m). $$

The contraction coefficients $d_{\mu p}$ and exponents $\alpha_p$ are tabulated once (in a "basis set library") so that the contracted function approximates an STO or an atomic SCF orbital. A few Gaussians with cleverly chosen exponents reproduce both the cusp and the tail well enough for chemistry. Every modern code uses contracted Gaussians for molecules.

§2The basis-set zoo

Three families dominate.

Pople sets: minimal STO-3G (one contracted function per atomic orbital), then split-valence 3-21G, 6-31G, 6-311G. Add polarization $(d)$ on heavy atoms and $(p)$ on hydrogens with $*$ or $**$; add diffuse functions for anions and excited states with $+$ or $++$. 6-31G** means six primitives for the core, three+one for the valence, plus polarization. Cheap, ubiquitous, useful as a baseline.

Correlation-consistent (Dunning) sets: cc-pVDZ, cc-pVTZ, cc-pVQZ, cc-pV5Z, cc-pV6Z. Each successive set adds one more shell of higher-angular-momentum polarization functions, designed so that correlation energy converges monotonically toward the basis-set limit. The augmented prefix aug- adds diffuse functions; the prefix cc-pCVnZ adds tight functions for core correlation. This family is the workhorse for correlated calculations because it admits a known extrapolation to the CBS limit.

Karlsruhe (Ahlrichs) sets: def2-SVP, def2-TZVP, def2-QZVP. Designed for DFT and large molecules; balanced across the periodic table; in widespread use in TURBOMOLE and ORCA.

Two more knobs you will see:

  • Polarization functions — basis functions of higher angular momentum than the valence orbitals (e.g. $d$ functions on carbon). They allow orbitals to deform away from spherical symmetry — needed for any directional bonding.
  • Diffuse functions — additional Gaussians with very small exponents, providing amplitude far from the nucleus. Essential for anions, weakly bound electrons, Rydberg states, and the tails of donor–acceptor interactions.

Basis-set superposition error (BSSE)

When two molecules approach to form a complex, each can "borrow" basis functions from the other to lower its own energy artificially. The complex looks more bound than it is. The standard fix is the counterpoise correction (Boys & Bernardi 1970): compute monomer energies in the full dimer basis (with ghost atoms) and subtract. Disappears in the CBS limit; matters most at small bases.

§3What HF misses

Hartree–Fock treats each electron in the mean field of the others. Same-spin electrons are kept apart by antisymmetry (the exchange term); opposite-spin electrons are not — in the mean-field picture, two electrons of opposite spin can sit on top of each other. In reality they avoid each other. The energy gained from this avoidance is the correlation energy,

$$ E_{\text{corr}} \;\equiv\; E_{\text{exact}} - E_{\text{HF}}, $$

defined within a given basis. There are two qualitatively distinct kinds.

Dynamic correlation is the local, short-range avoidance of opposite-spin electrons — the Coulomb hole around each electron. It is what HF misses when the right ground state is still basically a single determinant: nearly all closed-shell molecules at equilibrium. Recovered by single-reference methods (MP2, CCSD, CCSD(T)).

Static (or non-dynamical) correlation is what HF misses when the ground state is not even close to a single determinant: stretched bonds, biradicals, transition-metal complexes with near-degenerate $d$-orbitals. The mean-field reference itself is wrong; perturbing around it fails. Requires multi-reference methods (CASSCF, CASPT2, NEVPT2), which we touch in Chapter VII.

Most "ordinary" chemistry — barriers, equilibria, vibrational frequencies of stable molecules — is dominated by dynamic correlation. The ladder of methods below handles it.

§4MP2: perturbation around HF

Write the electronic Hamiltonian as $\hat H = \hat H_0 + \hat V$, where $\hat H_0 = \sum_i \hat f(i)$ is the sum of Fock operators (a "non-interacting" Hamiltonian whose ground state is the HF determinant) and $\hat V$ is the fluctuation potential — what is left over of the true electron–electron repulsion after the mean field has been subtracted. Apply Rayleigh–Schrödinger perturbation theory to $\hat H_0$.

The zeroth-plus-first-order energy is the HF energy: $E^{(0)} + E^{(1)} = E_{\text{HF}}$. The first correction beyond HF is the second-order Møller–Plesset energy:

$$ E_{\text{MP2}} \;=\; -\frac{1}{4} \sum_{ij}^{\text{occ}} \sum_{ab}^{\text{virt}} \frac{|(ia|jb) - (ib|ja)|^2}{\varepsilon_a + \varepsilon_b - \varepsilon_i - \varepsilon_j}. $$

Where

$i, j$
Indices over occupied HF spin orbitals.
$a, b$
Indices over virtual (unoccupied) HF spin orbitals.
$(ia|jb)$
Two-electron integral in chemists' notation, in the molecular-orbital basis. Transforming these from the atomic-orbital basis $(\mu\nu|\lambda\sigma)$ is itself an $O(N^5)$ operation — the dominant cost of MP2.
$\varepsilon_a + \varepsilon_b - \varepsilon_i - \varepsilon_j$
Orbital-energy denominator. It penalizes excitations to far-removed virtuals, so the sum converges quickly for ordinary molecules.

MP2 typically recovers 80–90% of the correlation energy at a modest $O(N^5)$ cost — and at no additional cost beyond the integral transformation, once HF is done. It is the cheapest method that captures van der Waals attraction (though it overshoots for $\pi$-stacking and underbinds metallic systems). MP2 is the default first step beyond HF for almost any problem where correlation matters and you cannot afford coupled cluster.

§5Coupled cluster: the exponential ansatz

The deepest single-reference method available is coupled-cluster theory. Its central insight is that the exact wavefunction can be written as the action of an exponential operator on the HF reference:

$$ |\Psi_{\text{CC}}\rangle \;=\; e^{\hat T}\, |\Psi_{\text{HF}}\rangle, \qquad \hat T \;=\; \hat T_1 + \hat T_2 + \hat T_3 + \cdots $$

where $\hat T_n$ promotes $n$ electrons from occupied to virtual orbitals:

$$ \hat T_1 \;=\; \sum_{ia} t_i^a\, \hat a_a^\dagger \hat a_i, \qquad \hat T_2 \;=\; \frac{1}{4}\sum_{ijab} t_{ij}^{ab}\, \hat a_a^\dagger \hat a_b^\dagger \hat a_j \hat a_i. $$

The unknown amplitudes $t_i^a, t_{ij}^{ab}, \dots$ are determined by projecting the Schrödinger equation $\hat H e^{\hat T}|\Psi_{\text{HF}}\rangle = E\, e^{\hat T}|\Psi_{\text{HF}}\rangle$ onto excited determinants. Two non-obvious properties make the method powerful:

  • Size-extensivity. Because the wavefunction is an exponential, the energy of two well-separated subsystems is exactly the sum of their energies. Truncated CI does not have this property; truncated CC does. This is the single most important reason CC dominates high-accuracy work.
  • Even the truncated cluster operator generates all higher excitations. Expanding $e^{\hat T_2}$ gives $1 + \hat T_2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\hat T_2^2 + \cdots$, where $\hat T_2^2$ generates quadruple excitations. So CCSD ($\hat T = \hat T_1 + \hat T_2$) implicitly includes a controlled amount of higher-excitation character.

The standard truncation is CCSD: keep $\hat T_1$ and $\hat T_2$, drop the rest. The amplitude equations are nonlinear in $t$; they are solved iteratively until convergence.

The "gold standard" of single-reference electronic structure adds a perturbative estimate of triple excitations on top of converged CCSD:

$$ E_{\text{CCSD(T)}} \;=\; E_{\text{CCSD}} + E^{(T)}_{\text{pert}}. $$

The $(T)$ correction is non-iterative — built from converged CCSD amplitudes via a fourth-and-fifth-order perturbative expression (Raghavachari et al. 1989). Including it raises the cost from $O(N^6)$ to $O(N^7)$ but gives near-exact answers for closed-shell ground states in large basis sets.

§6The cost–accuracy plane

Stack the methods we now have by scaling and typical error in a CBS-quality basis:

$$ \underbrace{\text{HF}}_{O(N^4),\;\sim 1\%\text{ correlation}} \;\to\; \underbrace{\text{MP2}}_{O(N^5),\;80–90\%} \;\to\; \underbrace{\text{CCSD}}_{O(N^6),\;\sim 99\%} \;\to\; \underbrace{\text{CCSD(T)}}_{O(N^7),\;\geq 99.9\%}. $$

For closed-shell molecules at equilibrium geometries, CCSD(T) extrapolated to the CBS limit is accurate to roughly 1 kcal/mol in bond energies, barriers, and atomization energies — the "chemical accuracy" target. Below that, relativistic corrections, core correlation, and post-CCSD(T) effects start to matter; this is the territory of W4 / HEAT thermochemistry protocols.

For systems with significant static correlation (stretched bonds, biradicals, transition-metal centers), this hierarchy can fail badly. The single-reference assumption breaks; Chapter VII picks up multireference methods. For non-covalent interactions in large complexes, MP2 + counterpoise + a CBS extrapolation is often a reasonable compromise.

§7Extrapolating to the basis-set limit

For correlation-consistent basis sets, two empirical formulae get you close to the complete-basis-set (CBS) limit from finite-basis data. The Helgaker–Klopper extrapolation for the correlation energy is

$$ E_{\text{corr}}(X) \;=\; E_{\text{corr}}^{\text{CBS}} + A\, X^{-3}, $$

where $X$ is the cardinal number of cc-pVXZ (X = 2 for DZ, 3 for TZ, ...). The $X^{-3}$ scaling reflects the slow convergence of correlation due to the electron–electron cusp. The HF energy converges much faster — typically exponentially — and uses a separate formula. Doing a CCSD(T)/cc-pVTZ + CCSD(T)/cc-pVQZ pair of calculations and extrapolating gets you within ~0.1 kcal/mol of the CBS limit for many properties; this is the standard practice in high-accuracy thermochemistry.
