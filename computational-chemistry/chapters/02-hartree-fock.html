Chapter II

Hartree–Fock: Mean-Field Electrons

If every electron saw only the average cloud of every other electron, the $N$-body problem would collapse to $N$ one-body problems. Hartree–Fock is the price you pay — and the picture you get — for taking that shortcut seriously.

§1The mean-field idea

The electronic Hamiltonian of Chapter I has one term that makes it hard: the $\sum_{i

We cannot just throw away electron–electron repulsion, but we can replace it with a self-consistent average. Each electron sees, instead of the explicit Coulomb pull of the other $N-1$ electrons, the mean charge distribution they produce. The mean field is determined by the orbitals, the orbitals are determined by the mean field, and we iterate until they agree.

This is the Hartree–Fock approximation. Done correctly — with antisymmetry — it produces the orbital picture every chemist already uses to think about molecules.

§2Slater determinants

Antisymmetry is non-negotiable. The cleanest way to build an antisymmetric $N$-electron wavefunction from $N$ one-electron functions $\chi_i(\mathbf{x})$ — the spin orbitals — is the Slater determinant:

$$ \Psi(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2, \dots, \mathbf{x}_N) \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!}}\, \begin{vmatrix} \chi_1(\mathbf{x}_1) & \chi_2(\mathbf{x}_1) & \cdots & \chi_N(\mathbf{x}_1) \\ \chi_1(\mathbf{x}_2) & \chi_2(\mathbf{x}_2) & \cdots & \chi_N(\mathbf{x}_2) \\ \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ \chi_1(\mathbf{x}_N) & \chi_2(\mathbf{x}_N) & \cdots & \chi_N(\mathbf{x}_N) \end{vmatrix}. $$

A determinant changes sign when any two rows are swapped, so $\Psi$ is automatically antisymmetric under exchange of any two electrons (rows). Two electrons in the same spin orbital make two identical columns and the determinant is zero — that is the Pauli exclusion principle. The factor $1/\sqrt{N!}$ normalizes $\Psi$ when the $\chi_i$ are orthonormal.

Hartree–Fock makes one large assumption: $\Psi$ is a single Slater determinant. This is the smallest antisymmetric wavefunction we can write down. Chapter III will rebuild $\Psi$ as a linear combination of many determinants; everything in this chapter is what we get from one.

§3The variational principle

How do we pick the orbitals? Through the variational principle:

$$ E[\Psi] \;=\; \frac{\langle \Psi | \hat H_{\text{el}} | \Psi \rangle}{\langle \Psi | \Psi \rangle} \;\geq\; E_0, $$

with equality only when $\Psi$ is the exact ground state. So if we restrict $\Psi$ to be a single Slater determinant and minimize $E[\Psi]$ over the orbitals, we get the best possible single-determinant approximation to the true ground-state energy. That is the Hartree–Fock energy $E_{\text{HF}}$.

Substituting the determinant into $E[\Psi]$ and using the rules for matrix elements between determinants (the Slater–Condon rules, SO §2.3) gives

$$ E_{\text{HF}} \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} h_{ii} \;+\; \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j=1}^{N} \big[\, (ii|jj) \,-\, (ij|ji) \,\big]. $$

Where

$h_{ii}$
One-electron core integral: $h_{ii} = \int \chi_i^*(\mathbf{x})\bigl[-\tfrac{1}{2}\nabla^2 - \sum_A Z_A/|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{R}_A|\bigr]\chi_i(\mathbf{x})\,\dd\mathbf{x}$. The kinetic energy plus the electron–nucleus attraction of an electron in spin orbital $\chi_i$.
$(ii|jj)$
Coulomb integral in chemists' notation: $(ij|kl) = \iint \chi_i^*(1)\chi_j(1)\,\frac{1}{r_{12}}\,\chi_k^*(2)\chi_l(2)\,\dd 1\,\dd 2$. So $(ii|jj)$ is the classical Coulomb repulsion between charge clouds $|\chi_i|^2$ and $|\chi_j|^2$.
$(ij|ji)$
Exchange integral. Has no classical analogue; it is the term antisymmetry adds to mean-field repulsion. Same-spin electrons repel each other less than classical Coulomb would predict — they "exchange" through the antisymmetrizer.

The factor of $1/2$ in front of the two-electron sum avoids double counting; the $j = i$ term contributes zero because $(ii|ii) - (ii|ii) = 0$.

Two integrals capture the full structure of mean-field electron–electron interaction: Coulomb, which any classical theory would write down, and exchange, which is the price antisymmetry charges and the source of every interesting quantum effect HF describes.

§4The Fock operator

Minimizing $E_{\text{HF}}$ with respect to the orbitals (subject to orthonormality, via Lagrange multipliers) gives a single-particle eigenvalue equation. For each spin orbital,

$$ \hat f\, \chi_i(\mathbf{x}) \;=\; \varepsilon_i\, \chi_i(\mathbf{x}), $$

where the Fock operator is

$$ \hat f \;=\; \hat h \;+\; \sum_{j=1}^{N} \big[\, \hat J_j - \hat K_j \,\big]. $$

Where

$\hat h$
The one-electron core Hamiltonian: kinetic energy plus all electron–nucleus attractions.
$\hat J_j$
Coulomb operator: $\hat J_j(1) \chi_i(1) = \chi_i(1)\int |\chi_j(2)|^2/r_{12}\,\dd 2$. The classical electrostatic field of orbital $j$ acting on orbital $i$.
$\hat K_j$
Exchange operator: $\hat K_j(1)\chi_i(1) = \chi_j(1)\int \chi_j^*(2)\chi_i(2)/r_{12}\,\dd 2$. A nonlocal operator with no classical counterpart.
$\varepsilon_i$
Orbital energy. Loosely, the negative of the ionization energy from that orbital (Koopmans' theorem, §6).

This is the moment to notice the self-referential structure. The Fock operator $\hat f$ is built from the occupied orbitals $\{\chi_j\}_{j=1}^N$. Solving $\hat f \chi_i = \varepsilon_i \chi_i$ gives a new set of orbitals, from which we rebuild $\hat f$. The orbitals satisfy a fixed-point equation: the orbitals that diagonalize the Fock operator are the same ones that build it. That is what self-consistent means.

§5From integrals to matrices: Roothaan–Hall

In practice we never solve the Fock equation on a grid in $\mathbf{x}$-space. We expand each molecular orbital $\psi_i(\mathbf{r})$ in a finite basis of atom-centered functions $\phi_\mu(\mathbf{r})$ (Gaussians, in Chapter III):

$$ \psi_i(\mathbf{r}) \;=\; \sum_{\mu=1}^{K} C_{\mu i}\, \phi_\mu(\mathbf{r}). $$

Substituting into the Hartree–Fock equation collapses an integrodifferential equation into a generalized matrix eigenvalue problem — the Roothaan–Hall equations:

$$ \mathbf{F}\, \mathbf{C} \;=\; \mathbf{S}\, \mathbf{C}\, \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}. $$

Where

$\mathbf{F}$
Fock matrix: $F_{\mu\nu} = h_{\mu\nu} + \sum_{\lambda\sigma} P_{\lambda\sigma}\bigl[(\mu\nu|\lambda\sigma) - \tfrac{1}{2}(\mu\sigma|\lambda\nu)\bigr]$ for closed-shell molecules.
$\mathbf{S}$
Overlap matrix: $S_{\mu\nu} = \int \phi_\mu^*(\mathbf{r})\phi_\nu(\mathbf{r})\,\dd\mathbf{r}$. Atom-centered Gaussians are not orthogonal, so $\mathbf{S} \neq \mathbf{I}$.
$\mathbf{C}$
MO coefficients: column $i$ holds the basis-function weights of molecular orbital $\psi_i$.
$\mathbf{P}$
Density matrix: $P_{\lambda\sigma} = 2\sum_{i \in \text{occ}} C_{\lambda i}^* C_{\sigma i}$ (factor 2 for closed-shell double occupancy).
$\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$
Diagonal matrix of orbital energies.

The self-consistency is now visible in code: $\mathbf{P}$ is built from $\mathbf{C}$, $\mathbf{F}$ is built from $\mathbf{P}$, $\mathbf{C}$ is obtained by diagonalizing $\mathbf{F}$ in the $\mathbf{S}$-metric. Loop until $\mathbf{P}$ stops changing.

§6The SCF cycle

The self-consistent field (SCF) algorithm — Hartree–Fock with finite basis — is a fixed-point iteration:

  1. Build the one-electron matrices $\mathbf{S}$, $\mathbf{h}$ and all two-electron integrals $(\mu\nu|\lambda\sigma)$ from the basis and the geometry. (Done once, before the loop.)
  2. Guess an initial density matrix $\mathbf{P}^{(0)}$. (Often: $\mathbf{P} = 0$, i.e. core-Hamiltonian guess; or superposition of atomic densities.)
  3. Build $\mathbf{F}^{(k)}$ from $\mathbf{P}^{(k)}$.
  4. Solve $\mathbf{F}^{(k)} \mathbf{C} = \mathbf{S}\, \mathbf{C}\, \boldsymbol\varepsilon$.
  5. Build $\mathbf{P}^{(k+1)}$ from $\mathbf{C}$. Compute total energy $E_{\text{HF}}^{(k+1)}$.
  6. Check convergence: is $\|\mathbf{P}^{(k+1)} - \mathbf{P}^{(k)}\| < \tau$? If not, go to 3.

Plain fixed-point iteration is fragile — it can oscillate or diverge. In real codes the iteration is accelerated by DIIS, which extrapolates from previous iterates to suppress oscillation.

When the loop converges, you have the orbitals $\{\chi_i\}$, their energies $\{\varepsilon_i\}$, and the total energy. Koopmans' theorem reads chemistry off the orbital energies: the highest occupied orbital energy $\varepsilon_{\text{HOMO}}$ approximates $-\text{IP}$, the ionization potential.

§7What HF gets right, and what it misses

Hartree–Fock typically recovers about 99% of the total electronic energy and roughly the right shape for the wavefunction. It produces a correct orbital picture: HOMO and LUMO, bonding and antibonding, the symmetry adaptation of orbitals, the qualitative shapes that chemistry teaches every undergraduate. Geometries and dipole moments are usually within ~10% of experiment.

The missing 1% is the correlation energy,

$$ E_{\text{corr}} \;\equiv\; E_{\text{exact}} \,-\, E_{\text{HF}}. $$

It is small as a fraction of the total but comparable in magnitude to chemical bond energies, reaction barriers, and intermolecular forces — exactly the quantities chemistry cares about. Hartree–Fock energies are wrong by tens of kilocalories per mole. HF systematically underbinds molecules, overestimates bond lengths, misses van der Waals interactions entirely (they require correlation to exist), and breaks qualitatively at the dissociation of any bond made of more than one electron pair.

What HF leaves out is the correlated motion of opposite-spin electrons. Two electrons of opposite spin can sit on top of each other in the mean-field picture; in reality they avoid each other, and that avoidance lowers the energy. Same-spin electrons already avoid each other through exchange — that part HF gets right. The dynamics correlation of opposite-spin pairs is what every method in Chapter III adds back.

§8Restricted, unrestricted, open shells

Three flavors of HF appear constantly in the literature:

  • RHF (restricted Hartree–Fock): closed-shell molecules. Each spatial orbital holds one α and one β electron, so spatial orbitals are doubly occupied. The simplest and cleanest.
  • UHF (unrestricted): α and β electrons get independent spatial orbitals. Necessary for radicals and at bond-breaking geometries. Buys flexibility at the cost of spin contamination: the UHF determinant is not an eigenfunction of $\hat S^2$.
  • ROHF (restricted open-shell): doubly occupied orbitals share spatial parts; singly occupied orbitals get their own. Restores spin purity at the cost of more delicate equations.
