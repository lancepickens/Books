Chapter VI

Dynamics on the Surface

Once we have $V(\mathbf{R})$, the nuclei don't care how it was computed. Newton's equations turn an energy surface into trajectories — and free-energy methods turn trajectories into the thermodynamics most chemistry experiments measure.

§1Newton on the PES

The Born–Oppenheimer approximation has done its job: every electron-structure method in Chapters II–V produces a function $V(\mathbf{R})$ on which the nuclei move. If the nuclei are treated classically, their motion is governed by Newton's equations,

$$ M_A\, \ddot{\mathbf{R}}_A \;=\; -\nabla_{\mathbf{R}_A} V(\mathbf{R}), $$

one second-order ODE per nucleus. The gradient $-\nabla V$ is the force on atom $A$. In electron-structure codes it is obtained by the Hellmann–Feynman theorem plus, for non-stationary atom-centered bases, Pulay corrections — but as a user of MD you only need to know that "the code returns forces" alongside the energy at each geometry.

Quantum effects on nuclear motion (zero-point energy, tunneling, isotopomer differences) require path-integral methods (PIMD, RPMD) that we will not develop here; they bolt onto the classical infrastructure below.

§2Symplectic integration

To integrate Newton numerically, discretize time with step $\Delta t$. The simplest scheme that conserves the right quantities is the velocity-Verlet integrator:

$$ \begin{aligned} \mathbf{R}(t + \Delta t) &= \mathbf{R}(t) + \mathbf{v}(t)\,\Delta t + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{a}(t)\,\Delta t^2, \\[2pt] \mathbf{v}(t + \Delta t) &= \mathbf{v}(t) + \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl[\mathbf{a}(t) + \mathbf{a}(t+\Delta t)\bigr]\,\Delta t. \end{aligned} $$

Verlet is symplectic, time-reversible, and second-order in $\Delta t$. Energy is not strictly conserved at each step, but the drift remains bounded and oscillates around the true value, which is exactly what you want for an equilibrium simulation.

Higher-order symplectic integrators exist (Yoshida 4th-order, Forest–Ruth) but Verlet is the standard. For molecules the time step is set by the fastest vibration in the system. A C–H stretch has period $\sim 10$ fs, so $\Delta t = 0.5$–$1$ fs is typical; with constrained bonds (SHAKE/RATTLE) the C–H stretch is frozen out and $\Delta t = 2$ fs becomes safe.

§3Thermostats and barostats

Plain Verlet samples the microcanonical ensemble (NVE): constant energy and particle number. Experiments mostly happen at constant temperature (NVT) or constant temperature-and-pressure (NPT). To reach those ensembles in MD we couple the system to a thermostat (and barostat).

Three approaches dominate.

  • Berendsen rescales velocities by a factor that pushes the instantaneous kinetic energy toward the target. Fast, simple, but doesn't produce a rigorous canonical ensemble; suitable for equilibration only.
  • Nosé–Hoover couples the system to an extra "thermostat" degree of freedom; the resulting dynamics are non-Hamiltonian but conserve an extended energy. The dynamics are deterministic and time-reversible; correctly produces the canonical ensemble for ergodic systems. Standard for production MD.
  • Langevin adds a damping force $-\gamma m \mathbf{v}$ and a stochastic force $\sqrt{2\gamma m k_B T}\, \boldsymbol{\eta}(t)$ to the equations of motion. Stochastic, rigorous, and robust — the modern default in many biomolecular codes.

Barostats (Parrinello–Rahman, Berendsen, Martyna–Tobias–Klein) couple cell vectors to an external pressure and let the unit cell fluctuate. They live in the same algorithmic family as thermostats.

§4Ab initio MD

If the forces $-\nabla V$ are computed by an electronic-structure method at every step, the simulation is ab initio molecular dynamics (AIMD). Two main flavors:

  • Born–Oppenheimer MD (BOMD). At each step, converge the electronic SCF; compute analytical forces from the converged density; advance nuclei by Verlet. Conceptually clean; expensive because every step is a full SCF.
  • Car–Parrinello MD (CPMD; Car & Parrinello 1985). Treat the Kohn–Sham orbitals as additional dynamical variables with a fictitious mass; propagate them by Lagrangian dynamics alongside the nuclei. Avoids the SCF at every step, but requires a small time step ($\sim 0.1$ fs) to keep the orbitals on the BO surface.

For systems of a few hundred atoms over picoseconds, AIMD is routine on modern hardware with plane-wave DFT codes (CP2K, Quantum ESPRESSO, VASP). Larger and longer simulations demand classical force fields or — increasingly — machine-learned potentials (Chapter VIII–IX).

§5Free energies and rare events

Equilibrium properties are integrals over phase space, weighted by the Boltzmann factor:

$$ \langle \mathcal{O} \rangle \;=\; \frac{1}{Z} \int \mathcal{O}(\mathbf{R})\, e^{-\beta V(\mathbf{R})} \,\dd \mathbf{R}, \qquad \beta = \frac{1}{k_B T}. $$

The free energy along a reaction coordinate $s(\mathbf{R})$ — sometimes called the potential of mean force — is

$$ F(s) \;=\; -k_B T \ln \int \delta\bigl(s(\mathbf{R}) - s\bigr) e^{-\beta V(\mathbf{R})}\,\dd \mathbf{R}. $$

$F(s)$ at the saddle minus $F(s)$ at the reactant minimum is the activation free energy, the thing that controls rates by Eyring's $k = (k_B T / h)\, e^{-\beta \Delta F^\ddagger}$.

The fundamental problem of MD-based free-energy work is that ordinary dynamics rarely cross significant barriers. A 30 kJ/mol barrier has a Boltzmann weight $e^{-12}$ at room temperature, so on average the system stays in the reactant well $\sim 10^5$ times longer than it visits the saddle — crossings are already rare events on simulation timescales, and for the higher barriers typical of covalent chemistry ($\gtrsim 100$ kJ/mol) direct sampling would take longer than the universe is old.

The fix is to bias the dynamics — visit the rare states deliberately, then undo the bias in the analysis.

Umbrella sampling

Add a harmonic restraint $V_b(s) = \tfrac{1}{2} k (s - s_0)^2$ that holds the system near a chosen reference value $s_0$. Run a series of biased simulations at $s_0 = s_1, s_2, \dots, s_M$ that tile the reaction coordinate; reweight each biased histogram by $e^{+\beta V_b(s)}$; stitch the overlapping histograms together with the WHAM equations (Kumar et al. 1992) to recover the unbiased $F(s)$. The strategy is robust but laborious.

Metadynamics

Laio and Parrinello (2002) automated the biasing. As the system explores the chosen collective variables $s_1(\mathbf{R}), \dots, s_d(\mathbf{R})$, the simulation deposits a Gaussian "hill" of bias potential at the current location every $\tau_G$ time steps:

$$ V_{\text{bias}}(\mathbf{s}, t) \;=\; \sum_{t' < t} w\, \exp\!\left[-\sum_{i=1}^{d} \frac{(s_i - s_i(\mathbf{R}(t')))^2}{2 \sigma_i^2}\right]. $$

The accumulated bias progressively fills wells in the free-energy surface. Once they are level, the system diffuses freely between basins, and the negative of the deposited bias estimates the free-energy surface:

$$ F(\mathbf{s}) \;\approx\; -V_{\text{bias}}(\mathbf{s}, t\to\infty). $$

Well-tempered metadynamics (Barducci, Bussi, Parrinello 2008) makes the hill height decay with the local accumulated bias, $w \to w_0\, e^{-V_{\text{bias}}/(k_B \Delta T)}$, which makes the estimate converge rather than oscillate around the true $F$.

Variants and refinements abound (parallel-bias metadynamics, infrequent metadynamics for kinetics, OPES). The intuition is the same: don't wait for thermal fluctuations to drive rare events — deposit bias, force the system to leave wells, then reconstruct the free-energy surface from what you deposited.

The Müller–Brown potential (Müller & Brown 1979) is a 2D analytic surface with three minima and two saddle points — the canonical test bed for enhanced sampling. Plain Langevin dynamics at low temperature get stuck in the basin where they start. Switch on metadynamics and Gaussian hills are deposited every 50 steps, filling the visited well; the particle escapes, deposits more bias in the next well, and eventually sees the full landscape.

§6What MD tells you

From an equilibrium MD trajectory you can read off: structural distribution functions ($g(r)$, dihedral histograms), transport coefficients (diffusion, viscosity from Green–Kubo), vibrational spectra (from the velocity autocorrelation function), free-energy landscapes along chosen coordinates, and — given enough sampling — rates of activated processes.

The two costs are accuracy and time. Force-field MD (AMBER, CHARMM, OPLS, GROMOS) scales to millions of atoms and microseconds of simulation, but the underlying parameters are fitted to specific chemistries: covalent bonds don't break, polarization is rudimentary or absent, transition-metal chemistry is largely outside scope. AIMD captures all bond-making and bond-breaking but tops out at hundreds of atoms and tens of picoseconds. Machine-learned potentials (Chapters VIII–IX) aim to close this gap — to deliver AIMD-quality forces at force-field-like speed.
