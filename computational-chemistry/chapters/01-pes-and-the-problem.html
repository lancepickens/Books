Chapter I

The Potential Energy Surface, and the Problem

A molecule, to a computer, is one number that depends on the positions of its nuclei. Producing that number is the entire technical problem of computational chemistry.

§1What we are actually computing

Fix the nuclei of a molecule in space. Ask: what is its energy?

That single number, computed as the nuclei move, traces out a function

$$ V : \mathbb{R}^{3N_{\text{nuc}}} \longrightarrow \mathbb{R}, \qquad \mathbf{R} \mapsto V(\mathbf{R}). $$

This is the potential energy surface, the PES. It is the central object of this book. Almost everything we will compute is either a value of $V$, a derivative of $V$, a stationary point of $V$, a sample drawn from a distribution shaped by $V$, or — in the later chapters — a learned approximation to $V$.

The reason for its centrality is that physics gives you a lot of chemistry for free once you know $V(\mathbf{R})$:

  • Equilibrium geometries are local minima: $\nabla V = 0$ with $\nabla^2 V$ positive-definite.
  • Forces on nuclei are $\mathbf{F}_A = -\nabla_{\mathbf{R}_A} V$, so any molecular dynamics simulation is just integrating Newton's equations on this surface.
  • Vibrational frequencies are eigenvalues of the mass-weighted Hessian $\nabla^2 V$ at a minimum.
  • Reaction barriers are saddle points connecting two minima; reaction rates follow from transition state theory applied to the surface.
  • Thermochemistry — enthalpies of formation, bond energies, equilibrium constants — comes from values of $V$ at different geometries, dressed with partition-function factors.

If you have $V$, you have most of chemistry. The catch is that computing $V$ requires solving a quantum mechanical problem for the electrons, at every $\mathbf{R}$ you care about. The rest of the book is methods for doing that, ranked roughly by what they cost and what they can deliver.

§2Ninety seconds of quantum mechanics

The state of a quantum system is a complex-valued function on its configuration space, the wavefunction $\Psi$. Observable quantities are eigenvalues of self-adjoint operators acting on $\Psi$. The energy is the eigenvalue of the Hamiltonian operator $\hat H$,

$$ \hat H \Psi = E \Psi. $$

This is the time-independent Schrödinger equation. For an $N$-electron system $\Psi$ depends on $4N$ variables (three spatial coordinates and one spin label per electron), and $|\Psi|^2$ is the probability density of finding the electrons in a given arrangement.

Atomic units, from here on

We will work in atomic units throughout the book: $\hbar = m_e = e = 1/(4\pi\epsilon_0) = 1$. Energies come out in Hartrees (1 Ha $= 27.2114$ eV) and lengths in Bohr radii ($a_0 = 0.5292$ Å). The Hamiltonians below have no $\hbar^2/(2m_e)$ prefactors because both are equal to one.

That is essentially all we will need. The challenge is not the equation itself — it is solving it for systems with more than one or two electrons.

§3The molecular Hamiltonian

A molecule contains $N$ electrons and $M$ nuclei. Label the electrons by lowercase $i, j$ and their coordinates by $\mathbf{r}_i$; label nuclei by uppercase $A, B$, charges $Z_A$, masses $M_A$, coordinates $\mathbf{R}_A$. In atomic units the full molecular Hamiltonian has five terms:

$$ \hat H_{\text{mol}} \;=\; -\sum_{A=1}^{M} \frac{1}{2 M_A}\nabla_A^2 \;-\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{1}{2}\nabla_i^2 \;-\; \sum_{i,A} \frac{Z_A}{|\mathbf{r}_i - \mathbf{R}_A|} \;+\; \sum_{i

Where

$\hat T_n = -\sum_A \tfrac{1}{2M_A}\nabla_A^2$
Kinetic energy of the nuclei. The masses $M_A$ are in units of the electron mass; protons are $\approx 1836$.
$\hat T_e = -\sum_i \tfrac{1}{2}\nabla_i^2$
Kinetic energy of the electrons.
$\hat V_{en}$
Electron–nucleus attraction. The sum runs over every electron–nucleus pair.
$\hat V_{ee}$
Electron–electron Coulomb repulsion. The $i < j$ avoids double-counting.
$\hat V_{nn}$
Nucleus–nucleus repulsion. With $\mathbf{R}$ fixed, this is a number, not an operator.

Every term here is geometric. $\hat T$ is a Laplacian on the configuration space, telling us how the wavefunction varies in space. The Coulomb potentials are $1/r$ — the only force in the problem — pulling electrons toward nuclei and pushing them away from each other. There are no other interactions to worry about for chemistry: relativistic corrections matter for heavy elements, but at this level they are post-processing.

§4The Born–Oppenheimer split

Electrons are roughly two thousand times lighter than even the lightest nucleus, so they move much faster. Over the time it takes nuclei to rearrange noticeably, electrons have relaxed many times into their ground state for whatever nuclear configuration $\mathbf{R}$ they happen to find.

This separation of timescales motivates the Born–Oppenheimer approximation. We split the Hamiltonian into a piece that depends on the electrons (at fixed $\mathbf{R}$) and the nuclear kinetic energy:

$$ \hat H_{\text{el}}(\mathbf{R}) \;=\; -\sum_i \frac{1}{2}\nabla_i^2 \;-\; \sum_{i,A} \frac{Z_A}{|\mathbf{r}_i - \mathbf{R}_A|} \;+\; \sum_{i

The nuclear repulsion $\hat V_{nn}(\mathbf{R})$ is a constant at fixed $\mathbf{R}$ and we add it back at the end. We solve the electronic Schrödinger equation parametrically in $\mathbf{R}$:

$$ \hat H_{\text{el}}(\mathbf{R})\, \Psi_{\text{el}}(\mathbf{r}; \mathbf{R}) \;=\; E_{\text{el}}(\mathbf{R})\, \Psi_{\text{el}}(\mathbf{r}; \mathbf{R}). $$

The semicolon is a reminder that $\Psi_{\text{el}}$ is a function of electron coordinates $\mathbf{r}$ and depends parametrically on the nuclear positions $\mathbf{R}$. The PES is then

$$ V(\mathbf{R}) \;=\; E_{\text{el}}(\mathbf{R}) \;+\; \sum_{A

This is the operational definition of the PES. Every electronic-structure method in Chapters II–IV is a recipe for $E_{\text{el}}(\mathbf{R})$ at one nuclear geometry. The PES is what you get by doing that calculation many times, once per geometry of interest.

When Born–Oppenheimer fails

The approximation breaks down when two electronic states come close in energy — most importantly at conical intersections, where the gap closes to zero. Chapter VII spends time there because that is exactly where photochemistry, internal conversion, and many radiationless transitions live. For ground-state thermal chemistry, BO is essentially exact.

§5Why this is hard

The electronic Schrödinger equation looks innocent. The wavefunction lives in a ridiculous amount of space.

For $N$ electrons in three dimensions, $\Psi$ is a function on $\mathbb{R}^{3N}$ (plus spin). If we naively discretize each coordinate axis with $K$ grid points, we need $K^{3N}$ numbers to represent $\Psi$ on a grid. For methanol — a small organic molecule with 18 electrons — and a coarse 10-point grid per axis, that is $10^{54}$ numbers. The observable universe contains around $10^{80}$ atoms; we exhaust them long before reaching molecules of even modest size.

The wavefunction is also more constrained than a generic function: electrons are fermions, so $\Psi$ must change sign whenever we exchange the labels of any two electrons. Collecting each electron's spatial and spin coordinates into a single label $\mathbf{x}_i \equiv (\mathbf{r}_i, \sigma_i)$, antisymmetry reads

$$ \Psi(\dots, \mathbf{x}_i, \dots, \mathbf{x}_j, \dots) \;=\; -\Psi(\dots, \mathbf{x}_j, \dots, \mathbf{x}_i, \dots). $$

Antisymmetry is the deep origin of the Pauli exclusion principle and, ultimately, of all of chemistry: it is the reason atoms have shell structure, the reason two helium atoms repel each other at short range, the reason the periodic table is shaped like it is. Every method we discuss has to respect it.

If we expand $\Psi$ as a sum of antisymmetrized products of one-electron functions, drawing from a basis of $K$ spatial orbitals (and therefore $2K$ spin orbitals, counting α and β spin), the number of such products — called Slater determinants — is

$$ \binom{2K}{N} \;\sim\; \frac{(2K)^N}{N!}, $$

which grows factorially. The exact treatment, full configuration interaction, becomes intractable beyond about 18 electrons in any reasonable basis. This is the wall the field has been climbing around since the 1950s.

The history of computational chemistry is essentially a history of clever approximations that scale polynomially in $N$ instead of factorially, paying for that scaling with controlled errors. Each chapter introduces another rung on this ladder.

§6The hydrogen atom — and what stays useful

The one piece of the puzzle we can solve in closed form is the hydrogen atom: one electron, one proton, no electron–electron repulsion. The eigenstates of

$$ \hat H_{\text{H}} \;=\; -\frac{1}{2}\nabla^2 - \frac{1}{r} $$

are labelled by three quantum numbers $(n, l, m)$, with energies (in Hartrees)

$$ E_n \;=\; -\frac{1}{2 n^2}, \qquad n = 1, 2, 3, \dots $$

and wavefunctions $\psi_{nlm}(r,\theta,\varphi) = R_{nl}(r)\, Y_l^m(\theta,\varphi)$. The radial factor $R_{nl}$ is built from associated Laguerre polynomials; the angular factor $Y_l^m$ is a spherical harmonic.

What chemists actually use are real linear combinations of the $Y_l^m$: the familiar $p_x, p_y, p_z$ dumbbells, the $d_{z^2}$ donut-with-a-collar, the $d_{xz}, d_{yz}, d_{xy}, d_{x^2-y^2}$ clovers. Real combinations are eigenfunctions of $\hat L^2$ but not of $\hat L_z$. They are the orbitals every textbook draws because they are the ones that point along chemical bonds. Two things to take away from this widget for the rest of the book.

First, atomic orbitals are localized, oriented, and finite-ranged: they decay exponentially. This is why basis sets in computational chemistry (Chapter III) are built from atom-centered functions. The wavefunction of a molecule looks, near each nucleus, very much like an atom.

Second, every quantum number $(n,l,m)$ corresponds to a geometric feature you can see: $n - l - 1$ radial nodes, $l$ angular nodes, total $n - 1$ nodes. Counting nodes is how you read a wavefunction at a glance. We will rely on this kind of visual reading throughout the book.

§7A preview of the ladder

The remaining nine chapters are organized by how we attack the electronic Schrödinger equation, in roughly ascending cost and varying purpose.

  1. Hartree–Fock (Ch. II) replaces the interacting problem with a self-consistent one-electron problem: each electron sees only the average field of the others. The result is the orbital picture chemists already know.
  2. Correlated wavefunctions (Ch. III) — MP2, CCSD, CCSD(T) — add the correlation that mean-field misses. CCSD(T) is the gold standard of quantum chemistry, accurate to ~1 kcal/mol when it converges, but scaling as $N^7$.
  3. Density functional theory (Ch. IV) recasts everything in terms of the electron density $\rho(\mathbf{r})$, a function of just three coordinates rather than $3N$. With an approximate exchange–correlation functional it is the workhorse of computational chemistry and materials science.
  4. Periodic plane-wave DFT (Ch. V) handles crystals and surfaces, where the Born–Oppenheimer surface lives on a unit cell with Bloch boundary conditions.
  5. Molecular dynamics (Ch. VI) treats nuclei as classical particles moving on the PES, with free-energy methods like umbrella sampling and metadynamics added to bias the walk.
  6. Excited states (Ch. VII) — TDDFT, CASSCF, conical intersections, surface hopping — extends everything to electronic states above the ground state.
  7. Machine-learned potentials (Ch. VIII, IX) learn $V(\mathbf{R})$ from data instead of computing it from the Schrödinger equation. Kernels and equivariant neural networks now reach DFT accuracy at a small fraction of the cost.
  8. Quantum computers (Ch. X) attack the exponential scaling directly, encoding the electronic state in qubits and (the hope is) recovering polynomial cost for problems where every classical method fails.

The PES, in different chapters, is computed, sampled, traversed, learned, or simulated on quantum hardware. The frame holds throughout.
