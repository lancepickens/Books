Chapter V

Periodic Systems and Plane Waves

Atom-centered Gaussians are the wrong basis for crystals. Plane waves are the right one — and the moment you switch, the entire infrastructure of solid-state DFT falls out: Bloch states, Brillouin zones, k-point meshes, pseudopotentials.

§1Bloch's theorem

A crystal is a system whose external potential is periodic under translations $\mathbf{R}$ that belong to a Bravais lattice:

$$ v_{\text{ext}}(\mathbf{r} + \mathbf{R}) \;=\; v_{\text{ext}}(\mathbf{r}). $$

Translation by $\mathbf{R}$ commutes with the Hamiltonian, so eigenstates of $\hat H$ can be chosen to diagonalize all translation operators simultaneously. Each lattice translation can only multiply the wavefunction by a phase. The phase factors are characters of the translation group, and they are labeled by a vector $\mathbf{k}$ in reciprocal space. This is Bloch's theorem:

$$ \psi_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r}) \;=\; e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r}}\, u_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r}), \qquad u_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r} + \mathbf{R}) = u_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r}). $$

Where

$\mathbf{k}$
Crystal momentum, the quantum number that labels a Bloch state. Defined modulo a reciprocal lattice vector $\mathbf{G}$, so $\mathbf{k}$ lives in the first Brillouin zone — the Wigner–Seitz cell of the reciprocal lattice.
$n$
Band index. For each $\mathbf{k}$ there is an infinite tower of orbitals, labeled by $n = 1, 2, \dots$ in order of energy.
$u_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r})$
Cell-periodic part of the Bloch state. Reduces the unknown function from an infinite crystal to a single unit cell.

Geometrically: every Bloch wavefunction looks like a plane wave $e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r}}$ modulated by something that repeats with the crystal. Sliding by one lattice vector gives back the same function up to a phase $e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{R}}$. The reciprocal lattice $\{\mathbf{G}\}$ is generated by primitive vectors $\mathbf{b}_i$ satisfying $\mathbf{a}_i \cdot \mathbf{b}_j = 2\pi \delta_{ij}$ where $\{\mathbf{a}_i\}$ generate the direct lattice.

§2Plane waves as a basis

Periodic functions can be Fourier-expanded in reciprocal lattice vectors. Each cell-periodic $u_{n\mathbf{k}}$ becomes

$$ u_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r}) \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{\Omega}} \sum_{\mathbf{G}} c_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{G})\, e^{i\mathbf{G}\cdot\mathbf{r}}, $$

where $\Omega$ is the unit-cell volume. Combining with the Bloch phase, the full wavefunction is a sum of plane waves with wavevectors $\mathbf{k} + \mathbf{G}$:

$$ \psi_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r}) \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{\Omega}} \sum_{\mathbf{G}} c_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{G})\, e^{i(\mathbf{k}+\mathbf{G})\cdot\mathbf{r}}. $$

In this basis the kinetic-energy operator is diagonal — $-\tfrac{1}{2}\nabla^2$ becomes $\tfrac{1}{2}|\mathbf{k}+\mathbf{G}|^2$ — and any periodic potential is a sum of Fourier components. The Kohn–Sham equations become a matrix eigenvalue problem in plane-wave indices:

$$ \sum_{\mathbf{G}'} H_{\mathbf{G}\mathbf{G}'}(\mathbf{k})\, c_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{G}') \;=\; \varepsilon_{n\mathbf{k}}\, c_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{G}), $$

with

$$ H_{\mathbf{G}\mathbf{G}'}(\mathbf{k}) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}|\mathbf{k}+\mathbf{G}|^2\, \delta_{\mathbf{G}\mathbf{G}'} + V_{\text{eff}}(\mathbf{G}-\mathbf{G}'). $$

Plane waves are unbiased: they treat every point in the cell the same. They have no basis-set superposition error. They are the right basis for solids, surfaces, and any case where translational symmetry is the dominant feature.

The basis is truncated by a single number, the plane-wave cutoff energy:

$$ \tfrac{1}{2}|\mathbf{k} + \mathbf{G}|^2 \;\leq\; E_{\text{cut}}. $$

$E_{\text{cut}}$ is the single convergence parameter for the basis. Converging an energy difference to chemical accuracy typically takes $E_{\text{cut}} \approx 30$–$80$ Ry for valence-only calculations with pseudopotentials, much higher (hundreds of Ry) for hard pseudopotentials or all-electron methods.

§3The Brillouin zone and k-point sampling

Periodic-system observables are integrals over the Brillouin zone (BZ). The total electronic energy contains

$$ \sum_{n\in \text{occ}} \frac{1}{(2\pi)^d}\int_{\text{BZ}} \varepsilon_{n\mathbf{k}} \,\dd^d k, $$

where $d$ is the system's dimensionality. We approximate the integral by a finite sum,

$$ \int_{\text{BZ}} f(\mathbf{k})\,\dd^d k \;\approx\; \frac{\Omega_{\text{BZ}}}{N_k} \sum_{\mathbf{k}_i} w_i\, f(\mathbf{k}_i), $$

over a uniform mesh. The Monkhorst–Pack scheme places $N_1 \times N_2 \times N_3$ points on a uniform offset grid that respects crystal symmetry; equivalent k-points are reduced by symmetry weights $w_i$. A typical metal at the DFT level needs an $\sim 10^3$ point mesh; a wide-gap insulator might converge on $\sim 4^3$. The choice is the single most common convergence test in plane-wave DFT.

For metals, the discontinuity at the Fermi level makes BZ integrals slow to converge with $N_k$. Practical codes broaden the occupation with a smearing function (Fermi–Dirac, Gaussian, or Methfessel–Paxton) and extrapolate $T \to 0$ at the end.

§4Pseudopotentials

Plane waves have one terrible weakness: representing the sharp $1/r$ Coulomb singularity at each nucleus, and the rapid oscillations of core orbitals near it, would take millions of plane waves. The fix is to give up on the core entirely.

Pseudopotentials replace the all-electron Hamiltonian near each nucleus by a smoother potential, constructed so that:

  • The lowest valence eigenstate of the pseudopotential matches the all-electron valence orbital outside some core radius $r_c$ (typically 1–2 a₀).
  • Inside $r_c$, the pseudo-orbital is nodeless and smooth — representable in a few hundred plane waves.
  • The all-electron logarithmic derivative at $r_c$ is reproduced over an energy window (transferability).

Three flavors dominate. Norm-conserving pseudopotentials (Hamann–Schlüter–Chiang 1979; Troullier–Martins 1991) enforce equal pseudo- and all-electron norms inside $r_c$ — the strictest condition, the hardest cutoffs. Ultrasoft pseudopotentials (Vanderbilt 1990) relax the norm-conservation and gain factor-of-several-lower cutoffs. The projector augmented-wave method (Blöchl 1994) goes further: a linear transformation maps smooth pseudo-orbitals to all-electron orbitals inside an augmentation sphere, so that all-electron matrix elements can be computed from plane-wave calculations. PAW is now the default in VASP and ABINIT.

What you actually compute in a typical solid-state DFT run, then, is a plane-wave Kohn–Sham problem for the valence electrons in a PAW-pseudized external potential, sampled on a Monkhorst–Pack k-grid. The cost scales as $O(N_{\text{PW}} N_{\text{bands}} \log N_{\text{PW}})$ per SCF iteration (FFT-dominated), affordable for unit cells with hundreds of atoms.

§5What plane-wave DFT delivers

Lattice constants: typically within ~1% of experiment. Bulk moduli: ~10%. Vibrational (phonon) frequencies: a few percent. Cohesive energies: a few tenths of eV per atom. Band gaps of semiconductors and insulators: typically underestimated by 30–50% with semilocal functionals (LDA, PBE) — a structural failure linked to the same self-interaction error that affects molecular barriers. Hybrid functionals or many-body methods (GW, Chapter VII bullet) are needed for accurate gaps.

Plane-wave DFT is also the standard workhorse for surface science, defect formation energies, adsorption, and catalysis at solid interfaces. Heterogeneous catalysis modeling lives almost entirely in this framework. The supercell trick — embed a defect or a surface slab in a large unit cell with vacuum or extra solvent — lets you use the periodic infrastructure for almost any system, including individual molecules, if you don't mind some interaction with the periodic images.
