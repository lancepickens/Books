Chapter VIII

Learning the Surface I: Descriptors and Kernels

If $V(\mathbf{R})$ is just a function, why don't we fit it? The answer used to be "because it lives in $3N$ dimensions and respects symmetries no off-the-shelf regressor knows about." Atom-centered descriptors plus Gaussian process regression were the first clean way around both problems.

§1What a learned PES has to obey

A model $\hat V(\mathbf{R})$ that learns the energy from data has to respect three exact symmetries:

  • Translation: $\hat V(\mathbf{R} + \mathbf{t}) = \hat V(\mathbf{R})$ for any vector $\mathbf{t}$.
  • Rotation: $\hat V(R\,\mathbf{R}) = \hat V(\mathbf{R})$ for any rotation matrix $R$.
  • Permutation: $\hat V(P\,\mathbf{R}) = \hat V(\mathbf{R})$ for any permutation $P$ acting on atoms of the same element.

A vanilla neural net taking Cartesian coordinates as input has none of these. It will fit the training set but answer differently when you rotate or relabel — a non-starter. The trick is to feed the model only quantities that are already invariant under all three. These quantities are called descriptors.

Equally important: $\hat V$ should be size-extensive. Two non-interacting fragments far apart should have energy equal to the sum of their isolated energies. Behler and Parrinello (2007) achieved this with a simple ansatz that has dominated the field ever since.

§2The Behler–Parrinello decomposition

Write the total energy as a sum over atomic contributions:

$$ \hat V(\mathbf{R}) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N_{\text{atoms}}} E_i\bigl(\{\mathbf{r}_{ij}\}_{j \in \mathcal{N}_i}\bigr), $$

where $E_i$ depends only on atoms $j$ within a cutoff radius $R_c$ of atom $i$. Each $E_i$ is a function of relative coordinates inside a local sphere — translationally invariant by construction, and size-extensive because non-overlapping fragments contribute independent sums. Permutation invariance among same-element neighbors and rotation invariance still need to be built into the input descriptor.

Behler and Parrinello fed each $E_i$ through a per-element neural network, with the input vector built from atom-centered symmetry functions. The two standard types:

$$ G_i^{\text{rad}}(\eta, R_s) \;=\; \sum_{j \neq i} e^{-\eta (r_{ij} - R_s)^2}\, f_c(r_{ij}), $$
$$ G_i^{\text{ang}}(\eta, \zeta, \lambda) \;=\; 2^{1-\zeta}\!\!\sum_{j, k \neq i}\!\! \bigl(1 + \lambda \cos\theta_{ijk}\bigr)^{\zeta}\, e^{-\eta(r_{ij}^2 + r_{ik}^2 + r_{jk}^2)}\, f_c(r_{ij}) f_c(r_{ik}) f_c(r_{jk}), $$

with the cosine cutoff function $f_c(r) = \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl[\cos(\pi r / R_c) + 1\bigr]$ for $r < R_c$ and zero outside. Each $G_i^{\text{rad}}$ probes the radial distribution of neighbors at distance $\sim R_s$ with width $\sim 1/\sqrt{2\eta}$. Each $G_i^{\text{ang}}$ probes the angular distribution of triplets around atom $i$. A typical descriptor stack uses 30–80 such functions per atom with hand-chosen $\{\eta, R_s, \zeta, \lambda\}$.

Why these functions?

Summing over neighbors makes the descriptor invariant under permutation. The Gaussian-times-cutoff form is smoothly differentiable, so forces — the gradients $-\nabla_i \hat V$ — exist cleanly. The radial and angular forms together capture two-body and three-body structure; symmetry functions of higher order are possible but rarely needed in practice. Geometrically, each $G_i$ is a smoothed moment of the neighbor density inside the cutoff sphere.

§3SOAP: the smooth-density picture

An elegant generalization, due to Bartók, Kondor and Csányi (2013), starts from the local atomic density around each atom $i$,

$$ \rho_i(\mathbf{r}) \;=\; \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}_i} e^{-(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_{ij})^2 / (2\sigma^2)}, $$

Note: this $\rho_i$ is a Gaussian smear of the neighbor positions around atom $i$ — a similarity feature, not the electronic density $\rho(\mathbf{r})$ of Chapter IV. The shared glyph is unavoidable but the two objects never appear together.

and expands it in a basis of radial functions $g_n(r)$ times spherical harmonics:

$$ \rho_i(\mathbf{r}) \;=\; \sum_{nlm} c^{(i)}_{nlm}\, g_n(r)\, Y_l^m(\hat{\mathbf{r}}). $$

From the expansion coefficients $c^{(i)}_{nlm}$ build the SOAP power spectrum,

$$ p^{(i)}_{nn'l} \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{8\pi^2}{2l+1}} \sum_m c^{(i)}_{nlm}{}^{*}\, c^{(i)}_{n'lm}. $$

The sum over $m$ kills the rotation degree of freedom, making $p^{(i)}_{nn'l}$ rotation-invariant. Permutation invariance is automatic from the sum in $\rho_i$. The SOAP descriptor is the vector of all $p^{(i)}_{nn'l}$ for an atom $i$. Where Behler–Parrinello functions are a hand-picked discretization, SOAP is a continuous, geometrically natural representation that approaches a complete basis as the truncation $(n_{\max}, l_{\max})$ grows.

§4Gaussian Approximation Potentials

A descriptor turns each atom into a fixed-length feature vector. With a similarity (kernel) function $K(\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{p}')$ on feature vectors, we can do nonparametric regression via Gaussian processes. This is a Gaussian Approximation Potential (GAP; Bartók et al. 2010): predict each atom's energy as

$$ E_i \;=\; \sum_{t=1}^{N_{\text{train}}} \alpha_t\, K(\mathbf{p}^{(i)}, \mathbf{p}^{(t)}), $$

summing over training atoms $t$. The coefficients $\alpha_t$ are obtained by Gaussian process regression — fitting the total energies (and forces, and stresses) of training configurations under a Gaussian likelihood:

$$ \boldsymbol{\alpha} \;=\; (\mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I})^{-1}\, \mathbf{y}, $$

where $\mathbf{K}$ is the kernel matrix on training points, $\mathbf{y}$ is the training-target vector, and $\sigma_n$ is the assumed noise level. For SOAP-based GAPs the standard kernel is $K(\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{p}') = (\mathbf{p}\cdot\mathbf{p}')^\zeta$ with $\zeta = 2$ or $4$.

The Gaussian process picture has a useful side effect: it returns not just a predicted energy but a predictive variance. Wherever the kernel matrix indicates the test point is far from training data, the variance grows.

$$ \mathrm{var}[E_*] \;=\; K(\mathbf{p}_*, \mathbf{p}_*) - \mathbf{k}_*^\top (\mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I})^{-1} \mathbf{k}_*. $$

This is the signal for active learning: identify high-variance configurations during an MD trajectory, send them to a DFT (or CCSD(T)) calculation, retrain. Active learning converts the painful problem of "make a training set" into an automated loop that grows the set where it matters and stops where it doesn't.

§5Strengths, limits, and what comes next

SOAP-GAP and Behler–Parrinello neural-network potentials (HDNNPs) defined the first generation of ML potentials, roughly 2007–2018. Their successes are substantial: SiO₂ glass models with quantum-chemistry forces but classical-MD speed, Fe-C steels at scale, water with experimental accuracy. Both deliver smooth forces and stable MD over nanoseconds, the basic prerequisite for any production use.

Their limits are also clear. Three-body symmetry functions are blind to interactions that require four or more bodies for a faithful representation; kernel methods scale unfavorably with training-set size ($O(N_{\text{train}})$ to predict the mean energy — $O(N_{\text{train}}^2)$ for the predictive variance — and $O(N_{\text{train}}^3)$ at training, until sparse approximations are used). Both rely on hand-engineered descriptors whose parameters $\{\eta, R_s, \dots\}$ require trial and error.

The second generation, the subject of Chapter IX, replaces fixed descriptors with learned features that satisfy the symmetries by construction — and adds a network architecture that respects rotation not just by hiding it in invariants, but by tracking how features transform.
