Chapter VI

The Boundary Layer and Turbulence

Near the ground the atmosphere stops gliding and starts grinding: friction and turbulence churn the lowest kilometre, and the clean balanced flow of the free air dissolves into a froth of eddies we can never resolve, only average.

Before we start

Everything in the previous chapter assumed the wind was smooth — that you could draw a single arrow at each point and trust it. That picture is true a kilometre or two up, in the free atmosphere, where the only forces that matter are pressure gradients and the Earth's rotation. But it fails completely near the surface. The ground drags on the air, the sun heats it from below, and the result is turbulence: a chaotic stew of swirls of every size, from dust devils to invisible eddies a few metres across, all mixing momentum, heat, and moisture up and down. This bottom layer — typically the lowest few hundred metres to a couple of kilometres — is the boundary layer, and it is where the atmosphere actually touches the world.

We cannot track every eddy; there are too many and they are too small. So meteorologists do something clever and slightly desperate: they split every quantity into a smooth average plus a fluctuating wiggle, average the equations of motion, and accept that this leaves behind new terms — turbulent fluxes — that the averaged equations cannot themselves predict. Closing that gap is the central problem of this chapter. The simplest closure treats turbulence like a very efficient molecular diffusion; from it follow three of the most useful results in applied meteorology: the wind spirals as it descends to the ground (the Ekman spiral), it follows a clean logarithmic profile in the lowest tens of metres, and how vigorously it mixes depends on whether the ground is heating or cooling the air above it.

Terms you'll meet

Boundary layer
The lowest part of the atmosphere, directly affected by the surface through friction and heating — typically 0.1 to 2 km deep. Also called the planetary or atmospheric boundary layer (PBL / ABL).
Free atmosphere
The air above the boundary layer, where surface friction is negligible and the flow is nearly geostrophic.
Turbulence
Chaotic, three-dimensional, eddying motion across a wide range of sizes. It mixes far more efficiently than molecular diffusion.
Eddy
A swirl of fluid — a coherent rotating parcel. Turbulence is a population of eddies of many sizes.
Reynolds decomposition
Splitting a quantity into a slowly-varying mean plus a fast fluctuation: $u = \overline{u} + u'$.
Reynolds averaging
Applying that split to the equations of motion and averaging, to get equations for the mean flow.
Closure problem
The fact that averaging the equations introduces new unknowns (turbulent fluxes) faster than it introduces equations — so the system is never closed without extra assumptions.
Turbulent flux
The net transport of a quantity (momentum, heat, moisture) by correlated fluctuations, e.g. $\overline{u'w'}$.
Reynolds stress
The turbulent flux of momentum, $-\rho\,\overline{u'w'}$, which acts on the mean flow exactly like a frictional stress.
Eddy viscosity (K-theory)
The assumption that a turbulent flux is proportional to the mean gradient, with a proportionality constant $K$ — turbulence treated as a strong diffusion.
Mixing length
A typical distance an eddy travels before mixing with its surroundings; a way to estimate the eddy viscosity $K$.
Ekman layer
The part of the boundary layer where friction and the Coriolis force balance the pressure gradient, producing a wind that turns with height.
Ekman spiral
The shape traced by the wind vector as height increases through the Ekman layer — a spiral from the surface wind out to the geostrophic wind.
Hodograph
A plot of the wind vector's tip as you change height (or time) — the curve whose shape is the spiral.
Geostrophic wind
The frictionless balanced wind of the free atmosphere, parallel to the isobars (Chapter V).
Ekman pumping
The gentle vertical motion forced at the top of the boundary layer when frictional inflow converges (rising) or diverges (sinking).
Cross-isobar flow
Wind blowing across the lines of constant pressure rather than along them — a signature of friction, directed toward low pressure.
Surface layer
The lowest tenth or so of the boundary layer, where turbulent fluxes are nearly constant with height and the wind follows a logarithmic profile.
Logarithmic wind profile
The law that wind speed grows with the logarithm of height in a neutral surface layer.
Friction velocity
A velocity scale $u_*$ built from the surface stress; it sets the magnitude of the surface-layer wind and fluxes.
Roughness length
The height $z_0$ at which the extrapolated logarithmic wind formally vanishes — a measure of how rough the surface is.
von Kármán constant
The empirical constant $k \approx 0.40$ that appears in the logarithmic profile.
Static stability
Whether a lifted air parcel is buoyantly resisted (stable) or accelerated (unstable) — set by how temperature changes with height.
Virtual potential temperature
The potential temperature adjusted for moisture, $\theta_v$ — the right buoyancy variable for moist air.
Buoyancy flux
The turbulent heat flux $\overline{w'\theta_v'}$ that produces or consumes turbulence; positive when the surface heats the air.
Monin–Obukhov length
The height $L$ at which buoyant and mechanical (shear) production of turbulence are comparable — the master scale of the stratified surface layer.
Convective boundary layer
A daytime boundary layer driven by surface heating, full of rising thermals and vigorously mixed.
Stable boundary layer
A nighttime boundary layer where the surface cools, buoyancy suppresses turbulence, and mixing is weak.
Entrainment
The mixing of free-atmosphere air down into the growing boundary layer across its top.

§1What the boundary layer is

Stand in an open field on a sunny afternoon and the wind is gusty, swirling, never quite steady. Climb into an airplane and a few thousand feet up the ride smooths out. You have crossed a real boundary. Below it is the atmospheric boundary layer — the slice of atmosphere that feels the ground on a timescale of an hour or less. Above it is the free atmosphere, where the balanced flow of Chapter V holds and friction is a rounding error.

Two things distinguish the boundary layer. First, it is in direct frictional contact with the surface, so the wind must go to zero at the ground (the no-slip condition) and recover its free-atmosphere value only some hundreds of metres up. Second, it is turbulent: shot through with eddies that mix momentum, heat, and moisture far more effectively than molecular processes ever could. Turbulence is what makes the boundary layer a distinct object — without it, the influence of the surface would diffuse upward at a snail's pace and stay pressed against the ground.

The boundary layer breathes on a daily cycle, and the engine is the sun. After sunrise the ground warms, heats the air just above it, and sets off buoyant thermals that punch upward and stir the layer into a deep, well-mixed convective boundary layer. After sunset the ground radiates heat away, cools, and chills the air from below; buoyancy now opposes vertical motion, turbulence is throttled, and the layer collapses into a shallow, weakly-mixed stable boundary layer, sometimes only tens of metres deep. The whole structure inflates by day and deflates by night, and at its top, free-atmosphere air is continually mixed downward — a process called entrainment.

Note

"Turbulent" here is not a metaphor for "messy." It is a precise dynamical regime, reached when the Reynolds number $\mathrm{Re} = UL/\nu$ is enormous. For the atmosphere $\mathrm{Re} \sim 10^8$ or more, so molecular viscosity $\nu$ is utterly negligible against turbulent mixing — except in a millimetre-thin film right at the surface.

§2Reynolds decomposition and the closure problem

We have no hope of writing down, let alone solving, equations for every eddy. So we change the question. We ask only for the average wind, temperature, and moisture, and let the eddies enter as a statistical effect. The tool is the Reynolds decomposition: write each field as a mean plus a fluctuation.

$$ u = \overline{u} + u', \qquad \overline{u'} = 0. $$

Where

$u$
The instantaneous eastward velocity component (a field, $\unit{m\,s^{-1}}$) — the full, eddy-laden wind.
$\overline{u}$
The mean, formed by averaging over a window long compared with an eddy but short compared with the weather — typically tens of minutes. A smooth field.
$u'$
The turbulent fluctuation, $u' = u - \overline{u}$ (a field). It is what the average throws away.
$\overline{u'} = 0$
The fluctuation has zero mean by construction — averaging again changes nothing. This is the defining property of a Reynolds average.

The same split applies to every variable: $v = \overline{v} + v'$, $w = \overline{w} + w'$, $\theta = \overline{\theta} + \theta'$, and so on. The averaging operation obeys a few rules — it is linear, it commutes with derivatives, and the average of an already-averaged quantity is itself. From those rules a crucial fact follows: the average of a product is not the product of averages. For two fluctuating fields,

$$ \overline{u\,w} = \overline{(\overline{u}+u')(\overline{w}+w')} = \overline{u}\,\overline{w} + \overline{u'w'}. $$

Where

$\overline{u'w'}$
The covariance of the two fluctuations (a field, $\unit{m^2\,s^{-2}}$) — the average of their product. It does not vanish: if upward gusts ($w'>0$) systematically carry slower-than-average air ($u'<0$), then $\overline{u'w'} < 0$. This single surviving term is the seed of every turbulent flux.

The cross terms like $\overline{\overline{u}\,w'}$ vanish because the mean factors out of the average and $\overline{w'} = 0$. What survives, $\overline{u'w'}$, is the statistical fingerprint of turbulence — a measure of how the fluctuations in two directions move together.

Now apply this to the equations of motion. Take the horizontal momentum equation from Chapter IV, written for the eastward component, decompose every field, and average the whole equation. After the dust settles the mean flow obeys an equation that looks almost like the original — but with one extra term:

$$ \frac{\pd \overline{u}}{\pd t} + \overline{\vu}\cdot\grad\,\overline{u} = -\frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\pd \overline{p}}{\pd x} + f\,\overline{v} \;-\; \frac{\pd}{\pd z}\overline{u'w'} \;-\; \frac{\pd}{\pd x}\overline{u'u'} \;-\; \frac{\pd}{\pd y}\overline{u'v'}. $$

Where

$\overline{u},\ \overline{v}$
Mean wind components ($\unit{m\,s^{-1}}$) — what we are solving for.
$\overline{\vu}\cdot\grad\,\overline{u}$
Advection of mean eastward momentum by the mean wind (the nonlinear transport term from Chapter IV).
$\rho$
Air density (a field, $\unit{kg\,m^{-3}}$).
$\overline{p}$
Mean pressure (a field, $\unit{Pa}$); $-\tfrac{1}{\rho}\pd\overline{p}/\pd x$ is the pressure-gradient force per unit mass.
$f$
The Coriolis parameter $f = 2\Omega\sin\phi$ (a scalar at each latitude, $\unit{s^{-1}}$; here $f>0$, Northern Hemisphere), with $\Omega = 7.292\times10^{-5}\ \unit{s^{-1}}$ and latitude $\phi$.
$-\dfrac{\pd}{\pd z}\overline{u'w'},\ \dots$
The turbulent-flux divergence terms (a field, $\unit{m\,s^{-2}}$) — the new force per unit mass that turbulence exerts on the mean flow. The vertical one (in $z$) dominates, because gradients are far steeper in the vertical than the horizontal.

Here is the catch, and it is fundamental. To predict the mean wind $\overline{u}$ we now need to know the covariances $\overline{u'w'}$, $\overline{u'u'}$, and friends. If we write equations for those covariances — and we can — they turn out to depend on triple products like $\overline{u'u'w'}$, whose equations depend on quadruple products, and so on without end. Each level of averaging spawns more unknowns than equations. This is the closure problem, and it is not a temporary embarrassment but a structural feature of turbulence. There is no exact, self-contained set of equations for the mean flow.

WHY AVERAGING DOESN'T CLOSE

The nonlinear advection term $\vu\cdot\grad\,\vu$ is quadratic in velocity. Averaging a quadratic gives a covariance; writing an equation for that covariance requires the next moment up, because the original nonlinearity reappears at every level. A linear equation would close instantly — its average is just an equation for the mean. Turbulence is hard precisely because the Navier–Stokes equations are nonlinear. Every turbulence model is, at bottom, a guess that truncates this infinite hierarchy at some level and models the rest.

§3Turbulent stress and eddy fluxes

Before modeling the fluxes, let us understand what they physically are. Move the dominant term to where it belongs and multiply by density:

$$ \tau_{\mathrm{turb}} = -\rho\,\overline{u'w'}. $$

Where

$\tau_{\mathrm{turb}}$
The Reynolds stress (a field, $\unit{Pa} = \unit{N\,m^{-2}}$) — the vertical turbulent transport of horizontal momentum, expressed as a stress. It acts on the mean flow exactly like a frictional shear stress between fluid layers.
$-\rho\,\overline{u'w'}$
The flux written so that $\tau_{\mathrm{turb}}>0$ corresponds to a downward transport of eastward momentum (since fast air carried down, $u'>0,\ w'<0$, gives $\overline{u'w'}<0$).

The interpretation is mechanical and intuitive. Picture a horizontal surface inside the flow. An eddy that carries air upward ($w'>0$) drags up with it whatever eastward momentum that air had. If the air below is slow (which it is, near the ground, where the wind is weaker) then upward gusts carry a deficit of eastward momentum upward, and downward gusts carry a surplus downward. On average, faster air is shuttled toward the ground and slower air away from it — a downward flux of eastward momentum. That flux is a force: it decelerates the faster air aloft and accelerates the sluggish air below, smearing the wind profile toward uniformity. That is exactly what friction does, which is why $-\rho\,\overline{u'w'}$ earns the name "stress."

The same logic gives the turbulent fluxes of heat and moisture, which is where the boundary layer earns its keep in the climate system:

$$ H = \rho\,\cp\,\overline{w'\theta'}, \qquad E = \rho\,\Lv\,\overline{w'q'}. $$

Where

$H$
The sensible heat flux (a field, $\unit{W\,m^{-2}}$) — the rate at which turbulence carries warmth upward. Positive on a sunny day.
$\overline{w'\theta'}$
Covariance of vertical velocity and potential-temperature fluctuations ($\unit{K\,m\,s^{-1}}$). Warm rising air ($w'>0,\ \theta'>0$) makes it positive.
$\cp$
Specific heat of air at constant pressure, $\cp = 1004\ \unit{J\,kg^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$ (a constant).
$E$
The latent heat flux (a field, $\unit{W\,m^{-2}}$) — energy carried upward as water vapor.
$\overline{w'q'}$
Covariance of vertical velocity and specific-humidity fluctuations ($\unit{kg\,kg^{-1}\,m\,s^{-1}}$); $q$ is specific humidity.
$\Lv$
Latent heat of vaporization, $\Lv = 2.5\times10^{6}\ \unit{J\,kg^{-1}}$ (a constant).

These two fluxes are how the surface unloads the energy it absorbs from the sun back into the atmosphere — the lower boundary condition for the entire weather machine. The boundary layer is, quite literally, where the atmosphere talks to the surface, and these covariances are the language.

§4K-theory: turbulence as diffusion

We need a closure — a rule that gives the fluxes in terms of the mean fields we are solving for. The oldest and most useful is K-theory, also called first-order closure or the eddy-viscosity hypothesis. Its idea is a bold analogy: turbulence mixes momentum so efficiently that it behaves like a souped-up molecular viscosity, so let the flux be proportional to the mean gradient and point down it.

$$ \overline{u'w'} = -K_m\,\frac{\pd \overline{u}}{\pd z}. $$

Where

$K_m$
The eddy viscosity (a field, $\unit{m^2\,s^{-1}}$) — the turbulent analogue of molecular viscosity, but typically $10^5$ times larger. The subscript $m$ marks momentum; heat and moisture get their own $K_h$, $K_q$.
$\dfrac{\pd \overline{u}}{\pd z}$
The vertical shear of the mean wind ($\unit{s^{-1}}$) — how fast the mean wind changes with height.
minus sign
Ensures the flux runs down the gradient: from fast wind to slow, from warm to cool. Turbulence, like diffusion, erodes gradients.

The down-gradient form is exactly Fick's law for diffusion or Fourier's law for heat, with $K_m$ in place of the molecular coefficient. Substituting it into the mean momentum equation turns the awkward flux-divergence term into something familiar:

$$ -\frac{\pd}{\pd z}\overline{u'w'} = \frac{\pd}{\pd z}\!\left(K_m\,\frac{\pd \overline{u}}{\pd z}\right) \;\xrightarrow{\ K_m=\text{const}\ }\; K_m\,\frac{\pd^2 \overline{u}}{\pd z^2}. $$

The mean wind now obeys a diffusion equation, and friction has become a second derivative — a Laplacian in the vertical. That is enormous progress: the closed, solvable equations of the next three sections all flow from this one substitution. But honesty requires a caveat.

WHERE K-THEORY GOES WRONG

The down-gradient assumption says a flux can only flow from high to low values of the mean — but the daytime convective boundary layer flatly violates this. Large thermals carry heat upward through layers where the mean potential temperature increases with height, a counter-gradient flux that no positive $K_h$ can reproduce. K-theory is a local closure: it assumes eddies are small compared with the scale over which the mean varies, like molecules. When the dominant eddies span the whole boundary layer, that assumption breaks, and one needs non-local or higher-order closures (Chapter VII touches on how forecast models handle this). K-theory is right where eddies are small — which is exactly the surface layer, our next two destinations.

One way to estimate $K_m$ from physics rather than fiat is the mixing-length hypothesis: an eddy carries its momentum a typical distance $\ell$ before blending in, which gives $K_m \approx \ell^2\,\bigl|\pd\overline{u}/\pd z\bigr|$. Near the ground the eddies can be no larger than their height, so $\ell \approx k\,z$ — a guess that, as we will see, delivers the logarithmic wind profile exactly.

§5The Ekman layer and the spiral

Now we cash in. Consider steady, horizontally uniform flow above flat ground, with a constant eddy viscosity $K_m$ and a fixed geostrophic wind aloft that we orient along the $x$-axis, $(\overline{u},\overline{v}) \to (u_g,\,0)$ as $z\to\infty$. In the free atmosphere the pressure-gradient force balances Coriolis (geostrophic balance, Chapter V). Down in the boundary layer a third force enters — friction, now written as $K_m\,\pd^2/\pd z^2$ — and the three-way balance is the Ekman balance:

$$ K_m\,\frac{\dd^2 \overline{u}}{\dd z^2} + f\,\overline{v} = 0, \qquad K_m\,\frac{\dd^2 \overline{v}}{\dd z^2} - f\,(\overline{u} - u_g) = 0. $$

Where

$\overline{u},\,\overline{v}$
The mean wind components ($\unit{m\,s^{-1}}$), now functions of height $z$ alone.
$u_g$
The geostrophic wind speed (a constant, $\unit{m\,s^{-1}}$), built from the pressure gradient via $f\,u_g = -\tfrac{1}{\rho}\pd\overline{p}/\pd y$. We chose axes so the geostrophic wind is purely eastward.
$K_m\,\dd^2\overline{u}/\dd z^2$
The frictional force per unit mass (eddy viscosity acting on curvature of the profile).
$f\,\overline{v},\ -f(\overline{u}-u_g)$
The Coriolis terms; the $u_g$ stands in for the pressure-gradient force, since geostrophy fixes the two together.

This is a linear system, and it solves cleanly. With the no-slip condition $\overline{u}=\overline{v}=0$ at $z=0$ and the requirement that the wind approach geostrophic far aloft, the solution is the celebrated Ekman spiral:

$$ \overline{u}(z) = u_g\Bigl(1 - e^{-z/d}\cos(z/d)\Bigr), \qquad \overline{v}(z) = u_g\,e^{-z/d}\sin(z/d), \qquad d = \sqrt{\frac{2K_m}{f}}. $$

Where

$d$
The Ekman depth scale (a constant, $\unit{m}$) — the e-folding height over which the frictional deviation decays. With $K_m \sim 5\ \unit{m^2\,s^{-1}}$ and $f \sim 10^{-4}\ \unit{s^{-1}}$, $d \sim 300\ \unit{m}$.
$e^{-z/d}$
The decay envelope: the frictional correction shrinks by $1/e$ every depth $d$, so the wind is nearly geostrophic a few $d$ up.
$\cos(z/d),\,\sin(z/d)$
The rotation: the deviation from geostrophic turns through a full angle as $z/d$ grows, which is what makes the hodograph spiral rather than simply shrink.

Read the geometry off the formula. As height increases the tip of the wind vector traces a spiral — the hodograph — winding inward from the surface value to the geostrophic point $(u_g, 0)$. Right at the ground, expand the exponential and trig for small $z/d$: both components grow like $u_g\,z/d$, so $\overline{u}\approx\overline{v}$ and the surface wind points at 45° to the left of the geostrophic wind (Northern Hemisphere). Since the geostrophic wind runs along the isobars with low pressure on its left, a wind deflected leftward blows partly toward low pressure: friction makes the near-surface air spiral inward across the isobars.

Note

Be careful with two "depths." Our $d=\sqrt{2K_m/f}$ is the e-folding scale. The height at which the wind first swings back into alignment with the geostrophic direction (where $\overline{v}$ returns to zero) is $z = \pi d$ — Holton calls that the Ekman layer depth $D_e = \pi\sqrt{2K_m/f}$. The spiral reaches geostrophic near $z=\pi d$, not $z=d$. The factor of $\pi$ belongs to $D_e$ and nowhere else; it does not appear in $d$ or in the pumping formula below.

The idealized 45° deflection is larger than what is observed — real surface winds cross the isobars at more like 10–30° over land — because the constant-$K_m$ assumption is crude and the surface layer (next section) does not obey it. But the qualitative result is robust and consequential: friction turns the boundary-layer wind toward low pressure. That single fact drives the weather.

Ekman pumping

Here is the payoff. Because the frictional wind spirals inward toward low pressure, air converges into the bottom of a low. It has nowhere to go but up. Mass conservation then forces a gentle vertical velocity out the top of the boundary layer — Ekman pumping. Integrating the continuity equation through the layer, the convergence of the spiraling flow gives

$$ w_{\mathrm{Ek}} \approx \frac{d}{2}\,\zeta_g \qquad\text{(up over lows, down over highs).} $$

Where

$w_{\mathrm{Ek}}$
The vertical velocity at the top of the boundary layer (a field, $\unit{m\,s^{-1}}$), forced by frictional convergence below. Small — centimetres per second — but relentless and large-scale.
$\zeta_g$
The geostrophic relative vorticity (a field, $\unit{s^{-1}}$), $\zeta_g = \pd v_g/\pd x - \pd u_g/\pd y$. A low-pressure center has cyclonic spin, $\zeta_g > 0$, so $w_{\mathrm{Ek}} > 0$: rising motion.
$d/2$
Half the Ekman depth ($\unit{m}$); equivalently $\tfrac{d}{2}=\sqrt{K_m/2f}$. No $\pi$ appears here.

The sign is everything. A cyclone ($\zeta_g>0$) gets rising motion pumped through its boundary-layer top — cooling, condensation, clouds, the engine of weather. An anticyclone ($\zeta_g<0$) gets sinking motion — warming, drying, fair skies. And because that rising motion of a cyclone removes the very spin that drives it (this is Ekman spin-down), friction in the boundary layer quietly throttles the rotation of the entire system above. A mid-latitude cyclone spun down purely by molecular friction would take months; Ekman pumping does it in a few days. The boundary layer reaches up and brakes the free atmosphere.

§6The surface layer and the logarithmic wind profile

The constant-$K_m$ Ekman picture is wrong in its bottom tenth — the surface layer, the lowest few tens of metres. Here two things change. First, the eddies cannot be larger than their distance from the ground, so $K_m$ is emphatically not constant — it grows with height. Second, the layer is thin enough that the Coriolis force is negligible over it, so the turning of the wind can be ignored and the stress is nearly constant with height. That constancy is the defining property and gives the layer its other name, the constant-flux layer.

Constant stress means a constant velocity scale. Define the friction velocity $u_*$ from the surface stress:

$$ u_*^2 = \frac{\tau_0}{\rho} = -\overline{u'w'}\big|_{z=0}. $$

Where

$u_*$
The friction velocity (a constant within the surface layer, $\unit{m\,s^{-1}}$) — the natural velocity scale of surface turbulence; typically a few tenths of $\unit{m\,s^{-1}}$.
$\tau_0$
The surface stress (a scalar, $\unit{Pa}$) — the drag the ground exerts on the wind.
$\rho$
Air density ($\unit{kg\,m^{-3}}$).

Now combine constant stress with the mixing-length closure and the geometric fact that eddy size scales with height, $\ell = k\,z$. The down-gradient flux is $u_*^2 = K_m\,(\dd\overline{u}/\dd z)$ with $K_m = (kz)^2\,(\dd\overline{u}/\dd z)$, which collapses to $u_* = k z\,(\dd\overline{u}/\dd z)$, i.e. a shear that falls off inversely with height:

$$ \frac{\dd \overline{u}}{\dd z} = \frac{u_*}{k\,z}. $$

Integrate from the height $z_0$ where the wind formally vanishes, and you get one of the most-used formulas in all of applied meteorology — the logarithmic wind profile:

$$ \overline{u}(z) = \frac{u_*}{k}\,\ln\!\frac{z}{z_0}. $$

Where

$k$
The von Kármán constant, $k \approx 0.40$ (a dimensionless empirical constant). Not to be confused with any unit vector — it is just the number $0.4$.
$z_0$
The roughness length (a constant, $\unit{m}$) — the height at which the formula gives zero wind. Smooth surfaces have tiny $z_0$ (open water, $\sim 0.1\ \unit{mm}$); rough ones large ($\sim 1\ \unit{m}$ for a forest or a city).
$z$
Height above the surface ($\unit{m}$); the profile is valid for $z_0 \ll z \lesssim$ (surface-layer top).
$\ln$
The natural logarithm. Wind grows fast near the ground, then ever more slowly — equal ratios of height add equal increments of wind.

The logarithmic shape is the signature of the constant-flux layer, and it is the reason a 10 m anemometer reading can be sensibly translated to the 80 m hub height of a wind turbine, or why surface gusts and aircraft winds differ so sharply. The roughness length $z_0$ packages everything about the surface — grass, ocean swell, skyscrapers — into a single number. This profile holds, however, only when the air is neither heating nor cooling from below. The moment buoyancy enters, the log law bends.

§7Stability and the Monin–Obukhov length

Turbulence has two engines. Shear generates it mechanically — the wind tearing past itself, the energy source behind the log law. Buoyancy generates or destroys it thermally — on a sunny day, warm parcels rising freely add turbulent energy; on a clear night, the cold dense surface air resists being lifted and saps it. The character of the surface layer depends entirely on which engine wins, and the variable that decides static stability for moist air is the virtual potential temperature $\theta_v$.

Monin and Obukhov's insight (1954) was that the entire stratified surface layer is governed by a single length scale, formed from the competition between the two engines. Mechanical production of turbulence scales with $u_*^3$; buoyant production scales with the surface buoyancy flux $\overline{w'\theta_v'}$. Their ratio defines a height — the Monin–Obukhov length:

$$ L = -\frac{u_*^3\,\overline{\theta_v}}{k\,g\,\overline{w'\theta_v'}}. $$

Where

$L$
The Monin–Obukhov length (a scalar, $\unit{m}$) — the height at which buoyant and shear production of turbulence are comparable. Its sign reports the stability; its magnitude reports how strongly.
$u_*$
Friction velocity ($\unit{m\,s^{-1}}$); the $u_*^3$ measures mechanical (shear) production.
$\overline{\theta_v}$
Mean virtual potential temperature ($\unit{K}$) — the reference buoyancy state; here just a normalizing temperature near $288\ \unit{K}$.
$g$
Gravitational acceleration, $g = 9.81\ \unit{m\,s^{-2}}$ (a constant); $g/\overline{\theta_v}$ converts a temperature anomaly into a buoyant acceleration.
$\overline{w'\theta_v'}$
The surface buoyancy flux (a scalar, $\unit{K\,m\,s^{-1}}$) — the turbulent vertical heat flux that drives buoyant production. Positive when the surface heats the air.
$k$
von Kármán constant, $0.40$ (dimensionless).

The minus sign makes the sign convention work out as follows.

  • Unstable (daytime, $L<0$). The surface heats the air, so $\overline{w'\theta_v'}>0$, and the minus sign makes $L$ negative. Buoyancy adds turbulence; thermals rise; the layer mixes vigorously. The convective boundary layer.
  • Stable (nighttime, $L>0$). The surface cools the air, so $\overline{w'\theta_v'}<0$, and $L$ comes out positive. Buoyancy suppresses turbulence; mixing is throttled; the layer is shallow. The stable boundary layer.
  • Neutral ($L\to\pm\infty$). No surface heat flux, $\overline{w'\theta_v'}\to 0$; buoyancy is irrelevant and shear rules. This is the regime in which the plain log law of §6 is exact.

The dimensionless height $z/L$ is the master variable. When $|z/L|\ll 1$ — close to the ground, or under strong winds — mechanical production dominates and the flow is effectively neutral; the log law holds. When $|z/L|\gtrsim 1$, buoyancy takes over and the profiles must be corrected. Monin–Obukhov similarity theory asserts that once you nondimensionalize with $u_*$, $z$, and $L$, the wind and temperature profiles become universal functions of $z/L$ alone, the same over any flat surface on Earth. The neutral log law gets a stability correction $\psi_m(z/L)$:

$$ \overline{u}(z) = \frac{u_*}{k}\left[\ln\!\frac{z}{z_0} - \psi_m\!\left(\frac{z}{L}\right)\right]. $$

Where

$\psi_m(z/L)$
The stability correction function (dimensionless) — an empirically-determined universal function. Unstable conditions ($z/L<0$) make $\psi_m>0$, weakening the shear (more mixing, flatter profile); stable conditions ($z/L>0$) make $\psi_m<0$, sharpening it. It vanishes in the neutral limit, recovering the log law.

This is the quiet workhorse beneath every weather and climate model. To couple the atmosphere to the land or ocean, a model needs the surface fluxes of momentum, heat, and moisture given the wind, temperature, and humidity at the lowest model level — and Monin–Obukhov similarity is how nearly every model computes them. The 45° spiral, the log law, and the $z/L$ corrections are not curiosities; they are the bottom boundary condition for the atmosphere in motion. The next chapter takes the spiraling, converging, frictionally-braked flow of this one and lets it organize itself into the great cyclones and fronts of the mid-latitudes.
