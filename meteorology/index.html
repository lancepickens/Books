An Introduction

Meteorology

Ten chapters on the atmosphere as a moist, compressible fluid on a rotating planet — from hydrostatic balance to baroclinic storms to machine-learning forecasts. With something to play with in every chapter.

10 chapters · in browser · self-contained

  1. I The Atmosphere as a System Composition, the vertical structure of the layers, the ideal-gas and hydrostatic balance that fix pressure with height, and the scale height that sets the atmosphere's thinness.
  2. II Atmospheric Thermodynamics The first law for a moving parcel, potential temperature, dry and moist adiabats, the Clausius–Clapeyron law, and the stability that decides whether air rises — read off a Skew-T.
  3. III Radiation and the Energy Balance Blackbody emission, the Stefan–Boltzmann and Schwarzschild laws, the emission temperature of the planet, and how a gray absorbing atmosphere warms the surface — the greenhouse effect, derived.
  4. IV The Equations of Motion The material derivative, Newton's second law in a rotating frame, the Coriolis and centrifugal forces, continuity, and the scale analysis that turns the full equations into the ones forecasters use.
  5. V Balanced Flow and Vorticity Geostrophic and gradient wind, the thermal wind that ties wind shear to temperature gradients, vorticity and Ertel's potential vorticity, and the Rossby waves PV makes possible.
  6. VI The Boundary Layer and Turbulence Reynolds averaging and turbulent fluxes, the Ekman spiral that turns the wind toward low pressure near the ground, the logarithmic surface layer, and Monin–Obukhov similarity.
  7. VII Mid-latitude Weather Systems Quasi-geostrophic theory, the omega equation and Q-vectors, baroclinic instability through the Eady model, and how growing waves become the cyclones and fronts of the daily map.
  8. VIII Moist Convection and Mesoscale Storms Buoyancy and CAPE, the role of wind shear, ordinary cells versus supercells and squall lines, storm-relative helicity, and the hurricane read as a Carnot heat engine.
  9. IX The General Circulation and Climate Hadley, Ferrel, and polar cells, the angular-momentum budget that builds the jet, eddy transports, ENSO and monsoons, and a one-dimensional energy-balance model with ice–albedo feedback.
  10. X Numerical Prediction and the Frontier Discretizing the primitive equations, the CFL condition, data assimilation, chaos and the Lorenz attractor, ensemble forecasting, and the machine-learning models now rivaling the great physics codes.

Conventions used throughout: SI units, with pressure quoted in hectopascals (hPa, equal to millibars). Vectors in bold ($\mathbf{u}$, $\mathbf{V}$). Height $z$ is the vertical coordinate in Chapters I–III and VI; pressure $p$ with geopotential $\Phi$ is the vertical coordinate for the large-scale dynamics of Chapters V, VII, and IX, with the switch flagged where it happens. Reference constants: $g = 9.81\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$, $R_d = 287\ \mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}K^{-1}}$, $c_p = 1004\ \mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}K^{-1}}$, $\Omega = 7.292\times10^{-5}\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}$, $\sigma = 5.67\times10^{-8}\ \mathrm{W\,m^{-2}K^{-4}}$, reference pressure $p_0 = 1000\ \mathrm{hPa}$. The rotating-frame conventions are Northern Hemisphere, Coriolis parameter $f > 0$. Sources cited inline are real and verifiable — textbook section or arXiv ID given where appropriate. The audience is assumed comfortable with multivariable calculus and introductory physics.
