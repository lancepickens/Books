A Modern Introduction

Machine Learning

Ten chapters from the learning problem to the 2026 frontier — transformers, alignment, reasoning models, agents, diffusion. With something to play with in every chapter.

10 chapters · in browser · self-contained

  1. I Foundations: Data, Loss, and the Learning Problem Empirical risk minimization, generalization, and the bias–variance picture that underlies every method to follow.
  2. II Linear Models, Regularization, and the Kernel Bridge Linear and logistic regression as geometry, the ridge/lasso path, and how kernels reach nonlinearity without leaving the linear toolbox.
  3. III Neural Networks and Backpropagation MLPs as compositions of differentiable maps, reverse-mode autodiff, and the optimizers (SGD, Adam, AdamW) that make modern training work.
  4. IV Vision: Convolutions, ResNets, and Vision Transformers Translation equivariance, why residual connections changed everything, and the move from CNNs to ViTs and image foundation models.
  5. V Sequences, Attention, and the Transformer Self-attention as a soft lookup, the decoder-only block, RoPE positions, and the residual-stream view that the rest of the book runs on.
  6. VI Pretraining and Scaling Laws Self-supervised next-token prediction, tokenization, Chinchilla-style compute-optimal scaling, MoE, and the alternatives challenging dense transformers.
  7. VII From Base Model to Assistant: SFT, RLHF, DPO Supervised fine-tuning, reward modeling, PPO, the closed-form DPO objective, constitutional methods, and parameter-efficient adaptation.
  8. VIII Reasoning and Test-Time Compute Chain-of-thought, the o-series shift, RL with verifiable rewards, best-of-N, and how spending more compute at inference changes what models can do.
  9. IX Agents, Tool Use, and Retrieval Function calling, MCP, ReAct loops, planning, code agents, and the embedding/retrieval stack that grounds them in real data.
  10. X Generative Beyond Text, and the Frontier Diffusion and flow matching, multimodal models, video and world models, mechanistic interpretability, and the open questions of 2026.

Conventions used throughout: vectors in bold lowercase ($\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{w}$); matrices in bold uppercase ($\mathbf{W}, \mathbf{X}$); parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}$; loss $\mathcal{L}$; expectation $\mathbb{E}$; data distribution $\mathcal{D}$; KL divergence $\mathrm{KL}$. Logs are natural unless noted. Datasets, papers and library names cited inline are real and verifiable; arXiv IDs and venue/year given where appropriate. The audience is assumed comfortable with multivariable calculus, linear algebra, and basic probability.
