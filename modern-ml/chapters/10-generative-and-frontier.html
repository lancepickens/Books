Chapter X

Generative Beyond Text, and the Frontier

Language was the first modality to fall to transformers. Images, audio, and video took a different path — through diffusion — and the question of what to do once you can generate anything is now most of what serious research is about.

Before we start

How do you generate a realistic image, audio clip, or video? Two early answers — VAEs and GANs — were each clever and limited. The dominant idea since 2021 is diffusion: train a network to gradually remove noise from a noisy image, then run that in reverse from pure noise to get a fresh sample. The math is more elegant than it sounds, and the chapter builds it up step by step.

Then a tour of the frontier as of 2026. Multimodal foundation models (GPT-4o, Claude, Gemini, Llama 4) handle text, images, audio, and video in a shared backbone. World models learn to predict how scenes evolve. Mechanistic interpretability tries to reverse-engineer what models actually do inside, using tools like sparse autoencoders to find human-meaningful "features" in their activations. The chapter closes with the open questions nobody has answered — where scaling ends, how to align increasingly capable systems, and whether models can ever self-improve.

Terms you'll meet

Generative model
A model that learns the distribution of the training data and can sample from it (generate new examples), as opposed to a discriminative model that just classifies.
VAE (Variational Autoencoder)
A 2013 generative model: an encoder maps inputs to a probability distribution over a latent space, a decoder maps latents back to inputs. Clean theory, tractable training, samples tend to be blurry.
ELBO (Evidence Lower Bound)
The trainable proxy for log-likelihood that VAEs maximize. Reconstruction quality minus a KL term that keeps latents well-behaved.
Encoder / decoder / latent
Encoder maps input → latent; decoder maps latent → reconstruction; the latent is a low-dimensional compressed representation in between.
GAN (Generative Adversarial Network)
A 2014 generative model: a generator and a discriminator play a minimax game until the generator's samples are indistinguishable from real. Sharp samples, unstable training, dominant 2015–2020.
Discriminator
The "judge" network in a GAN: tries to tell real samples from generated ones.
Diffusion model
The dominant generative paradigm since 2021. Train a network to denoise; sample by starting from pure noise and iteratively denoising.
DDPM
Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models — Ho, Jain & Abbeel 2020, the paper that made diffusion practical at scale. The canonical formulation.
Forward process
The fixed (not learned) procedure that gradually adds Gaussian noise to a clean sample over many steps until it's indistinguishable from random noise.
Reverse process
The learned procedure that gradually removes noise — the actual generative model.
Noise schedule ($\beta_t$, $\bar\alpha_t$)
The fixed sequence of noise levels used by the forward process. $\beta_t$ = noise added at step $t$; $\bar\alpha_t$ = total signal remaining at step $t$.
Denoising network ($\eps_\theta$)
The neural network at the heart of a diffusion model. Given a noisy sample and a timestep, predicts the noise that was added.
U-Net
An encoder–decoder CNN with skip connections from each encoder layer to the matching decoder layer. The standard backbone for image-diffusion models.
Score
The gradient of log-density: $\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} \log p(\mathbf{x})$. Points "uphill" toward higher data probability. The denoising network is equivalent to a score estimator.
Score matching
An alternative formulation: learn the score directly instead of learning to predict noise. Mathematically equivalent to DDPM up to scaling.
SDE / ODE
Stochastic / ordinary differential equation. The reverse-time SDE makes the diffusion process continuous; the related "probability-flow ODE" gives faster (deterministic) samplers.
DDIM, DPM-Solver, EDM
Fast diffusion samplers that produce a high-quality sample in 20–50 steps instead of the original 1000.
Classifier-free guidance
The trick that makes text-to-image models actually follow prompts: blend the conditional and unconditional noise predictions at sample time, amplifying the conditional signal.
Latent diffusion
Run the diffusion process not on raw pixels but on the latent of a pretrained VAE. ~64× cheaper. The architecture behind Stable Diffusion.
Stable Diffusion
The open-source latent-diffusion text-to-image model that launched in 2022 and brought generative imagery to the masses.
Flow matching
A 2023 alternative to diffusion: learn the velocity field of an ODE that transports noise to data. Simpler training, straighter sampling paths, increasingly the choice for new generative models.
Rectified flow
A specific flow-matching variant that re-straightens the learned trajectory. Used in Stable Diffusion 3 and similar.
DiT (Diffusion Transformer)
A diffusion model that uses a transformer as its denoising backbone instead of a U-Net. The architecture under Sora and modern large image/video generators.
Conditional generation
Generation given a control input (text prompt, class label, edge map). The opposite of unconditional sampling from "the data distribution."
Text encoder (CLIP, T5)
A frozen language model that turns the prompt into a vector for a text-to-image model to condition on.
Multimodal foundation model
A model that handles multiple modalities (text + images + audio + video) in a single shared backbone. GPT-4o, Claude 3.5+, Gemini 2.0+, Llama 4.
World model
A model that predicts how a scene (or game state) will evolve in time, optionally under actions. Video generators are world models by accident.
JEPA, V-JEPA
Yann LeCun's Joint-Embedding Predictive Architecture. Predicts latent features of future frames rather than pixels, on the bet that pixel-perfect prediction wastes capacity.
Mechanistic interpretability
The research program of reverse-engineering neural networks to understand what they compute inside.
Feature (in interpretability)
A direction in activation space that corresponds to a human-meaningful concept ("Golden Gate Bridge," "first person," "is this code").
Circuit
A small connected subgraph of features and weights that implements an identifiable behavior. "How the model does X."
Superposition
The phenomenon where a neural network packs many more features than it has dimensions by storing them in overlapping directions. Why neurons are usually "polysemantic" (mean several things).
Sparse autoencoder (SAE)
An autoencoder trained on a model's activations with a sparsity penalty on the hidden layer. The hidden units learn to be interpretable features. The main interpretability tool of 2023–26.
Monosemanticity
The property of corresponding to one clean concept rather than several mixed together. The goal of SAE-based interpretability.
Induction head
The earliest fully-understood attention-head circuit: implements pattern continuation. Discussed briefly in Chapter V; the cleanest worked example of a circuit.
Sora
OpenAI's headline video-generation model (February 2024). A Diffusion Transformer trained on a large video corpus; minute-long high-resolution text-to-video.
Jailbreak
A prompt that makes a model do something its post-training is supposed to refuse.
Scalable oversight
The research problem of how humans can usefully supervise model behavior on tasks the humans themselves can't fully evaluate. A central alignment-research thread.

§1Three generative paradigms, briefly

Before diffusion ate the field there were two main lines of generative modeling for continuous data.

Variational autoencoders (VAE; Kingma & Welling 2013) learn an encoder $q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})$ and decoder $p_\theta(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{z})$ jointly by maximizing the evidence lower bound. Clean theory, tractable likelihoods, decent representations — but blurry samples.

Generative adversarial networks (GAN; Goodfellow et al. 2014) train a generator and a discriminator in a min-max game. Sharper samples than VAEs, no likelihood, training is famously unstable. Dominant for image synthesis 2015–2020.

Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al. 2015; Ho, Jain & Abbeel 2020 — DDPM) overtook GANs around 2021 for unconditional and text-to-image generation, and have been the dominant paradigm for continuous data since. They are stable to train, scalable, and produce samples that have repeatedly exceeded what GANs could.

§2Diffusion: forward and reverse

A diffusion model defines two processes.

Forward: gradually add Gaussian noise to a data sample $\mathbf{x}_0$ over $T$ steps, producing $\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2, \dots, \mathbf{x}_T$ such that $\mathbf{x}_T$ is approximately pure noise:

$$ q(\mathbf{x}_t \mid \mathbf{x}_{t-1}) \;=\; \mathcal{N}\bigl(\mathbf{x}_t;\, \sqrt{1 - \beta_t}\, \mathbf{x}_{t-1},\, \beta_t \mathbf{I}\bigr). $$

The $\beta_t$ are a fixed noise schedule (typically cosine; Nichol & Dhariwal 2021). A useful closed form follows from composing $T$ Gaussian steps: letting $\bar\alpha_t = \prod_{s=1}^{t}(1 - \beta_s)$,

$$ q(\mathbf{x}_t \mid \mathbf{x}_0) \;=\; \mathcal{N}\bigl(\mathbf{x}_t;\, \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\, \mathbf{x}_0,\, (1 - \bar\alpha_t) \mathbf{I}\bigr). $$

So we can sample $\mathbf{x}_t$ at any timestep directly from $\mathbf{x}_0$ — useful for training.

Reverse: learn to invert the forward process. A neural network $\eps_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t)$ predicts the noise added to $\mathbf{x}_0$ at step $t$. The training objective collapses to a simple regression:

$$ \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol\theta) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{t,\, \mathbf{x}_0,\, \eps}\bigl[\,\norm{\eps - \eps_\theta(\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t} \mathbf{x}_0 + \sqrt{1 - \bar\alpha_t}\, \eps,\, t)}^2 \,\bigr]. $$

Where (the three diffusion equations above)

$\mathbf{x}_0$
A clean data sample (e.g. a real image), as a vector.
$\mathbf{x}_t$
The sample after $t$ steps of added noise; $\mathbf{x}_T$ is (almost) pure noise.
$t$
A scalar (integer) — the timestep / noise level, from $1$ to $T$.
$\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x};\, \mu,\, \Sigma)$
A Gaussian (normal) distribution over $\mathbf{x}$ with mean $\mu$ and covariance $\Sigma$ — the bell curve in many dimensions.
$\beta_t$
A scalar in $(0,1)$ — how much noise is injected at step $t$ (the fixed noise schedule).
$\bar\alpha_t$
A scalar — the cumulative product $\prod_{s\le t}(1-\beta_s)$: the fraction of the original signal still present at step $t$. Lets you jump straight to any noise level.
$\mathbf{I}$
The identity matrix — here it means the noise is added independently in every dimension with equal variance.
$\eps$
A noise vector drawn from a unit Gaussian $\mathcal{N}(0,\mathbf{I})$ — the actual randomness added.
$\eps_\theta(\cdot)$
The neural network — given a noisy input and the timestep, it predicts the noise $\eps$ that was added. Training regresses its guess against the true $\eps$.
$\norm{\cdot}^2$
The squared length of the error vector — plain mean-squared error between true and predicted noise.

Sample $\mathbf{x}_0$ from data, sample $t$ uniformly from $\{1, \dots, T\}$, sample $\eps$ from a unit Gaussian, regress. To generate, start from $\mathbf{x}_T \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I})$ and iteratively denoise using $\eps_\theta$ for $T$ steps, ending at $\mathbf{x}_0$. The denoising network is typically a U-Net (Ronneberger et al. 2015) for images, or a transformer (DiT; Peebles & Xie 2023) for larger models.

§3The score-matching and SDE view

Song & Ermon (2019) and Song et al. (2021) gave the cleanest formulation: the model learns the score $\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} \log p_t(\mathbf{x})$ — the gradient of log-density at noise level $t$, a vector that at every point indicates the direction of higher data probability. The reverse process is then a stochastic differential equation

$$ \dd \mathbf{x} \;=\; \bigl[\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}, t) - g(t)^2 \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} \log p_t(\mathbf{x})\bigr] \dd t + g(t)\, \dd \bar{\mathbf{w}}, $$

Where

$\dd\mathbf{x}$
An infinitesimal change in the sample over an infinitesimal time $\dd t$ — the equation says how $\mathbf{x}$ evolves moment to moment.
$\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}, t)$
The drift term — the deterministic pull (set by the forward process being reversed).
$g(t)$
A scalar — the diffusion coefficient, controlling how much randomness enters at time $t$.
$\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} \log p_t(\mathbf{x})$
The score (a vector) — points toward higher-probability regions. Following it is what turns noise back into data; the network supplies it.
$\dd\bar{\mathbf{w}}$
A step of Brownian motion (random Gaussian wiggle) running backward in time — the stochastic part.

which moves $\mathbf{x}_T$ noise back to $\mathbf{x}_0$ data along the score field. The DDPM loss above is one specific discretization. The SDE view also exposes a deterministic probability-flow ODE with the same marginals, giving samplers (DDIM, DPM-Solver, EDM) that produce samples in 20–50 steps rather than 1000.

§4Conditional generation and classifier-free guidance

For text-to-image generation, the model is conditioned on a text embedding $\mathbf{c}$ (typically from a frozen CLIP or T5 encoder). The training objective becomes

$$ \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol\theta) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{t,\, \mathbf{x}_0,\, \mathbf{c},\, \eps}\bigl[\,\norm{\eps - \eps_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t,\, t,\, \mathbf{c})}^2 \,\bigr]. $$

Classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans 2022) is the trick that made text-to-image models actually follow prompts. Train with a fraction of conditioning dropped to $\emptyset$; at sample time, take a weighted combination of conditional and unconditional predictions:

$$ \tilde\eps_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t, \mathbf{c}) \;=\; (1 + w)\, \eps_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t, \mathbf{c}) - w\, \eps_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t, \emptyset). $$

Where (both equations above)

$\mathbf{c}$
The conditioning — a vector encoding the text prompt (from a frozen CLIP or T5 text encoder). The model's noise prediction now depends on it.
$\emptyset$
The "null" / empty condition — what you feed when there's no prompt. The model is trained on both, dropping $\mathbf{c}$ a fraction of the time.
$\eps_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t, \mathbf{c})$ vs. $\eps_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t, \emptyset)$
The conditional and unconditional noise predictions. Their difference is the direction the prompt pulls the image.
$w$
A scalar — the guidance strength ($\approx 5$–$8$). Larger $w$ amplifies prompt adherence at the cost of diversity; $w=0$ recovers plain conditional sampling.
$\tilde\eps_\theta$
The guided noise prediction actually used during sampling.

With $w > 0$ the sampler is pushed in the direction the conditioning shifts, amplifying prompt adherence at the cost of some sample diversity. $w$ around 5–8 is standard for Stable Diffusion-class models.

Latent diffusion (Rombach et al. 2022, the architecture under Stable Diffusion) runs the diffusion process not on raw pixels but on the latent of a pretrained VAE — about 8× smaller per side, $64\times$ less compute. Every modern text-to-image and text-to-video system uses a latent space of some kind.

§5Flow matching: the simpler successor

Diffusion's noise schedule is fiddly and its training objective is one specific discretization of a more general principle. Flow matching (Lipman et al. 2023) reframes the problem: instead of learning to denoise, learn the velocity field $\mathbf{v}_\theta(\mathbf{x}, t)$ of a continuous-time ODE that transports the noise distribution to the data distribution. Training is a regression to a target velocity (e.g. straight-line interpolation between paired noise and data):

$$ \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol\theta) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{t,\, \mathbf{x}_0,\, \mathbf{x}_1}\bigl[\,\norm{(\mathbf{x}_1 - \mathbf{x}_0) - \mathbf{v}_\theta(t \mathbf{x}_1 + (1-t)\mathbf{x}_0,\, t)}^2 \,\bigr]. $$

Where

$\mathbf{x}_0$
A noise sample (here the start of the path), as a vector.
$\mathbf{x}_1$
A data sample (the end of the path). Flow matching learns to travel from $\mathbf{x}_0$ to $\mathbf{x}_1$.
$t \mathbf{x}_1 + (1-t)\mathbf{x}_0$
A point on the straight line between noise and data, at fraction $t \in [0,1]$ — where the network is asked for its velocity.
$\mathbf{x}_1 - \mathbf{x}_0$
The target velocity: the constant direction that walks straight from noise to data.
$\mathbf{v}_\theta(\cdot)$
The network's predicted velocity field. Training regresses it onto the target velocity — no noise schedule, no $\bar\alpha_t$.
$t$
A scalar in $[0,1]$ — position along the path (continuous time, not discrete steps).

Simpler than DDPM (no noise schedule, no $\bar\alpha_t$ algebra), trains stably, often gives straighter sampling paths so you need fewer integration steps. Flow matching and its variants (Rectified Flow, Optimal Transport flow) are the technique behind several 2024–25 frontier image and video models (Stable Diffusion 3, Flux, and Sora-class systems by inference).

§6Multimodal models

The 2024–26 frontier is dominated by models that handle multiple modalities natively. The basic recipe:

  • A modality-specific tokenizer turns each input (text, image patches, audio frames, video patches) into a sequence of tokens or token-like embeddings.
  • A shared transformer backbone processes the unified sequence with self-attention across all modalities.
  • Modality-specific decoders generate output text, images (via diffusion or autoregressive token prediction), or audio.

GPT-4o, Claude 3.5+, Gemini 2.0+, Llama 4 (April 2025) are all native multimodal in this sense — the vision encoder feeds into the same transformer that handles text. Image generation increasingly happens inside the model rather than via a separate diffusion call; the line between "LLM" and "image model" has gotten blurry.

Sora (OpenAI, Brooks & Peebles et al. 2024) was the headline video model: a Diffusion Transformer trained on a very large video corpus, producing minute-long high-resolution video from text prompts. Subsequent systems (Veo 2/3 from Google, Kling, Runway Gen-3, Pika 2, Hailuo) made video generation a competitive product space by 2025.

§7World models

A world model is a learned simulator: given a current state (an image, a video clip, a game frame), predict what comes next, possibly under a control input. Video generation models are world models in disguise — to predict the next video frame they must learn the physics, persistence, and interactions of the depicted scene.

Two more pointed research lines. Yann LeCun's JEPA architecture predicts latent features of future frames rather than pixels — the bet being that pixel-perfect prediction wastes capacity on irrelevant detail. V-JEPA (Bardes et al. 2024) demonstrated this empirically on video. Separately, OpenAI's Sora paper frames video generation explicitly as "world simulation" and ties it to potential downstream use in robotics and planning. The empirical question — can video generators learn enough of physics to be useful for non-video downstream tasks — is unsettled but increasingly serious.

§8Mechanistic interpretability

If models are increasingly the systems we deploy, understanding what they actually do inside is a research goal in its own right. Mechanistic interpretability (a term the Anthropic interpretability group has done the most to define) studies neural networks as artifacts to be reverse-engineered.

The field has produced a few generalizable concepts.

  • Features and circuits. The unit of analysis is not a neuron but a feature — a direction in activation space corresponding to some concept — and a circuit, a connected subgraph of features and weights implementing some behavior. Olah et al.'s pre-transformer Circuits work and the Anthropic transformer-circuits agenda (Elhage et al. 2021 onward) made this concrete.
  • Superposition. Networks pack many more features than they have dimensions (Elhage et al. 2022, "Toy Models of Superposition"); a feature direction can be cleanly recovered with a sparse decomposition.
  • Sparse autoencoders (SAEs; Cunningham et al. 2023; Bricken et al. 2023) train an autoencoder with a sparse hidden layer on a network's activations; the learned dictionary atoms are interpretable feature directions. Anthropic's "Scaling Monosemanticity" (Templeton et al. 2024) applied this to Claude 3 Sonnet, finding millions of human-meaningful features ranging from "the Golden Gate Bridge" to "deception."
  • Induction heads (Olsson et al. 2022) and similar discovered circuits gave the first detailed account of how a specific transformer behavior — in-context learning — is implemented mechanically.

This is the closest thing to a science of what's inside a model. It is still small relative to the scale of the systems being studied; SAEs on a frontier model produce millions of features without exhausting them. Whether this scales to "we can verify what a model will do before deploying it" is the bet that funds the field.

§9The open frontier, in one paragraph each

Where does scaling end? Pretraining loss continues to drop with compute; pretraining-quality data is approaching exhaustion (high-quality text in the trillions of tokens); reasoning-style post-training has opened a new axis. Whether the next 10× of compute brings the next qualitative jump or returns diminishing returns is the most-watched question in the field. Current public bets are split.

Alignment at the frontier. RLHF and DPO handle current models. The harder problem is alignment of more capable systems that can plan over long horizons, deceive evaluators if it serves their objective, or exploit reward misspecification creatively. Scalable oversight, debate, weak-to-strong generalization, and the Anthropic/OpenAI/DeepMind alignment teams' programs all attack this.

Robustness and safety. Jailbreaks, prompt injection, model evaluations that don't measure what they claim, capability elicitation that misses what a model can do — the gap between "the model has not exhibited capability X in evaluation" and "the model cannot do X" remains uncomfortably wide.

Energy and infrastructure. The leading systems consume gigawatts; the constraints on further scaling are increasingly physical (power, fab capacity, datacenter siting) rather than algorithmic.

The unsupervised problem. Most of the value the field has produced has come from supervised or self-supervised pretraining + RL on verifiable rewards. Whether models can self-improve on hard problems without external scaffolding — and what theory could underwrite such self-improvement — remains the deepest open question.

The book ends here. Across ten chapters we built one frame — data + function class + loss, optimized by gradient descent — and watched it absorb every modality and method the modern field has produced. The frame survives. What's inside is changing faster than any book about it can; the goal was to leave you ready to read what comes next.

Interactive · Diffusion on a 2D mixture

play forward / reverse

A four-mode 2D Gaussian mixture stands in for "data." Press noise to run the forward process: samples diffuse outward via $\mathbf{x}_{t+1} = \sqrt{1 - \beta}\, \mathbf{x}_t + \sqrt{\beta}\, \eps$ until they're indistinguishable from $\mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I})$. Press denoise to run the reverse process, using the true score $\nabla \log p_t(\mathbf{x})$ analytically derivable from the noised mixture density. The clouds collapse back into the four modes — what a trained $\eps_\theta$ approximates with a neural net.
