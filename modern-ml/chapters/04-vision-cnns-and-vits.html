Chapter IV

Vision: Convolutions, ResNets, and Vision Transformers

An image is a long list of numbers — but with a structure (translation equivariance, locality, hierarchy) that almost begs for a particular architectural prior. Two decades of vision research is the story of building it in, and then a third decade of partially abandoning it.

Before we start

An image is a grid of numbers (pixels) with two structural properties: locality (nearby pixels are related) and translation equivariance (the same object looks the same wherever it appears in the frame). The convolutional neural network (CNN) bakes these properties in by sliding a small filter over the image, reusing the same weights at every position. That's most of what made deep learning practical for vision in the 2010s.

The 2020s twist: the vision transformer (ViT) throws that structure away. It chops the image into patches, treats each patch as a token, and feeds the whole sequence to a standard transformer (Chapter V). Given enough data, this generic architecture beats hand-crafted CNNs — the "right" inductive bias can be learned. The chapter walks the historical arc (LeNet → AlexNet → ResNet → ViT) and lands on the modern image-foundation-model landscape (CLIP, MAE, DINOv2).

Terms you'll meet

Pixel
A single dot in an image. For a color image, three numbers (red, green, blue intensity).
Channel
One slice of the image at a given color (RGB → 3 channels) or, inside a CNN, one of the learned feature maps a layer produces.
Feature map
The output of one convolution layer — a stack of channels, each one a learned "where does this pattern appear" image.
Convolution
The operation of sliding a small filter across an image and computing a weighted sum at every position.
Kernel / filter
The small grid of weights (typically $3 \times 3$) that the convolution slides. Same weights reused at every spatial position.
Parameter sharing
Using the same weights at many spatial positions instead of learning separate weights everywhere. Cuts parameter count dramatically and bakes in translation symmetry.
Translation equivariance
Shift the input, the output shifts by the same amount. The mathematical property convolutions automatically have.
Stride
The step size of the sliding filter. Stride 2 halves the spatial resolution each layer.
Padding
Adding extra pixels (usually zeros) at the edges so the convolution can be applied near borders.
Pooling
A non-learnable downsampling step. Max-pooling keeps the largest value in each local window; average-pooling averages them.
Receptive field
The patch of the original image that influences a given unit deep in the network. Grows with depth.
Inductive bias
The built-in assumptions of an architecture about what kinds of functions are likely. CNNs assume locality and translation symmetry; ViTs assume almost nothing.
LeNet, AlexNet, VGG, GoogLeNet, ResNet
The historical lineage of CNN architectures, in order: 1998, 2012, 2014, 2014, 2016. Each was a step up in depth or efficiency.
Residual / skip connection
An "identity shortcut" that adds a layer's input back to its output: $\mathbf{h}_{\ell+1} = \mathbf{h}_\ell + F(\mathbf{h}_\ell)$. The trick that let networks train past a dozen layers.
ImageNet
A 1.2M-image labeled dataset of 1000 object categories. The benchmark whose 2012 leaderboard tipped over started the modern deep-learning era.
Vision Transformer (ViT)
An image model built from transformer blocks instead of convolutions. Cuts the image into patches and treats them as a sequence of tokens.
Patch
A small square of pixels (e.g. $16 \times 16$) that a ViT treats as one token.
Foundation model
A large model pretrained on broad data, designed to be reused for many downstream tasks. CLIP, DINOv2, and Llama are foundation models.
CLIP
A 2021 OpenAI model that learns an image and a text encoder jointly so matching image–caption pairs end up nearby in a shared embedding space.
Contrastive learning / InfoNCE
Training a model to pull "matching" pairs together in embedding space and push "non-matching" pairs apart. The loss CLIP uses.
MAE (Masked Autoencoder)
Hide most patches of an image, train a network to reconstruct them. The vision analogue of BERT's masked-word task.
DINOv2
A self-supervised image model (2023) whose features work zero-shot on classification, segmentation, depth, and retrieval. Currently the best general-purpose pretrained vision backbone.
Multimodal model
A model that handles more than one modality (image + text, video + audio, …) in a shared backbone.
ConvNeXt
A 2022 CNN architecture that absorbed tricks from transformers; the strongest pure-CNN response to ViT.

§1The problem with applying an MLP to a picture

A $224 \times 224 \times 3$ RGB image is 150,528 numbers. A fully-connected first layer with 1024 hidden units already costs $\sim$150 million weights — most of them learning the same simple feature ten thousand times over at different spatial positions. Worse, the model has no built-in notion that a cat at the top-left of the frame is the same cat as the same image shifted by ten pixels.

The right inductive bias for natural images is translation equivariance: shift the input, get the same output, shifted. Convolution gives this for free, with parameter sharing as a bonus.

§2Convolution as equivariant linear map

A 2D convolution layer applies the same small filter $\mathbf{K} \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times k}$ at every spatial position of the input feature map $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times C_{\text{in}}}$:

$$ (\mathbf{K} * \mathbf{x})_{h, w, c'} \;=\; \sum_{c=1}^{C_{\text{in}}} \sum_{i, j} K_{i, j, c, c'}\, x_{h + i,\, w + j,\, c}. $$

Where

$\mathbf{x}$
The input feature map — a 3D array of shape $H \times W \times C_{\text{in}}$ (height, width, channels).
$*$
The convolution operator: slide the filter to every position and take a weighted sum. (Strictly a cross-correlation, but everyone calls it convolution.)
$\mathbf{K} \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times k \times C_{\text{in}} \times C_{\text{out}}}$
The kernel — $C_{\text{out}}$ independent filters, each of which scans the input.
$h, w$
Output spatial coordinates. The same filter weights are applied at every $(h, w)$ — this is the parameter sharing.
$c, c'$
Input and output channel indices. RGB inputs have $C_{\text{in}} = 3$.
$i, j$
Indices into the local window. For a typical $3 \times 3$ kernel, $i, j \in \{-1, 0, 1\}$.

Convolution is translation-equivariant: $\mathbf{K} * (T_\delta \mathbf{x}) = T_\delta(\mathbf{K} * \mathbf{x})$, where $T_\delta$ is shift by $\delta$. The same filter that fires on a vertical edge in the top-left fires on a vertical edge anywhere. A $3 \times 3$ filter has $3 \times 3 \times C_{\text{in}} \times C_{\text{out}}$ parameters — independent of image size. That is the parameter-sharing dividend.

§3Pooling, strides, and the receptive field

Two further building blocks shape what a CNN can see.

Stride. Convolutions with stride $s > 1$ output every $s$-th position, halving spatial resolution per stride-2 layer. The feature map shrinks geometrically with depth while the number of channels typically grows.

Pooling. Max-pooling or average-pooling over local windows downsamples non-parametrically. Modern designs often replace pooling with strided convolution.

The receptive field of a unit deep in the network is the patch of the input image that can possibly affect its value. A stack of $L$ layers of $3 \times 3$ convs (no stride) gives a receptive field of $2L + 1$ pixels per side. Stride-2 layers expand it much faster. By the time you're a dozen layers in, every unit sees the whole image — and the features it computes have become abstract enough to talk about objects rather than edges.

§4The arc of CNN architectures, briefly

The history of vision architectures is mostly a story of fighting depth.

  • LeNet (LeCun et al. 1998) — 7 layers, MNIST. Showed end-to-end gradient-based training of a convolutional network worked.
  • AlexNet (Krizhevsky, Sutskever, Hinton 2012) — 8 layers, won ImageNet by a 10-point margin, used ReLU + GPU training + dropout. The result that started modern deep learning.
  • VGG (Simonyan & Zisserman 2014) — homogeneous stacks of $3 \times 3$ convs, 16–19 layers. The first widely used "deeper is better" architecture.
  • GoogLeNet / Inception (Szegedy et al. 2014) — parallel branches with different kernel sizes (the "Inception module"); 22 layers but parameter-efficient.
  • ResNet (He et al. 2016) — the breakthrough that unblocked depth. We will dwell on it briefly.

The pre-2016 problem: stacking more layers made performance worse, even on training data. The signal degraded through depth. The fix was the residual connection: add the input of each block back to its output,

$$ \mathbf{h}_{\ell+1} \;=\; \mathbf{h}_\ell + F(\mathbf{h}_\ell; \mathbf{W}_\ell). $$

Where

$\mathbf{h}_\ell$
The input feature map to block $\ell$ (and, added back at the end, the "skip" that lets signal bypass the block).
$F(\cdot\,; \mathbf{W}_\ell)$
The block's learned transformation — typically two or three convolutions with weights $\mathbf{W}_\ell$.
$\mathbf{h}_\ell + F(\dots)$
The addition is the whole point: the block only has to learn the correction $F$ to $\mathbf{h}_\ell$, and gradients flow straight through the $+\mathbf{h}_\ell$ path unobstructed.

The block now learns a residual $F$ — the correction to apply to $\mathbf{h}_\ell$ — rather than a full transformation. Gradients flow through the identity branch unobstructed, so deep nets train. ResNet trained 152-layer networks; descendants train arbitrarily deep. The residual stream is the single most important architectural pattern of the last decade; it returns in every chapter that follows, including in the transformer (Chapter V).

Subsequent CNN refinements (ResNeXt, EfficientNet, ConvNeXt; Liu et al. 2022) tuned width vs. depth vs. resolution and absorbed tricks from transformers. They remain competitive on ImageNet-class benchmarks. They are no longer the default for new vision systems.

§5The vision transformer

The Vision Transformer (Dosovitskiy et al. 2021) discards convolutions almost entirely. The image is cut into a grid of non-overlapping patches (typically $16 \times 16$ pixels), each patch is flattened, projected linearly to a token embedding, and a learned positional embedding is added. The resulting sequence of $\sim 200$ tokens is fed to a standard transformer encoder (Chapter V).

ViT throws away translation equivariance. It does not bake in the convolutional prior. It just has many more parameters and learns the prior — or something better than the prior — from very large datasets. The original paper's headline result: pretrained on the JFT-300M dataset, ViT beat the best CNNs on ImageNet at large scale. The right inductive bias for vision, given enough data, can be learned.

The implication generalized: across modalities, the transformer is the architecture that absorbs scale best. Vision is no exception. Modern image models — from CLIP to DINOv2 to MLLMs — are almost all ViT-based.

The convolution-vs-attention truce

The 2020s consensus is not "transformers won, CNNs lost." It's: transformers scale better with data and compute; convolutions are still excellent for small data and edge inference. Hybrids (early conv stages + attention, or attention-augmented convs) are common. The benchmark gap closed at scale; CNNs remain the right default for small mobile networks.

§6Foundation models for vision

Three influential pretraining strategies define the modern image-model landscape.

CLIP (Radford et al. 2021) trains a paired image and text encoder so that matching image–caption pairs have similar embeddings via a contrastive InfoNCE loss on 400M web-scraped image–text pairs. The result is an image encoder whose features generalize across thousands of downstream tasks and whose embedding space is aligned with natural language — you can classify images by writing the class names as English prompts.

MAE (Masked Autoencoder; He et al. 2022) is the vision analogue of BERT: mask out most patches of an image (typically 75%), train an encoder–decoder to reconstruct them. The encoder learns surprisingly strong features without any labels.

DINOv2 (Oquab et al. 2023) combines self-distillation and patch-level objectives on a curated 142M-image dataset; produces feature vectors that work zero-shot on segmentation, depth estimation, retrieval, and classification — currently the best general-purpose pretrained vision backbone.

The 2024–25 frontier is multimodal foundation models that share a transformer backbone across vision, language, audio, and video (Chapters IX, X). The vision encoder becomes one of several input modalities feeding a shared latent space.

A small input image is convolved with one of several hand-set kernels. The "Sobel-x" filter computes a horizontal-edge response; "Sobel-y" the vertical; "blur" averages a $3 \times 3$ neighborhood; "sharpen" subtracts the local average from the center. Convolutional layers in a CNN start with random kernels and learn variants of these (and then far more abstract patterns at deeper layers).
