Chapter VIII

Reasoning and Test-Time Compute

The 2024 shift: instead of making models bigger, give them more time. A model that thinks before it answers can outperform a model many times its size — and the way to train that ability turns out to be reinforcement learning on problems with checkable answers.

Before we start

For a long time, the way to make a language model smarter was to make it bigger. The 2024 shift, kicked off by OpenAI's o1 and quickly matched by DeepSeek-R1 and others, is to instead give the model more time: train it to think through problems step by step — generating long internal "reasoning traces" — before answering. A smaller model that thinks for a minute often beats a much larger model that answers in a second.

The training recipe is reinforcement learning, but with a twist: the reward signal isn't human preference judgments (as in RLHF, Chapter VII) — it's whether a verifier program says the model got the final answer right. This is called RLVR (RL with verifiable rewards). The chapter covers the recipe (RLVR, GRPO, process vs. outcome reward models), the new inference-time scaling law that parallels pretraining's, and the techniques (long chain-of-thought, best-of-N sampling, tree search) for trading more inference compute for better answers.

Terms you'll meet

Chain-of-thought (CoT)
Having a model write out its reasoning step by step before producing a final answer. Originally a prompting trick ("Let's think step by step"); now trained in by default.
Reasoning model
An LLM specifically post-trained to produce long chains of thought before answering. o1, o3, DeepSeek-R1, Claude reasoning modes, Gemini Thinking.
Test-time / inference-time compute
The compute spent when answering a query, as opposed to training compute spent once during model building.
Inference-time scaling law
The empirical observation that spending more compute at inference time (longer thoughts, more samples) keeps improving accuracy along a smooth curve, parallel to the pretraining scaling laws.
RLVR (RL with verifiable rewards)
Reinforcement learning where the reward is computed by a deterministic verifier (math grader, code test suite, formal proof checker), not a learned reward model.
Verifier
A piece of code that checks whether a candidate answer is correct. For math, an answer matcher; for code, a test suite.
Rollout / trace
One full sampled response from the model — the chain of thought plus the final answer. The unit of training data for RL.
Policy-gradient update
The RL update rule: take a step that increases the probability of high-reward rollouts and decreases the probability of low-reward ones.
PPO
The standard RL algorithm (also used in RLHF, Chapter VII). Heavy and unstable for long-sequence sparse-reward problems.
GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization)
The 2025 DeepSeek-R1 algorithm. Skips the value head; computes "advantage" by normalizing rewards within the group of $K$ samples drawn from the same prompt. Simpler than PPO, memory-efficient at long context, now the default for open reasoning training.
Advantage
Roughly, "how much better this rollout did than the average rollout from the same prompt." Used to weight the policy-gradient update.
Outcome reward model (ORM)
Scores whole traces as right/wrong based on the final answer only.
Process reward model (PRM)
Scores each intermediate step of a trace. More expensive to label, but much better training signal for math reasoning.
Self-consistency
Sample $N$ chains of thought, take the majority-vote answer. A cheap way to boost accuracy at inference time.
Majority vote
Of the $N$ sampled answers, pick whichever appeared most often.
Best-of-N
Sample $N$ candidate answers, pick the one a reward model (or verifier) scores highest.
Tree of Thoughts (ToT)
An inference-time strategy that branches the reasoning into multiple paths, expands the promising ones, and prunes dead ends. More expensive than best-of-N but allows backtracking.
MCTS (Monte-Carlo Tree Search)
A classical search algorithm that builds a tree of partial solutions, biased by random rollouts. Used in AlphaGo and some modern theorem-proving / code-generation systems.
AIME, GSM8K, HumanEval, SWE-bench
Standard benchmarks. AIME = competition math; GSM8K = grade-school word problems; HumanEval = single-function code; SWE-bench = real-world GitHub issues. Used to measure reasoning-model progress.
o-series
OpenAI's reasoning-model line: o1 (2024), o3 (2025). The first widely-released models trained explicitly to think before answering.
DeepSeek-R1
The January 2025 open-weights reasoning model whose tech report popularized GRPO and showed that pure RLVR on a base model can elicit complex reasoning behavior.
Emergence (revisited)
The phenomenon, from Chapter VI, where capabilities appear at scale rather than scaling smoothly. Reasoning behavior emerging from RL training is one of the cleaner examples.

§1Chain-of-thought, the original observation

Take a large language model. Give it an arithmetic word problem. Direct it to "give the final answer" and accuracy is bad. Prepend "Let's think step by step" or include a few worked examples, and accuracy rises dramatically. This is chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting, characterized by Wei et al. (2022).

The intuition is that complex problems decompose into intermediate steps, and a model has more capacity to compute a correct step-by-step trace than a single-shot answer. Each generated token is an additional forward pass through the model — that is the computational lever. Spend more tokens, do more computation, get a better answer.

For a 2022-era model, CoT was a prompting trick. It made some problems solvable that weren't before; on others it didn't help. The shift in 2024 was to train models that produce a CoT by default, are rewarded for arriving at correct final answers via long traces, and explicitly extend the trace until they're confident.

§2RL with verifiable rewards (RLVR)

RLHF (Chapter VII) trains policies against a learned reward model fit to human preferences. Reasoning models lean on a different signal: verifiable rewards. For some problem classes — math problems with known answers, code that must pass test cases, logic puzzles, formal proofs — correctness is a deterministic function we can write down. There is no need to model human preferences; we can score every rollout exactly.

The training loop, schematically:

  1. Sample a problem $\mathbf{x}$ and generate $K$ candidate solution traces $\mathbf{y}^{(1)}, \dots, \mathbf{y}^{(K)}$ from the current policy.
  2. Score each by a verifier: $r^{(k)} = \mathbb{1}[\text{trace } k \text{ produces a correct answer}]$.
  3. Apply a policy-gradient update toward correct traces, away from incorrect ones.

The catch is the third step. PPO works but is heavy and unstable for very long sequences with sparse rewards. The DeepSeek-R1 paper (DeepSeek-AI 2025) made GRPO — Group Relative Policy Optimization — the de-facto algorithm: drop the value head, compute advantages by normalizing rewards within the group of K samples for the same prompt, apply a standard clipped surrogate. Simpler than PPO, dramatically more memory-efficient at long context, and competitive in quality.

The most striking result of the R1 line of work is that RLVR on a base model — with no human preference data and minimal SFT — produces emergent multi-step reasoning behavior: the model spontaneously learns to backtrack, double-check, try alternative approaches, and reach the right answer. Training is the answer; no prompting trick is needed once the model has internalized the format.

§3The o-series and the inference-time scaling law

OpenAI's o1 (2024) was the first widely-released model trained explicitly to spend long chains of thought before answering, hidden from the user and counted as billable output tokens. Its successors (o3 family) and competitors (DeepSeek R1, Anthropic Claude reasoning modes, Gemini Thinking) all share the same shape: an LLM trained with RLVR (or a related variant) that produces extensive internal reasoning before a final response.

The empirical lesson is captured by a second scaling law, parallel to the pretraining one:

$$ \text{accuracy}(\text{problem}) \;\approx\; f(\text{model size},\; \text{training compute},\; \underbrace{\text{inference compute}}_{\text{new axis}}). $$

Snell et al. (2024) showed empirically that, on hard problems, spending compute at inference time — through long CoT, repeated sampling, or tree search — can substitute for spending compute at training time. A smaller model that thinks for a minute often beats a larger model that answers in a second. The implication for the field is large: pretraining is no longer the only place to scale.

§4Process vs. outcome reward models

Verifiable rewards are great when they exist. For most things they don't. The intermediate question is how to grade steps of a reasoning trace, not just the final answer.

An outcome reward model (ORM) labels whole traces correct/incorrect. Cheap to train (just need ground-truth answers), but rewards the right answer for the wrong reasons, and gives no signal for partially-correct work.

A process reward model (PRM) labels each step of a trace as "looks right" or "looks wrong," typically trained on human-labeled step-by-step traces. Lightman et al. (2023) — "Let's Verify Step by Step" — showed that PRMs substantially outperform ORMs for math reasoning, by giving credit at every step rather than only at the end. The cost is the step-level annotation, an expensive labeling regime.

Modern reasoning training mixes both — outcome reward where verifiable, process reward where available, and self-generated step labels (a stronger model annotating a weaker one's traces) to fill the gap.

§5Inference-time scaling, in detail

Three families of techniques exchange more inference compute for better answers.

Long chain-of-thought. Sample one very long trace. Train (with RLVR) to use the length productively. This is the o-series strategy. Scales smoothly: doubling output budget yields measurable accuracy gains.

Best-of-N / self-consistency. Sample $N$ independent traces; either majority-vote the final answer (self-consistency; Wang et al. 2022) or pick the highest-scoring one under a reward model (best-of-N). On problems with verifiable rewards, you can sample and verify, only taking samples that pass. For $N$ samples each of length $L$, total cost is $NL$ — linear in $N$, embarrassingly parallel.

Tree search. Explore branching reasoning paths; expand promising prefixes; prune dead ends. Tree of Thoughts (Yao et al. 2023) framed this for language models; MCTS-style search appears in code generation and theorem proving. Heavier per-token but can solve problems that pure best-of-N cannot, by allowing backtracking.

Accuracy as a function of inference compute is generally a power law: smooth, sublinear, no obvious knee. Doubling compute typically buys a few percent of additional accuracy on hard benchmarks — small per dollar but reliable, and the gains compound across many problems.

§6What reasoning models do well — and don't

By mid-2025, reasoning models are clearly best on:

  • Math. Competition problems (AIME, USAMO) once considered well past LLM capability are now near-saturated for the top reasoning models.
  • Code. Both single-function generation (HumanEval, MBPP) and longer-form coding agent tasks (SWE-bench).
  • Multi-step logic and planning in well-defined domains.

They are not yet a free lunch:

  • Latency: a reasoning model can take 30 seconds to 5 minutes per query. For interactive use this matters.
  • Cost: 10–100× the tokens of a non-reasoning answer.
  • Hallucinations in the reasoning trace itself: a confident wrong intermediate step can derail the rest of the trace before the model corrects.
  • Generalization out of the training distribution: reasoning models are strongest on the problem families they were RL'd on (math, code, structured puzzles), and only weakly better than base models on free-form questions where there is no checkable answer.

The synthesis from the field as of 2026 is that reasoning + RLVR has unlocked a new axis of capability that pretraining alone wasn't reaching. It is the most important architectural-class shift since RLHF in 2022. The next chapter takes a related step: hook these models up to tools and an environment, and call them agents.

Interactive · Inference-time scaling, by sampling

drag per-sample accuracy · drag log N

For a problem where a verifier can mark each sample correct or incorrect, the chance of getting at least one correct answer in $N$ independent attempts is $1 - (1 - p)^N$ where $p$ is the single-attempt accuracy. For self-consistency (majority vote over $N$ samples), the curve follows the cumulative binomial. Both are smooth power-law-like climbs in $N$ — small per-sample accuracy gets you most of the way there for a few hundred samples. This is the empirical underpinning of inference-time scaling.
