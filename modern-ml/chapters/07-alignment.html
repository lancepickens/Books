Chapter VII

From Base Model to Assistant: SFT, RLHF, DPO

A pretrained model knows how to continue text. It does not know how to be helpful, follow instructions, or refuse harmful requests. The post-training stack is what turns a next-token predictor into something a person would want to talk to.

Before we start

The output of Chapter VI is a "base model" — a transformer that's seen trillions of tokens and is great at completing text. Ask it "What is the capital of France?" and it might respond with another question instead of "Paris," because that's how the internet often continues a question. This chapter is about turning that base model into a useful assistant.

Three techniques do most of the work. SFT (supervised fine-tuning) shows the model thousands of examples of "good answer to a request." RLHF (reinforcement learning from human feedback) lets human labelers say which of two answers they prefer, fits a reward model to those preferences, then uses reinforcement learning to push the model toward higher reward. DPO (direct preference optimization) is a 2023 shortcut that achieves the same effect with a simple supervised loss — no reward model, no RL. The chapter also covers Constitutional AI / RLAIF (have a model grade other models' answers) and LoRA / QLoRA (a memory-efficient way to fine-tune very large models).

Terms you'll meet

Base model
The output of pretraining — competent at next-token continuation, not yet an assistant.
Post-training
Everything that happens to a model after pretraining: SFT, RLHF/DPO, RL on verifiable rewards.
Alignment
Umbrella term for getting a model to do what users actually want and not do what they don't. The chapter's subject; the broader term is contested.
SFT (supervised fine-tuning)
Continue training on (prompt, good response) pairs using the standard next-token loss. Cheap, fast, and the foundation of every post-training pipeline.
Instruction tuning
A flavor of SFT specifically on instruction-following examples. Teaches the model the "assistant turn" format.
Chat template
The specific text format the model expects for a multi-turn conversation (system prompt, user turn, assistant turn). Different models use different templates.
Reward model
A separate network — usually a head on top of the SFT model — that scores a (prompt, response) pair. Trained on human preference data.
Preference data
Records of "human labeler preferred response $y_w$ over response $y_l$ for prompt $x$." The raw input to reward modeling and DPO.
Bradley–Terry model
The 1952 statistical model for pairwise comparisons: the probability that $y_w$ wins is $\sigma(r(y_w) - r(y_l))$. The likelihood the reward model is fit under.
Reinforcement learning (RL)
The framing where the model is an "agent" producing actions (tokens) to maximize a long-run reward. Used here for the optimization phase of RLHF.
Policy
The model itself, viewed as "the thing that picks the next token." Trained.
Reference policy
A frozen copy of the SFT model. The trained policy is penalized for drifting too far from it.
KL divergence
A measure of how different two probability distributions are. Used to keep the trained policy close to the reference, preventing reward hacking.
RLHF
Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback. Train reward model on preferences, then optimize the policy to maximize reward minus a KL penalty.
PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization)
The RL algorithm typically used in RLHF. Takes many small clipped policy-gradient steps; standard but complex.
Value head, advantage
PPO uses a "value head" that estimates expected reward from a state; the "advantage" is how much better an action was than that estimate. Used to reduce variance in the policy-gradient update.
Reward hacking
The policy finds responses that score high under the reward model but are not actually good — exploiting the reward model's blind spots. The central failure mode of RLHF.
InstructGPT
The 2022 OpenAI paper that made RLHF the default post-training method. Showed a 1.3B-parameter RLHF model could beat a 175B base GPT-3 on user preference.
DPO (Direct Preference Optimization)
A 2023 trick that derives a closed-form objective in the policy directly from the RLHF formulation. No reward model, no RL loop, no sampling — just a supervised contrastive loss on preference pairs.
IPO, KTO, ORPO, SimPO
DPO descendants that relax various assumptions of the original formulation. All conceptually similar; differ in details.
Constitutional AI
Anthropic's 2022 method: give the model a "constitution" of principles; have it critique and revise its own answers against the principles; train preferences from the result. The labels come from the model, the principles come from humans.
RLAIF
RL from AI Feedback. Like RLHF but the preferences are judged by a (typically stronger) model instead of humans. Faster, cheaper, scales further; inherits the judge's biases.
LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation)
Freeze the base model. Insert small low-rank trainable matrices into selected weight matrices. Trainable parameter count drops 100×–1000×; fine-tuning becomes tractable on small hardware.
QLoRA
LoRA plus 4-bit quantization of the frozen base model. Fits a 65B fine-tune on a single 48GB GPU.
Quantization
Storing weights in fewer bits (4-bit, 8-bit) instead of the usual 16- or 32-bit. Trades a tiny accuracy hit for big memory savings.
PEFT (Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning)
The umbrella for LoRA, prefix tuning, prompt tuning, adapters, etc. — any fine-tuning method that updates a small fraction of the model's parameters.
Adapter
A small inserted module trained while the rest of the model stays frozen. LoRA is the most popular kind.

§1The gap a base model leaves

A model that has only seen pretraining minimizes next-token cross-entropy on web text. Ask such a model "What is the capital of France?" and the most likely continuation is not "Paris" — it is a Q&A list, or a quiz preamble, or another question. The model's distribution is the internet's distribution, not a useful-assistant's distribution.

Three post-training techniques bridge that gap. Together they are usually called alignment, though the term is contested:

  1. Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on curated instruction-following demonstrations.
  2. Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) using preference data.
  3. Direct preference optimization (DPO) and successors: SFT-shaped variants that replace RL with a closed-form objective.

The modern open-weights recipe (Llama 3, DeepSeek-V3) is roughly SFT followed by DPO or one of its descendants, optionally followed by online RL. Closed-model recipes are similar in shape with substantially more engineering around the reward model and the data.

§2Supervised fine-tuning

SFT is the same next-token objective as pretraining, run on a much smaller dataset of instruction–response demonstrations. The dataset might be 10k to several million pairs of the form

$$ (\text{prompt}, \text{good response}) \quad\to\quad \text{minimize}\;-\log p_\theta(\text{response} \mid \text{prompt}). $$

Where

$p_\theta(\text{response} \mid \text{prompt})$
A scalar in $[0,1]$ — the probability the model assigns to the whole demonstrated response, given the prompt.
$-\log(\cdot)$
The same next-token cross-entropy as pretraining (Chapter VI) — only the data has changed, from raw web text to curated instruction–response pairs.

That's it. A handful of epochs at a small learning rate ($\sim 10^{-5}$ to $10^{-6}$) suffice. The model learns the format of an assistant turn — chat templates, system prompts, refusal language, code blocks — without forgetting the pretraining knowledge it sits on top of. SFT is fast (hours, not weeks) and gives the bulk of the apparent capability lift visible to a user. It does not do the harder work of capturing nuanced preferences over similar-looking responses; that is what RLHF and DPO are for.

Modern SFT data is heavily synthetic. A common recipe: prompt a stronger model with diverse instructions, filter the responses, and finetune a smaller model on the survivors. The Alpaca and Self-Instruct lines of work (2023) demonstrated this in the open; every frontier lab is doing some version of it now.

§3Reward modeling

The next step is to capture preferences: which of two responses is better. Human labelers compare pairs of completions for the same prompt and pick a winner. The model fits a scalar reward function $r_\phi(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})$ — typically a small head on top of the SFT model — under the Bradley–Terry likelihood:

$$ \mathcal{L}_R(\phi) \;=\; -\mathbb{E}_{(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}_w, \mathbf{y}_l)} \,\log \sigma\bigl(r_\phi(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}_w) - r_\phi(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}_l)\bigr). $$

Where

$\mathbf{x}$
A prompt.
$\mathbf{y}_w,\, \mathbf{y}_l$
Two responses to that prompt: the preferred ("winning") one $\mathbf{y}_w$ and the rejected ("losing") one $\mathbf{y}_l$.
$r_\phi(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})$
A scalar — the reward model's score for response $\mathbf{y}$ to prompt $\mathbf{x}$. $\phi$ are its trainable parameters.
$r_\phi(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}_w) - r_\phi(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}_l)$
The score gap; the loss pushes it positive, so winners outscore losers.
$\sigma(\cdot)$
The sigmoid — turns the gap into a probability (this is the Bradley–Terry model of preferences).
$\mathbb{E}_{(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}_w,\mathbf{y}_l)}$
Average over all preference triples in the dataset.

The model learns to assign higher reward to responses humans prefer, calibrated by how confident the preferences are.

Reward models are notoriously fragile. They overfit to the labeler distribution and extrapolate badly once the policy starts producing responses unlike the training pairs — a phenomenon called reward hacking. Constraining how far the optimized policy can drift from the SFT model is half the engineering of RLHF.

§4RLHF with PPO

Given a reward model, treat response generation as a sequential decision problem: prompts are states, tokens are actions, the reward fires at the end of a response. The policy $\pi_\theta$ is the language model itself. Optimize

$$ \max_{\boldsymbol\theta} \;\;\mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim \mathcal{D},\, \mathbf{y} \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid \mathbf{x})}\bigl[\, r_\phi(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) \,\bigr] \;-\; \beta\, \mathrm{KL}\bigl[\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid \mathbf{x}) \,\|\, \pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot \mid \mathbf{x})\bigr]. $$

Where

$\mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}\sim\mathcal{D},\,\mathbf{y}\sim\pi_\theta}$
Average over prompts $\mathbf{x}$ from the data and responses $\mathbf{y}$ sampled from the current policy — the policy generates its own training examples.
$\pi_\theta$
The trainable policy (the LLM being optimized). Initialized from the SFT checkpoint.
$\pi_{\text{ref}}$
The frozen reference policy (a copy of the SFT model). Never updated.
$r_\phi$
The reward model from §3, also frozen during PPO.
$\mathrm{KL}[\pi_\theta \,\|\, \pi_{\text{ref}}]$
The Kullback–Leibler divergence — a non-negative scalar measuring how far the trained policy's output distribution has drifted from the reference. Zero when identical, growing as they diverge.
$\beta > 0$
A scalar — the KL penalty strength. Small $\beta$: policy can run after reward (risk: hacking). Large $\beta$: policy stays close to SFT (risk: no improvement). Typical values $0.01 \le \beta \le 0.2$.

The KL term is the regularization that keeps the policy honest — it cannot wander into linguistic regions the reward model has never seen, because doing so spends KL budget. PPO (Schulman et al. 2017) is the policy-gradient algorithm typically used to optimize this objective. It samples responses from the current policy, scores them with the reward model, computes a clipped surrogate objective that limits per-step policy change, and takes a gradient step. A value head, trained alongside, reduces variance via advantage estimation.

RLHF works. InstructGPT (Ouyang et al. 2022) was the first prominent demonstration that a 1.3B-parameter RLHF'd model could be preferred to a 175B base GPT-3 — a striking result that made RLHF the de-facto post-training method for two years. The cost: complex infrastructure (four models in memory simultaneously, sampling and scoring loops, hyperparameter sensitivity), and a reward model that is easy to hack.

§5DPO: dispense with the reward model

A 2023 derivation (Rafailov et al.) noticed that the KL-regularized RL objective above has a closed-form optimal policy:

$$ \pi^*(\mathbf{y} \mid \mathbf{x}) \;\propto\; \pi_{\text{ref}}(\mathbf{y} \mid \mathbf{x})\, \exp\!\Bigl(\tfrac{1}{\beta}\, r(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})\Bigr). $$

Where

$\pi^*$
The optimal policy — the exact solution to the RLHF objective above, derived in closed form.
$\propto$
"Is proportional to" — the right-hand side isn't normalized; divide by its sum over all $\mathbf{y}$ to make a real probability.
$\pi_{\text{ref}}(\mathbf{y}\mid\mathbf{x})$
The reference policy's probability for response $\mathbf{y}$ — the starting point the optimum tilts away from.
$\exp(r/\beta)$
An exponential reweighting: responses with high reward $r$ get boosted, sharply when $\beta$ is small, gently when $\beta$ is large.

Invert this: the implicit reward of any policy is $r(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \beta \log(\pi(\mathbf{y} \mid \mathbf{x}) / \pi_{\text{ref}}(\mathbf{y} \mid \mathbf{x}))$ plus a prompt-only term. Plug into the Bradley–Terry loss for preferences and you get an objective in the policy directly:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}}(\boldsymbol\theta) \;=\; -\mathbb{E}_{(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}_w, \mathbf{y}_l)}\,\log \sigma\!\Bigl(\beta \log \tfrac{\pi_\theta(\mathbf{y}_w \mid \mathbf{x})}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(\mathbf{y}_w \mid \mathbf{x})} - \beta \log \tfrac{\pi_\theta(\mathbf{y}_l \mid \mathbf{x})}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(\mathbf{y}_l \mid \mathbf{x})}\Bigr). $$

Where

$\log(\pi_\theta / \pi_{\text{ref}})$
Shorthand for $\log\frac{\pi_\theta(\mathbf{y}\mid\mathbf{x})}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(\mathbf{y}\mid\mathbf{x})}$: the log-ratio of how much more (or less) likely the trained policy makes a response than the reference does — this is the implicit reward DPO uses, no separate reward model needed.
$\mathbf{y}_w,\, \mathbf{y}_l$
The winning and losing responses, as in the reward-model loss. The objective raises the policy's log-ratio on winners and lowers it on losers.
$\beta$
A scalar — the same KL strength as before, now just a coefficient that scales the implicit reward.
$\sigma(\cdot)$, $\mathbb{E}$
Sigmoid and average over preference triples — identical machinery to the reward-model loss, but optimizing the policy $\boldsymbol\theta$ directly.

This is a supervised loss in the standard sense. No reward model, no sampling loop, no value head, no PPO. Just a contrastive objective on preference pairs. It runs at the cost of two forward passes per example (the trainable policy and the frozen reference) and is dramatically simpler to implement than RLHF. Quality is comparable or better on standard benchmarks for most settings; the cases where PPO-RLHF wins are typically ones with very large or curated preference datasets.

The DPO recipe became the default post-training method in the open community within a year of publication, and its variants (IPO, KTO, ORPO, SimPO) explore different ways of relaxing the Bradley–Terry assumption or the implicit-reward parameterization. The Llama 3 paper and DeepSeek-V3 report use DPO-family methods in production.

§6Constitutional AI and RLAIF

Human labels are expensive, slow, inconsistent, and don't scale to every possible behavior. RLAIF (RL from AI Feedback; Lee et al. 2023) replaces human pairwise judgments with judgments from another model — usually a stronger model evaluating responses. The reward model trains on AI preferences, and the rest of the RLHF pipeline runs unchanged.

The most elaborated version is Constitutional AI (Bai et al. 2022, Anthropic). The model is given a constitution — a short list of principles ("be helpful, be harmless, refuse requests for instructions on synthesizing nerve agents"). It generates responses, critiques its own responses against each principle, revises, and uses the resulting (original, revised) pairs to train a preference model. Then RLHF (or DPO) as usual. The handwritten input is the constitution, not the labels; the model's judgment is the labeler.

Constitutional / RLAIF methods scale to specifications human labelers couldn't cover and produce models with more consistent behavior, at the cost of inheriting any biases of the judge model. They are increasingly mixed with smaller amounts of human preference data rather than replacing it entirely.

§7Parameter-efficient fine-tuning: LoRA and friends

Updating every parameter of a 70B model — memory for parameters, gradients, optimizer state — eats 1 TB+ of GPU memory before you have even seen a training example. LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation; Hu et al. 2022) freezes the base model and inserts a low-rank update to selected weight matrices:

$$ \mathbf{W} \;\to\; \mathbf{W} + \mathbf{B}\mathbf{A}, \qquad \mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times d_{\text{in}}},\; \mathbf{B} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{out}} \times r},\; r \ll \min(d_{\text{in}}, d_{\text{out}}). $$

Where

$\mathbf{W}$
A pretrained weight matrix ($d_{\text{out}} \times d_{\text{in}}$), kept frozen.
$\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{B}$
Two small trainable matrices. Their product $\mathbf{B}\mathbf{A}$ has the same shape as $\mathbf{W}$ but is built from far fewer numbers.
$r$
A scalar (integer) — the rank, typically $8$–$64$. The "width" of the bottleneck between $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$.
$r \ll \min(d_{\text{in}}, d_{\text{out}})$
"$r$ is much smaller than" the layer's dimensions — this is why $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ together have $100$–$1000\times$ fewer parameters than $\mathbf{W}$.
$d_{\text{in}}, d_{\text{out}}$
The input and output dimensions of the original layer.

Train only $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$, typically with $r = 8$–$64$. The total trainable parameter count drops by 100–1000×, gradients and optimizer state shrink correspondingly, and the resulting adapter is a small file (megabytes) you can swap in and out per task.

QLoRA (Dettmers et al. 2023) goes further: quantize the frozen base model to 4-bit, double-quantize the quantization constants, attach LoRA adapters. The result fits a 65B-parameter fine-tune on a single 48GB GPU. QLoRA-trained models are within a fraction of a percent of full fine-tuning quality on most tasks and have made small-lab fine-tuning of frontier-sized open models tractable.

The broader family of parameter-efficient finetuning (PEFT) includes prefix tuning, prompt tuning, IA³, and adapters of various shapes. LoRA dominates production use; the others remain useful for specific cases.
