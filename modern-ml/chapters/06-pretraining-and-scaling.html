Chapter VI

Pretraining and Scaling Laws

The Cambrian explosion of capability since 2018 came from one observation, repeated: predict the next token on a lot of text with a transformer, and the model gets reliably better at almost everything. Quantifying "reliably" is what scaling laws do.

Before we start

Take a transformer. Ask it to predict the next token on trillions of words of internet text. The model gets reliably better at almost everything — translation, summarization, coding, reasoning — as a side effect of getting better at next-token prediction. That phenomenon, called pretraining, is the engine behind every modern large language model.

The scaling laws are the empirical relationships that tell you how loss decreases as you spend more compute. They also tell you how to spend a fixed compute budget — what fraction on a bigger model vs. more training data. The chapter covers: tokenization (how text becomes integers), the next-token objective, Kaplan vs. Chinchilla scaling, Mixture of Experts (a trick for scaling capacity without scaling per-token compute), and the data-curation work that has quietly become the biggest source of progress.

Terms you'll meet

Pretraining
Training a model on a large generic corpus using a self-supervised objective, before any task-specific fine-tuning. The expensive phase that produces the "base model."
Base model
The output of pretraining: a model that completes text well, but doesn't yet act like a helpful assistant. Chapter VII fixes that part.
Self-supervised learning
Inventing a supervised task from unlabeled data — e.g., "predict the next word" or "fill in a hidden patch."
Pretext task
The made-up training task in self-supervised learning. Next-token prediction is the dominant pretext task today.
Next-token prediction
Given a sequence of tokens, predict what comes next. Repeated over trillions of tokens, this is the only objective most LLMs ever see.
Masked-token prediction
Hide a fraction of tokens, predict them from the surrounding context. The BERT-style objective.
Contrastive objective
Pull related pairs together in embedding space, push unrelated pairs apart. Used in CLIP, DINO, sentence embeddings.
Autoregressive likelihood
The probability of a sequence written as a product of next-token probabilities: $p(\mathbf{x}) = \prod_t p(x_t \mid x_{
Perplexity
$e^{\text{cross-entropy}}$. Roughly, "the model's average uncertainty per token." Lower is better; the metric pretraining tries to push down.
Tokenizer
The component that converts raw text to a sequence of integers (and back). Modern tokenizers split words into sub-word chunks so even rare words are representable.
Vocabulary
The fixed set of token IDs the model knows. Typically 32k–256k entries.
BPE (Byte-Pair Encoding)
A tokenization algorithm that starts from characters and repeatedly merges the most-frequent adjacent pair. Used in GPT-2 onward.
WordPiece, SentencePiece, Unigram
Alternative subword tokenizers. WordPiece in BERT; SentencePiece + Unigram in T5 and most multilingual models.
Scaling law
An empirical formula (usually a power law) that says how pretraining loss decreases as you scale up parameter count, data, or compute.
Kaplan scaling
The original 2020 OpenAI scaling-law paper. Recommended spending most extra compute on bigger models.
Chinchilla scaling
The 2022 DeepMind correction. For a fixed compute budget, you should grow model size and training data at roughly equal rates — about 20 training tokens per parameter.
$N$, $D$, $C$
The three scaling-law variables: $N$ = parameters, $D$ = training tokens, $C$ = total compute (roughly $C \approx 6ND$).
FLOPs
Floating-point operations. The standard unit for "training compute."
Compute-optimal
The allocation of $N$ and $D$ that minimizes loss for a given budget $C$.
Dense model
A transformer where every parameter is used for every token. The default.
Mixture of Experts (MoE)
An architecture that replaces each MLP with several parallel "experts" and a router that picks a small number per token. Total parameters go up; per-token compute stays roughly the same.
Expert
One of the parallel MLPs inside an MoE block.
Router
The tiny learned network that decides, per token, which expert(s) to send it to.
Top-$k$ routing
Each token uses the $k$ experts with the highest router scores (typically $k=1$ or $2$).
Emergent ability
A capability that appears suddenly at a particular model scale rather than improving smoothly. Real or measurement artifact (depending on the metric) is contested.
Common Crawl, FineWeb
Public web-scrape corpora used for pretraining. FineWeb is the modern open recipe for cleaning Common Crawl into usable training data.
Data mixing
Choosing the proportions of different data sources (code, books, math, conversation, multilingual) in the training mix. One of the most heavily-tuned and least-published hyperparameters at the frontier.
Distillation
Training a smaller "student" model to mimic the outputs of a larger "teacher" model. A common way to ship cheap inference-time models.
Synthetic data
Training data generated by another model rather than scraped from humans. Increasingly the dominant ingredient in frontier post-training and reasoning training.
Llama 3 / Llama 4 / DeepSeek-V3
Major open-weights frontier releases (2024–25) whose technical reports are among the most informative public sources on modern pretraining.
State-space model
An alternative-to-transformers architecture (Mamba, RWKV) that's linear in sequence length. Active research area; not dominant yet.

§1Self-supervised learning, briefly

Supervised learning needs labels — expensive, slow, often biased. Unsupervised learning of generic structure rarely produced features competitive with supervised baselines. The 2018 unlock was self-supervised learning: invent a supervised task from the data itself. Common pretext tasks:

  • Next-token prediction. Given tokens $1, \dots, t-1$, predict token $t$. Used by GPT-family models — and, by 2024, by almost every leading LLM.
  • Masked-token prediction. Hide 15% of tokens, predict them from the surrounding context. Used by BERT-family encoders.
  • Contrastive objectives. Learn an embedding where matched pairs (image–caption, two views of an image) are close and mismatched pairs are far. CLIP, DINO, Sentence-BERT.

The win is twofold. There is unlimited data (the web is 60+ trillion tokens of text). And the learned representations transfer to downstream tasks far better than features learned with explicit supervision, especially at scale. Why is unclear — there is no clean theory — but the experimental evidence is overwhelming.

§2Tokenization

Neural networks process vectors, not strings. A tokenizer converts raw text to a sequence of integer IDs from a fixed vocabulary (typically 32k–256k entries). The dominant subword schemes:

  • Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE; Sennrich, Haddow & Birch 2016). Start with characters; repeatedly merge the most frequent adjacent pair to grow the vocabulary. Used by GPT-2 onward.
  • WordPiece (Devlin et al. 2019). BPE-like but uses likelihood under a language model rather than raw frequency. Used by BERT.
  • SentencePiece + Unigram (Kudo 2018; Kudo & Richardson 2018). Treats the input as a raw byte stream (no pre-tokenization on spaces) and selects a vocabulary by EM under a unigram language model. Used by T5 and many multilingual models.

Modern tokenizers operate on bytes (so any Unicode string round-trips); use 100k–200k subwords; reserve a handful of special tokens (start of sequence, end of turn, tool-call markers). Tokenization is where the quirks live: numbers split unpredictably, code indentation eats tokens, languages other than English get 2–4× more tokens per word (a real cost when context windows are billed by the token).

§3Next-token prediction as a universal objective

Given a tokenized sequence $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, \dots, x_T)$, a decoder-only transformer parameterizes the autoregressive likelihood

$$ p_\theta(\mathbf{x}) \;=\; \prod_{t=1}^{T} p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{

and is trained to minimize the per-token cross-entropy

$$ \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \;=\; -\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{

Cheap to compute (one forward pass yields $T$ targets in parallel because of the causal mask), trivial to differentiate, and — astonishingly — sufficient. The objective has no notion of facts, reasoning, code, language understanding, or any other capability. Yet a sufficiently large network trained on a sufficiently broad corpus learns all of them as side effects of predicting the next token well.

The shape of the bet is unusual. Almost no other field believes its core problem can be solved by getting better at a proxy task. ML's relationship with next-token prediction is mostly empirical — it kept working, so people kept doing more of it.

§4The scaling laws

The 2020 OpenAI scaling-laws paper (Kaplan et al.) showed that pretraining loss follows clean power laws in three quantities: the number of parameters $N$, the number of training tokens $D$, and the total compute $C$. To good approximation, loss as a function of compute follows

$$ \mathcal{L}(C) \;\approx\; \mathcal{L}_\infty + \Bigl(\frac{C_0}{C}\Bigr)^{\alpha_C}, $$

with $\alpha_C \approx 0.05$, so loss drops slowly but reliably as you spend more compute. The implication: bigger model + more data + more compute = lower loss, with predictable returns.

The Kaplan paper recommended spending most additional compute on bigger models. Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al. 2022, DeepMind) revisited the analysis with a more careful experimental design and reached the opposite conclusion: for fixed compute, optimal allocation grows $N$ and $D$ at roughly equal rates. Their rule of thumb, "Chinchilla-optimal," is roughly 20 training tokens per parameter. Compute-optimal scaling looks like

$$ N_{\text{opt}}(C) \;\propto\; C^{0.5}, \qquad D_{\text{opt}}(C) \;\propto\; C^{0.5}. $$

Most models trained between 2020 and early 2022 were undertrained — too big for the amount of data they saw. Chinchilla, with $N = 70$B parameters and $D = 1.4$T tokens, matched the much larger Gopher's performance at a fraction of the inference cost. The follow-on lesson — well understood by 2023 — is that for inference-cost-aware deployment, you want to train smaller models on more than the Chinchilla-optimal data ratio (LLaMA 1 trained 7B params on 1T tokens, ~140× the parameter count; LLaMA 3 trained on 15T tokens). Training compute pays off slowly; inference compute pays every time someone uses the model.

Emergent abilities, or apparent emergence?

Some downstream capabilities (multi-step arithmetic, instruction following) appear suddenly at a particular scale rather than scaling smoothly — Wei et al. 2022 called these emergent abilities. Schaeffer et al. 2023 argued much of the apparent emergence is an artifact of discontinuous metrics (exact-match accuracy on multi-step tasks): swap for a continuous metric and the curves smooth out. The honest synthesis: log-perplexity scales smoothly; downstream task scores measured by sharp thresholds look emergent because of the thresholds.

§5Mixture of Experts

A dense transformer activates all of its parameters for every token. A Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) transformer replaces each MLP block with $E$ parallel experts and a small router that, for each token, chooses the top-$k$ experts (typically $k = 1$ or $2$) and combines their outputs:

$$ \mathrm{MoE}(\mathbf{x}) \;=\; \sum_{e \in \text{Top-}k(\mathbf{R}(\mathbf{x}))} g_e(\mathbf{x})\, \mathrm{Expert}_e(\mathbf{x}). $$

Total parameter count goes up by a factor of $E$; active parameters per token stay roughly constant. The result: a 600B-parameter model can run at the FLOP cost of a 60B dense model, with quality closer to the dense 600B. The router is learned end-to-end with auxiliary losses that encourage balanced expert use.

The Switch Transformer (Fedus et al. 2021) popularized this for language; modern open MoE models (DeepSeek-V3 in 2024 with 671B total / 37B active per token; Mixtral; Llama 4) make the architecture the default for large open-source releases. MoE is the answer to "make models bigger without making inference quadratically more expensive."

§6The alternatives challenging dense transformers

Three threads of work on the basic architecture deserve naming.

  • State-space models (Mamba; Gu & Dao 2023). A modernized RNN with selective state updates and a parallel-scan training algorithm. Linear in sequence length where attention is quadratic. As of 2025, competitive with dense transformers at small to medium scales; the verdict at frontier scale is still open. Often appears in hybrid architectures (attention + Mamba blocks).
  • Hybrid attention/recurrent models (Jamba, RWKV variants, Striped Hyena). Interleave attention and recurrent blocks to get long-context efficiency without giving up the inductive biases of attention entirely.
  • Diffusion language models. Treat next-token prediction as iterative denoising rather than autoregressive sampling. A 2025 line of work — early but rapidly improving — that may eventually loosen the "left-to-right" bottleneck of generation.

None of these have displaced dense or MoE transformers at the frontier as of 2026, but the architectural monoculture is less complete than it was in 2023.

§7Data is the hidden variable

The architecture has stabilized; data is where most of the alpha now lives. A frontier pretraining corpus is around 15 trillion tokens (Llama 3) to substantially more for closed models, and the composition matters enormously:

  • Filtering. Crawl scale (Common Crawl is petabytes) requires aggressive deduplication, low-quality removal, language identification, and toxicity / PII filtering. FineWeb (Penedo et al. 2024) documents the modern open recipe.
  • Mixing. Code, math, books, scientific papers, conversations, and multilingual data are weighted to maximize downstream capability per token. The mix is one of the most heavily-tuned hyperparameters at the frontier — and the least published.
  • Synthetic data. Increasingly, modern training mixes include synthetic data generated by other models (often a stronger model distilling into a weaker one, or self-play / step-by-step expansions for reasoning training; see Chapter VIII).

Open frontier model releases in 2024–25 (Llama 3 then Llama 4, DeepSeek-V3, Qwen2.5, Mistral) have made the recipe partly legible, but the highest-quality data work — and the question of what to do as we exhaust high-quality public text — remains the active research frontier of pretraining.
