Chapter X

Computing pKa

Predicting a pKa from the laws of physics is among the hardest routine tasks in chemistry — not because the equations are mysterious, but because one pKa unit is only 1.36 kcal/mol, and almost nothing computes a free energy that well.

Before we start

Every pKa in this book has so far been a measured number. This final chapter asks whether we can instead predict it — hand a molecule's structure to a computer and get its acidity back. The thread that makes this possible is the one we have used all along: a pKa is just a free energy in disguise, $\pKa = \Delta G^{\circ}_{\mathrm{aq}}/(2.303RT)$. If we can compute the free energy of pulling a proton off in water, we have the pKa. The trouble is that this aqueous free energy is the small difference of several enormous energies — gas-phase bond breaking near $+340$ kcal/mol, ion solvation near $-300$ — and the answer we want lives in the last decimal place.

We build the bridge in stages. First a thermodynamic cycle that routes the deprotonation through the gas phase and back, paying solvation free energies along the way — including the proton's, a notoriously awkward number. Then the implicit-solvent models (PCM, COSMO, SMD) that estimate those solvation terms. Then the trick that rescues the whole enterprise: relative cycles that let large systematic errors cancel. We end where the field is heading — empirical and machine-learned predictors that skip the physics and learn pKa directly from data.

§1A pKa is a free energy

Chapter II gave the pKa as $-\log_{10}\Ka$. Thermodynamics gives the same constant a second face. For any equilibrium $\Delta G^{\circ} = -RT\ln K$, so applying it to the deprotonation $\mathrm{HA}\eqm\mathrm{A^-}+\mathrm{H^+}$ and converting the natural log to base ten ($\ln 10 = 2.303$) yields the master equation of this chapter:

$$ \pKa = \frac{\Delta G^{\circ}_{\mathrm{aq,deprot}}}{2.303\,RT} = \frac{\Delta G^{\circ}_{\mathrm{aq,deprot}}}{1.364\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}} \quad (298\,\mathrm{K}). $$

Where

$\Delta G^{\circ}_{\mathrm{aq,deprot}}$
Standard free energy of deprotonating $\mathrm{HA}$ in water, the quantity a calculation must deliver.
$RT$
Gas constant times temperature; at $298\,\mathrm{K}$, $RT = 0.593\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}$ ($2.479\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$).
$2.303\,RT$
The conversion factor, $1.364\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}} = 5.71\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$ at $298\,\mathrm{K}$.

The factor $1.364\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}$ per pKa unit ($5.71\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$, equivalently $1.364\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}$) is the chapter's recurring villain. Run it the other way: acetic acid's measured $\pKa = 4.76$ implies a deprotonation free energy of $4.76 \times 1.364 = 6.49\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}$ ($27.2\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$). That is a tiny, delicate number. Worse, the relationship runs both ways: an error of $1.364\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}$ in a computed $\Delta G$ — well below "chemical accuracy" of $1\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}$ for many methods — moves the predicted pKa by a full unit, a tenfold error in $\Ka$. This error amplification is why pKa prediction is hard out of all proportion to its apparent simplicity.

§2The direct thermodynamic cycle

We cannot easily compute $\Delta G^{\circ}_{\mathrm{aq}}$ in one shot, because pulling a charged proton off a molecule embedded in solvent is a brutal calculation. So we take a detour through the gas phase, where quantum chemistry is at its most reliable. The thermodynamic cycle says: lift $\mathrm{HA}$ out of water into vacuum, break it there, then drop the fragments $\mathrm{A^-}$ and $\mathrm{H^+}$ back into water. Because free energy is a state function, the aqueous leg equals the sum around the loop:

$$ \Delta G^{*}_{\mathrm{aq}} = \Delta G^{\circ}_{\mathrm{gas}} + \Delta G^{*}_{\mathrm{solv}}(\mathrm{A^-}) + \Delta G^{*}_{\mathrm{solv}}(\mathrm{H^+}) - \Delta G^{*}_{\mathrm{solv}}(\mathrm{HA}), $$

where the gas-phase deprotonation free energy is itself a difference of three electronic-structure free energies,

$$ \Delta G^{\circ}_{\mathrm{gas}} = G_{\mathrm{gas}}(\mathrm{A^-}) + G_{\mathrm{gas}}(\mathrm{H^+}) - G_{\mathrm{gas}}(\mathrm{HA}). $$

Where

$\Delta G^{*}_{\mathrm{aq}}$
Aqueous deprotonation free energy (the asterisk marks the $1\,\mathrm{M}$ solution standard state).
$\Delta G^{\circ}_{\mathrm{gas}}$
Gas-phase deprotonation free energy, the gas-phase acidity; typically $320$–$360\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}$ for neutral acids.
$\Delta G^{*}_{\mathrm{solv}}(X)$
Free energy of transferring species $X$ from gas (1 M) into solution (1 M).
$G_{\mathrm{gas}}(X)$
The gas-phase free energy of $X$ from quantum chemistry plus thermal/vibrational corrections.

The structure of the problem is now visible. The gas-phase term is large and positive (it costs a lot to tear a proton off in vacuum, with no solvent to stabilize the resulting ions). The anion solvation term is large and negative (water embraces $\mathrm{A^-}$ eagerly). The neutral $\mathrm{HA}$ is only weakly solvated. And the proton's solvation term is enormous and negative. The pKa we want is the small residue left after these giants nearly cancel — which is exactly why the calculation is so sensitive.

§3The proton's awkward numbers

The proton enters every absolute cycle, and it carries two numbers that are hard to pin down. The first is its gas-phase free energy. A bare proton has no electrons, hence no electronic energy and no vibrations; its free energy is purely translational. Its enthalpy is $H = \tfrac52 RT$ (three translational degrees of freedom plus the $PV$ term), and its entropy follows from the Sackur–Tetrode equation. At $298\,\mathrm{K}$ and $1\,\mathrm{atm}$,

$$ G_{\mathrm{gas}}(\mathrm{H^+}) = H - TS = \tfrac52 RT - TS = 1.48 - 7.76 = -6.28\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}. $$

Where

$\tfrac52 RT$
The proton's gas-phase enthalpy, $1.48\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}$ ($6.20\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$) at $298\,\mathrm{K}$.
$TS$
$7.76\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}$ ($32.5\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$), the entropy term from the Sackur–Tetrode equation at $298\,\mathrm{K}$, $1\,\mathrm{atm}$.

The second number is far larger and far more contested: the proton's aqueous solvation free energy. A bare proton plunged into water is stabilized colossally. It cannot be measured directly — single-ion solvation free energies require an extra-thermodynamic assumption — but Tissandier and co-workers extracted it cleanly from cluster-ion data, giving

$$ \Delta G^{*}_{\mathrm{solv}}(\mathrm{H^+}) \approx -265.9\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}} \quad (\approx -1112\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}). $$

Independent analyses by Camaioni and Schwerdtfeger place it between $-264$ and $-266\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}$. A spread of $2\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}$ may sound small against a number that large, but recall §1: it is $1.5$ pKa units of uncertainty baked in before the molecule of interest is even considered.

The 1 atm → 1 M standard-state correction

Gas-phase quantum-chemistry tables use a $1\,\mathrm{atm}$ reference, but aqueous equilibria use $1\,\mathrm{M}$. Compressing one mole of ideal gas from $1\,\mathrm{atm}$ (a molar volume of $24.46\,\mathrm{L}$ at $298\,\mathrm{K}$) to $1\,\mathrm{M}$ costs $\Delta G = RT\ln(24.46) = 1.89\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}$ ($7.91\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$). This $+1.89$ term must be added for each species that changes phase reference; for the proton specifically the conventions are a notorious source of bookkeeping errors. Get the standard states wrong and you can be off by several pKa units for no chemical reason at all.

§4Implicit solvation models

The solvation free energies $\Delta G^{*}_{\mathrm{solv}}(\mathrm{HA})$ and $\Delta G^{*}_{\mathrm{solv}}(\mathrm{A^-})$ are where most of the modeling effort goes. Treating every water molecule explicitly is expensive and requires extensive sampling, so the workhorse approach is implicit solvation: replace the water with a featureless dielectric continuum of dielectric constant $\varepsilon = 78.4$ for water. The solute is placed in a molecule-shaped cavity; its charge distribution polarizes the surrounding medium, which polarizes the solute back, and the self-consistent electrostatic stabilization is the dominant part of $\Delta G^{*}_{\mathrm{solv}}$.

Three families dominate. The Polarizable Continuum Model (PCM) solves the dielectric boundary problem directly on the cavity surface. The Conductor-like Screening Model (COSMO) of Klamt and Schüürmann simplifies the math by first solving the easier conductor problem ($\varepsilon \to \infty$) and then scaling by a factor $f(\varepsilon) = (\varepsilon - 1)/(\varepsilon + \tfrac12)$; its extension COSMO-RS adds a statistical-thermodynamic treatment of how molecular surface patches interact, sidestepping the single-dielectric assumption. The SMD model of Marenich, Cramer, and Truhlar uses the solute's full electron density for the electrostatics and adds parameterized atomic surface tensions to capture cavitation, dispersion, and first-shell structure — the non-electrostatic pieces a pure dielectric misses.

Schematically, all three split the solvation free energy the same way:

$$ \Delta G^{*}_{\mathrm{solv}} = \underbrace{\Delta G_{\mathrm{elec}}}_{\text{dielectric polarization}} + \underbrace{\Delta G_{\mathrm{cav}} + \Delta G_{\mathrm{disp}} + \Delta G_{\mathrm{rep}}}_{\text{non-electrostatic (surface terms)}}. $$

Why anions are the weak point

Continuum models are tuned mostly on neutral molecules and small ions. A delocalized or strongly hydrogen-bonding anion $\mathrm{A^-}$ — a carboxylate, an alkoxide — interacts with the first water shell in ways a smooth dielectric cannot capture. Errors of several $\mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}$ in $\Delta G^{*}_{\mathrm{solv}}(\mathrm{A^-})$ are common, and since the anion is the species whose solvation matters most for acidity, these errors translate almost directly into pKa errors. Hybrid cluster-continuum schemes, which add one or two explicit waters to the cavity, are the standard remedy.

§5Why absolute pKa is hard

Now assemble the error budget. The absolute cycle adds up: the gas-phase acidity (good electronic-structure methods reach $1$–$2\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}$), the neutral and anion solvation free energies (continuum models, $\sim 3$–$5\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}$ for ions), the proton solvation free energy ($\pm 2\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}$), and the standard-state corrections (exact if done carefully, a multi-pKa blunder if not). These errors are partly independent and add roughly in quadrature, but even optimistically the residual is several $\mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}$.

Translate that with §1. An error of $5\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}$ ($20.9\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$) — entirely plausible for the absolute method on an anion — becomes

$$ \Delta(\pKa) = \frac{5\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}}{1.364\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}} = 3.7\ \text{pKa units}. $$

A predicted pKa good only to $\pm 3.7$ units is nearly useless: it cannot tell a moderate acid from a weak one. The careful benchmark by Ho and Coote bears this out — the direct (absolute) method delivers mean absolute deviations above $3$ pKa units for general acids, salvageable only within narrow, well-parameterized chemical classes. The absolute cycle is correct physics applied to a quantity too small to survive the propagation of its own ingredients' errors. The fix is not better physics but better cancellation.

§6Relative cycles: making errors cancel

The systematic errors that wreck the absolute method are largely shared across similar acids: the proton's free energies, the standard-state corrections, the continuum model's bias for a given functional group. If we compute a pKa relative to a reference acid $\mathrm{HRef}$ of known pKa, those shared errors subtract out. The trick is to write the deprotonation as a proton-exchange (isodesmic) reaction in which the free proton never appears:

$$ \mathrm{HA} + \mathrm{Ref^-} \;\eqm\; \mathrm{A^-} + \mathrm{HRef}. $$

This is the difference of two deprotonations, so its free energy contains no $G_{\mathrm{gas}}(\mathrm{H^+})$ and — crucially — no $\Delta G^{*}_{\mathrm{solv}}(\mathrm{H^+})$; both cancel exactly. The pKa of the target follows by adding back the known reference:

$$ \pKa(\mathrm{HA}) = \pKa(\mathrm{HRef}) + \frac{\Delta G^{*}_{\mathrm{exch}}}{2.303\,RT}, $$

Where

$\Delta G^{*}_{\mathrm{exch}}$
Aqueous free energy of the proton-exchange reaction — a difference of two solvation-corrected gas-phase energies.
$\pKa(\mathrm{HRef})$
The experimentally known pKa of the reference acid, ideally chemically similar to $\mathrm{HA}$.

The closer $\mathrm{HRef}$ resembles $\mathrm{HA}$ — same functional group, similar charge type — the more the anion-solvation errors of $\mathrm{A^-}$ and $\mathrm{Ref^-}$ mirror each other and cancel too. With a well-chosen reference, Ho and Coote found the proton-exchange scheme reaching mean absolute deviations below $2$ pKa units, and within a homologous series often better than $1$. The price is honesty about scope: a relative method is only as good as its reference, and it quietly imports that reference's experimental pKa. It is not quite "first principles" anymore — but it works.

§7Empirical and machine-learned pKa

If physics struggles to reach the accuracy that experiment routinely achieves, perhaps we should learn from experiment directly. The oldest version of this idea is the linear free-energy relationship (LFER) of Chapter VII. Hammett's equation expresses the pKa of a substituted acid as a linear shift from a parent value:

$$ \pKa = \pKa^{0} - \rxn\,\sub. $$

Where

$\pKa^{0}$
The pKa of the unsubstituted parent acid (e.g. benzoic acid).
$\sub$
The Hammett substituent constant — positive for electron-withdrawing groups (Chapter VII).
$\rxn$
The reaction constant, the sensitivity of this particular equilibrium to substituent effects ($\rxn = 1$ by definition for benzoic acid ionization).

This is supervised learning before the term existed: a one-parameter fit per reaction series, accurate to a few tenths of a pKa unit within its series. Group-contribution methods generalize the idea, summing tabulated fragment increments to estimate the pKa of arbitrary structures — fast, but blind to anything outside the training fragments.

The modern successor learns the features rather than hand-coding them. A graph neural network (GNN) treats the molecule as a graph — atoms are nodes, bonds are edges — and passes messages between neighboring atoms so that each atom's representation absorbs its chemical environment. Trained on hundreds of thousands of measured (or high-quality computed) values, such models predict the micro-pKa of each ionizable site directly from structure. MolGpKa, a graph-convolutional model trained on over a million compounds, predicts site pKa in milliseconds with errors competitive with far slower quantum methods. The 2024 QupKake model goes hybrid, using a GNN to localize ionizable sites and then a quantum-chemistry correction for accuracy — a deliberate marriage of the two cultures of this chapter.

As of 2024–2025 the practical picture is layered. For high-throughput screening, machine-learned predictors are the default: instant, and accurate to roughly half a pKa unit on drug-like molecules near their training distribution. For novel scaffolds where data is thin, proton-exchange continuum calculations remain the trustworthy fallback. And for the cases that matter most — a single equilibrium controlling a drug's solubility or a catalyst's turnover — chemists still do what this book has done throughout: measure it. The computed number tells you where to look; the experiment tells you the truth. That tension, between the elegance of $\Delta G^{\circ} = -RT\ln K$ and the stubborn $1.364\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}$ that stands between a free energy and a pKa, is a fitting place to close.
