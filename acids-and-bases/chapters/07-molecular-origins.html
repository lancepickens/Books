Chapter VII

The Molecular Origins of Acidity

Why does one molecule have a $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$ near $0$ and another near $50$? The answer is never the proton — it is always how well the molecule can live without it.

Before we start

The previous chapters took $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$ values as given — numbers read off a table, arranged on a ladder, fed into Henderson–Hasselbalch. This chapter asks where those numbers come from. The key move is to recognize that $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$ is a free energy in disguise: the free energy required to pull the proton off. Anything that lowers that cost — anything that makes the conjugate base more comfortable bearing its new negative charge — strengthens the acid. So the question "why is this acid strong?" always becomes "why is its conjugate base stable?"

With that single principle we can walk through the whole toolkit of structural reasoning: where the acidic atom sits in the periodic table, whether electron-withdrawing groups pull charge away from the conjugate base, whether resonance can spread the charge over several atoms, what orbital the lone pair ends up in, and how the solvent dresses the resulting ion. We end with the first quantitative version of all this reasoning — the Hammett equation — which turns "electron-withdrawing" into a number you can add up.

Electronegativity

Bond dissociation

§1$\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$ is a free energy

Strip away the logarithms and a $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$ is just an energy. The dissociation $\mathrm{HA}\eqm\mathrm{A^-}+\mathrm{H^+}$ has an equilibrium constant $K_{\mathrm{a}}$, and any equilibrium constant is fixed by the standard free-energy change of its reaction, $\Delta G^{\circ}=-RT\ln K_{\mathrm{a}}$. Converting from the natural logarithm to base ten introduces a factor of $\ln 10 = 2.303$, and the result is the equation that underlies the entire chapter:

$$ \pKa = \frac{\Delta G^{\circ}_{\mathrm{deprot}}}{2.303\,RT} = \frac{\Delta G^{\circ}_{\mathrm{deprot}}}{5.71\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}} \quad (298\,\mathrm{K}). $$

Where

$\Delta G^{\circ}_{\mathrm{deprot}}$
The standard free energy to remove the proton, in $\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$. Positive for weak acids (deprotonation is uphill).
$R$
The gas constant, $8.314\,\mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$.
$T$
Absolute temperature; $298\,\mathrm{K}$ throughout this book.
$2.303\,RT$
At $298\,\mathrm{K}$ this equals $5.71\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$ ($=1.364\ \mathrm{kcal\,mol^{-1}}$): the free-energy cost of one $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$ unit.

The number to commit to memory is that each $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$ unit is worth $5.71\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$ of free energy. The fifty-unit span from a strong mineral acid to ethane is therefore about $285\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$ — a chemical bond's worth of energy separating the easiest deprotonations from the hardest. Because the proton $\mathrm{H^+}$ is the same featureless particle in every reaction, it contributes a constant offset and drops out of any comparison. What is left, and what moves $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$ around, is the difference in stability between $\mathrm{HA}$ and $\mathrm{A^-}$. Since the neutral acids differ far less than their charged conjugate bases, the rule of thumb is blunt and reliable: a strong acid is one with a stable conjugate base. Every section below is one mechanism for stabilizing $\mathrm{A^-}$.

§2Periodic trends: across and down

Start with the simplest binary acids $\mathrm{H{-}A}$ and ask how acidity tracks the position of $\mathrm{A}$ in the periodic table. Two trends pull in apparently opposite directions, and resolving them is the first real test of the conjugate-base principle.

Across a period, acidity follows electronegativity. Compare $\mathrm{CH_4}$, $\mathrm{NH_3}$, $\mathrm{H_2O}$, $\mathrm{HF}$: their $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$ values fall from roughly $50$ to $36$ to $15.7$ to $3.2$. The conjugate bases are $\mathrm{CH_3^-}$, $\mathrm{NH_2^-}$, $\mathrm{OH^-}$, $\mathrm{F^-}$, and fluorine — the most electronegative of the four — is far the happiest atom to carry the lone pair and the negative charge. More electronegative central atom, more stable anion, stronger acid.

Down a group, the trend flips: $\mathrm{HF}\;(3.2) < \mathrm{HCl}\;(\!-6) < \mathrm{HBr}\;(\!-9) < \mathrm{HI}\;(\!-10)$ in acid strength, even though fluorine is the most electronegative. Here electronegativity is overruled by size. The $\mathrm{H{-}I}$ bond is long and weak — its bond dissociation energy is far below that of $\mathrm{H{-}F}$ — and the large, polarizable iodide ion spreads its charge over a big volume, lowering its energy. Both factors favor losing the proton.

Which effect wins where

Across a period the atoms are nearly the same size, so bond strength barely changes and electronegativity decides. Down a group the atomic radius changes enormously, so the bond-strength/size term swamps the modest change in electronegativity. The lesson is not that one factor is "right" — it is that you must ask which difference is largest along the axis you are walking.

§3Inductive and field effects

Hang an electronegative group near the acidic site and it siphons electron density away through the $\sigma$-bond framework — the inductive effect — and through space — the field effect. Both stabilize the negative charge of the conjugate base, and both strengthen the acid. The cleanest demonstration is the chloroacetic acid series:

$$ \begin{array}{lcc} \text{acid} & & \pKa \\[2pt] \mathrm{CH_3COOH} & \text{(acetic)} & 4.76 \\ \mathrm{ClCH_2COOH} & \text{(chloroacetic)} & 2.86 \\ \mathrm{Cl_2CHCOOH} & \text{(dichloroacetic)} & 1.29 \\ \mathrm{Cl_3CCOOH} & \text{(trichloroacetic)} & 0.66 \end{array} $$

Each chlorine added drops the $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$, by progressively diminishing amounts, as the carboxylate is stabilized by the electron-withdrawing chlorines pulling charge away from the $\mathrm{-COO^-}$ group. Going from acetic to trichloroacetic is a $4.1$-unit swing — more than $23\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$ — produced entirely by three chlorine atoms that never touch the acidic proton.

The effect falls off steeply with distance, the fingerprint of an inductive/field interaction. In the butanoic acid isomers, a chlorine on the carbon next to the carboxyl ($\alpha$) lowers $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$ to about $2.8$; move it one carbon further ($\beta$) and the $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$ rises back to about $4.0$; another carbon out ($\gamma$) and it is near $4.5$, almost back to the unsubstituted $4.82$. Proximity is everything.

Inductive vs. field — a genuine ambiguity

Disentangling charge transmitted "through the bonds" from charge transmitted "through space" is notoriously hard; carefully designed bicyclic systems (where the through-bond path and through-space path point in different directions) suggest the field component usually dominates. For predicting trends it rarely matters which you invoke — both say the same thing: electron-withdrawing groups near the charge make stronger acids.

§4Resonance: spreading the charge

The most powerful stabilizer of a conjugate base is resonance — letting the negative charge be shared over several atoms rather than localized on one. The textbook contrast is the carboxylic acids against the alcohols. Acetic acid has $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}\,4.76$; ethanol has $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}\approx 16$. They differ by more than eleven units — over $60\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$ — almost entirely because the carboxylate anion delocalizes its charge across two equivalent oxygens:

$$ \mathrm{CH_3CO_2^-} \;\longleftrightarrow\; \mathrm{{}^-O_2CCH_3} \qquad\text{(two equivalent resonance forms)}. $$

In the alkoxide $\mathrm{CH_3CH_2O^-}$ the charge has nowhere to go — it is pinned on a single oxygen — so the parent alcohol is a far weaker acid. The same logic explains why phenol is so much more acidic than a saturated alcohol:

$$ \pKa(\text{phenol}) = 10.0, \qquad \pKa(\text{cyclohexanol}) \approx 16. $$

Phenol's conjugate base, phenoxide, delocalizes the oxygen's negative charge into the aromatic ring (onto the ortho and para carbons), a luxury the saturated cyclohexoxide cannot share. Six orders of magnitude of acidity come from a ring that the proton never visits.

Resonance beats induction

Both phenol's ring and a chlorine's electronegativity stabilize an anion, but resonance is usually the larger effect because it actually moves charge density onto new atoms, rather than merely polarizing existing bonds. When a substituent can do both — like a nitro group, which is electron-withdrawing by induction and accepts charge by resonance — its influence is especially strong. That dual capacity is exactly what the Hammett $\sigma$ of §6 quantifies in one number.

§5Hybridization: where the lone pair sits

Even with the same element and no neighboring groups, acidity depends on the hybridization of the orbital that ends up holding the lone pair. The carbon acids make the point starkly. As the carbon's hybridization changes from $sp^3$ to $sp^2$ to $sp$, its C–H acidity climbs by twenty-five orders of magnitude:

$$ \begin{array}{lccc} & \text{hybrid} & s\text{ character} & \pKa\;(\text{approx.}) \\[2pt] \mathrm{CH_3{-}CH_3}\ (\text{ethane}) & sp^3 & 25\% & \sim 50 \\ \mathrm{CH_2{=}CH_2}\ (\text{ethene}) & sp^2 & 33\% & \sim 44 \\ \mathrm{CH{\equiv}CH}\ (\text{ethyne}) & sp & 50\% & \sim 25 \end{array} $$

An $s$ orbital is spherical and held close to the nucleus; a $p$ orbital sticks out. The more $s$ character a hybrid has, the closer it holds its electrons to the positively charged nucleus, and the lower the energy of a lone pair placed in it. The conjugate base of ethyne, the acetylide carbanion $\mathrm{HC{\equiv}C^-}$, parks its lone pair in an $sp$ orbital ($50\%$ $s$) and is therefore far better stabilized than the $sp^3$ ethyl anion ($25\%$ $s$). Ethyne, with $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}\sim 25$, is acidic enough to be deprotonated by amide bases — something no ordinary alkane will tolerate.

These pKa values are bracketed estimates

The acidity of $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}\sim 50$ acids cannot be measured directly — no solvent has the basicity to deprotonate ethane to a detectable extent. Values for very weak carbon acids are estimated by extrapolation, by kinetic methods, and by anchoring to DMSO acidity scales (Bordwell's work, cited below). Treat "$\sim 50$", "$\sim 44$", and "$\sim 25$" as well-supported brackets, not as four-figure measurements. The order and the $\approx 25$-unit total span are robust; the individual numbers carry an uncertainty of a few units.

§6The Hammett equation: making "electron-withdrawing" a number

Everything so far has been qualitative — "more electronegative," "better delocalized." In 1937 Louis Hammett turned the talk into arithmetic, founding the field of linear free-energy relationships. His insight: if a substituent stabilizes a charge by a fixed free energy, then its effect on $\log K$ should be the same constant from one reaction to the next, scaled only by how charge-sensitive each reaction is. He defined two numbers — one for the substituent, one for the reaction:

$$ \log_{10}\frac{K}{K_0} = \rho\,\sigma, \qquad \log_{10}\frac{k}{k_0} = \rho\,\sigma. $$

Where

$\sigma$
The substituent constant: positive for electron-withdrawing groups, negative for electron-donating, zero for $\mathrm{H}$. A property of the group and its ring position only.
$\rho$
The reaction constant: how strongly this particular reaction responds to substituents. A property of the reaction.
$K_0,\,k_0$
The equilibrium (or rate) constant for the unsubstituted parent — substituent $=\mathrm{H}$.

The scale needs an origin and a unit. Hammett fixed both with one choice: the ionization of substituted benzoic acids in water at $25\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ defines $\rho = 1$ exactly, and the unsubstituted benzoic acid ($\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}^{0}=4.20$) is the reference $K_0$. Because $\log_{10}(K/K_0)=\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}^{0}-\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$, the equation for any substituted benzoic acid reads

$$ \pKa = \pKa^{0} - \rho\,\sigma, \qquad \pKa^{0}(\text{benzoic}) = 4.20. $$

With $\rho = 1$ for this defining reaction, a substituent's $\sigma$ is the drop it produces in $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$. The constants come from the survey of Hansch, Leo, and Taft (1991):

$$ \begin{array}{lccc} \text{substituent} & \sigma & \pKa\;(=4.20-\sigma) \\[2pt] p\text{-NH}_2 & -0.66 & 4.86 \\ p\text{-OCH}_3 & -0.27 & 4.47 \\ p\text{-CH}_3 & -0.17 & 4.37 \\ \mathrm{H} & \;\;\;0.00 & 4.20 \\ p\text{-Cl},\ p\text{-Br} & +0.23 & 3.97 \\ m\text{-Cl} & +0.37 & 3.83 \\ m\text{-NO}_2 & +0.71 & 3.49 \\ p\text{-NO}_2 & +0.78 & 3.42 \end{array} $$

Electron-withdrawing groups ($\sigma > 0$) lower $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$ — they stabilize the carboxylate, strengthening the acid. Electron-donating groups ($\sigma < 0$) raise it. Two details reward attention. First, $p$-nitro ($+0.78$) exceeds $m$-nitro ($+0.71$): from the para position the nitro group can withdraw by resonance and induction, but from the meta position only the field/inductive path is open. Second, $p$-amino ($-0.66$) is strongly donating because the nitrogen lone pair pushes electron density into the ring by resonance — the opposite of nitro. The single $\sigma$ value packages both inductive and resonance contributions of §3 and §4 into one experimentally fitted number.

The power of the relationship is that the same $\sigma$ values predict other reactions once you measure that reaction's $\rho$. A reaction with large positive $\rho$ builds up negative charge near the ring in its rate-determining step (and so is accelerated by electron-withdrawing groups); a negative $\rho$ signals positive charge developing there. In this way $\rho$ became a mechanistic probe — a way to read off, from a slope, what charge a transition state carries. §7Solvation and the gas-phase reversal

One stabilizer of the conjugate base has been lurking behind every number above: the solvent. An anion in water is wrapped in oriented water molecules, and that solvation can be worth hundreds of $\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$ — often more than every intramolecular effect combined. Because small, charge-concentrated ions are solvated far more strongly than large, diffuse ones (the Born picture: solvation energy scales inversely with ionic radius), the solvent does not merely shift acidities uniformly. It can reorder them.

The classic case is the aliphatic alcohols. In water their acidity runs

$$ \text{water:}\qquad \mathrm{CH_3OH} \;>\; \mathrm{CH_3CH_2OH} \;>\; (\mathrm{CH_3})_3\mathrm{COH}, $$

methanol the most acidic, tert-butanol the least. Strip away the solvent and the order inverts completely:

$$ \text{gas phase:}\qquad (\mathrm{CH_3})_3\mathrm{COH} \;>\; \mathrm{CH_3CH_2OH} \;>\; \mathrm{CH_3OH}. $$

Brauman and Blair reported this reversal in 1968. In the gas phase the larger, more polarizable alkyl groups of tert-butoxide spread the anion's charge over a bigger volume, lowering its energy — so the bulkier alcohol is the stronger acid. In water that intrinsic ordering is overwhelmed by solvation: the small, naked methoxide is hydrated tightly, the bulky tert-butoxide is shielded from water by its alkyl shell and hydrated poorly, and the solvation difference flips the ranking. The "electron-donating alkyl group" story once told to explain the aqueous order is, it turns out, an artifact of the solvent, not a property of the molecule.

Why the aqueous tables still work

If solvent can invert orderings, why trust aqueous $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$ tables at all? Because we use them in water, to predict aqueous behavior, where the solvation term is present in both the table and the application — it cancels. The gas-phase reversal is a warning, not a refutation: a $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$ measured in water is a property of the molecule and its solvent together. Move to DMSO (Chapter VIII) and the numbers change, sometimes dramatically, precisely because the solvation term changes.

The same caution applies to comparing chemistry "on paper." Toluene's methyl C–H ($\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}\sim 43$) and water ($15.7$) differ by twenty-seven units in water, but the gap would be different again in the gas phase or in DMSO, because each anion is solvated differently. Acidity, in the end, is never a property of a lone molecule; it is the free-energy difference between a proton-bound state and a proton-free state, both dressed by their surroundings. With the molecular origins of $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$ in hand, the next chapter leaves water entirely — to the differentiating solvents and the superacids that live beyond its leveling window.
