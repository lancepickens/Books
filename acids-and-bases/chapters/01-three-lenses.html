Chapter I

Three Lenses on Acidity

Almost every acid–base reaction is the same event told three ways: a single proton leaving one molecule and arriving on another.

Before we start

Acids and bases are among the oldest categories in chemistry — sour versus bitter, things that fizz versus things that feel slippery. Over a century the categories were sharpened three times, each definition wider than the last. This chapter introduces all three, and argues that for the chemistry of water they collapse into one simple picture: an acid is something that gives up a hydrogen ion, a base is something that takes one, and the reaction is the handoff of that ion from one to the other.

The single most important fact in the book is that water plays this game with itself. A tiny fraction of water molecules are always passing protons to their neighbors, and that background reaction fixes a constant — $K_{\mathrm{w}}$ — that ties together every acid and base dissolved in water. Get comfortable with $K_{\mathrm{w}}$ here; the remaining nine chapters lean on it constantly.

§1The protagonist is a proton

Strip the single electron from a hydrogen atom and what remains is a lone proton: a positive charge with essentially no size, about $10^5$ times smaller than any other ion. That smallness is the whole story. A bare proton cannot exist in solution — its charge density is so enormous that it buries itself instantly in the electron cloud of whatever is nearby. In water it grabs a lone pair on an oxygen and becomes hydronium, $\mathrm{H_3O^+}$.

Acid–base chemistry is the chemistry of where this proton goes. An acid is a molecule willing to let go of a proton; a base is a molecule with a lone pair willing to receive one. Every reaction in the first six chapters is some version of

$$ \mathrm{HA} + \mathrm{B} \;\eqm\; \mathrm{A^-} + \mathrm{HB^+}, $$

a proton hopping from a donor $\mathrm{HA}$ to an acceptor $\mathrm{B}$. The three historical definitions of "acid" differ only in how broadly they draw the circle around this event.

§2Arrhenius: ions in water

The first quantitative definition came from Svante Arrhenius in 1884 (elaborated into his dissociation theory by 1887). An Arrhenius acid is a substance that releases hydrogen ions in water; an Arrhenius base releases hydroxide ions. Hydrochloric acid and sodium hydroxide are the archetypes:

$$ \mathrm{HCl} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H^+} + \mathrm{Cl^-}, \qquad \mathrm{NaOH} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Na^+} + \mathrm{OH^-}. $$

Neutralization, in this view, is the combination of those two ions back into water, $\mathrm{H^+} + \mathrm{OH^-} \to \mathrm{H_2O}$. The picture is correct as far as it goes, and it earned Arrhenius a Nobel Prize. But it is shackled to water and to hydroxide. It cannot explain why ammonia, $\mathrm{NH_3}$, which contains no $\mathrm{OH}$ at all, makes a solution basic; nor why a salt like sodium acetate does the same. We need a definition that talks about the proton itself, not about water.

§3Brønsted–Lowry: donors, acceptors, conjugate pairs

In 1923 Johannes Brønsted and Thomas Lowry independently proposed the definition this book uses by default. A Brønsted acid is a proton donor; a Brønsted base is a proton acceptor. Nothing is said about water or about hydroxide, so the definition travels to any solvent and to the gas phase.

The deep idea hiding in this definition is the conjugate pair. When an acid $\mathrm{HA}$ gives up its proton, what is left, $\mathrm{A^-}$, is itself a base — it can take the proton back. The two are linked:

$$ \underbrace{\mathrm{HA}}_{\text{acid}} \;\eqm\; \underbrace{\mathrm{A^-}}_{\text{conjugate base}} + \;\mathrm{H^+}. $$

Where

$\mathrm{HA}$
A generic acid; $\mathrm{A}$ stands for whatever the proton was attached to (e.g. $\mathrm{Cl}$, $\mathrm{CH_3COO}$, $\mathrm{CN}$).
$\mathrm{A^-}$
Its conjugate base — the same fragment, now bearing the negative charge the departed proton left behind.
$\mathrm{H^+}$
The proton in transit. In water it never travels free; it is shorthand for the proton handed to a water molecule.

Because a free proton is a fiction in solution, a real Brønsted reaction always couples two conjugate pairs — one donor pair and one acceptor pair. Dissolving acetic acid in water reads

$$ \mathrm{CH_3COOH} + \mathrm{H_2O} \;\eqm\; \mathrm{CH_3COO^-} + \mathrm{H_3O^+}. $$

Here acetic acid is the acid and water is the base; on the right, acetate ($\mathrm{CH_3COO^-}$) is the conjugate base of acetic acid, and hydronium is the conjugate acid of water. A strong acid sits far to the right (it nearly all dissociates); a weak acid like acetic acid sits far to the left, only a percent or so dissociated. Quantifying exactly how far is the subject of Chapter II.

Strong acid, weak conjugate base

There is a seesaw built into the conjugate relationship: the stronger an acid, the weaker its conjugate base, and vice versa. Hydrochloric acid gives up its proton so eagerly that the chloride left behind has essentially no appetite to take it back — $\mathrm{Cl^-}$ is a vanishingly weak base. Conversely, the conjugate base of a very weak acid (say, the amide ion $\mathrm{NH_2^-}$, conjugate to ammonia) is a ferocious base. Chapter II makes this quantitative: $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}} + \mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{b}} = 14$ for a conjugate pair in water.

§4What the proton actually does in water

We write $\mathrm{H^+}$ for brevity, but the proton in water is a busy, delocalized object. It does not belong to one water molecule. Spectroscopy and simulation show it flickering between two limiting structures: the Eigen cation $\mathrm{H_9O_4^+}$ — a hydronium core hydrogen-bonded to three waters — and the Zundel cation $\mathrm{H_5O_2^+}$, a proton shared symmetrically between two waters.

This structural flicker is why acids conduct so well. Rather than a hydronium ion physically swimming across the solution, a proton can hop along a hydrogen-bonded chain — one $\mathrm{O\!-\!H}$ bond forms as another breaks — so charge moves far faster than any ion could diffuse. This relay is the Grotthuss mechanism, proposed (remarkably) in 1806. For our equilibrium bookkeeping we still write $\mathrm{H^+}$ or $\mathrm{H_3O^+}$ and treat it as a single species; just remember the symbol stands for this restless, hydrated, hopping entity.

§5Water turns on itself

Water is amphiprotic: it can donate a proton (becoming $\mathrm{OH^-}$) or accept one (becoming $\mathrm{H_3O^+}$). With nothing else around, it does both at once, one water molecule protonating another. This is the autoionization of water:

$$ 2\,\mathrm{H_2O} \;\eqm\; \mathrm{H_3O^+} + \mathrm{OH^-}. $$

Its equilibrium constant is the most important number in the book. Because pure liquid water is the solvent, its activity is taken as 1 and drops out, leaving the ion-product constant of water:

$$ \Kw \;=\; \conc{H_3O^+}\,\conc{OH^-} \;=\; 1.0\times10^{-14} \quad (25\,\degC). $$

Where

$\Kw$
The ion-product constant of water — a dimensionless number (concentrations measured relative to $1\,\mathrm{M}$). It depends only on temperature, not on what is dissolved.
$\conc{H_3O^+}$
Hydronium concentration in mol·L⁻¹. We often abbreviate it $\conc{H^+}$.
$\conc{OH^-}$
Hydroxide concentration in mol·L⁻¹.

Taking $-\log_{10}$ of both sides and writing $\pH = -\log_{10}\conc{H^+}$ and $\pOH = -\log_{10}\conc{OH^-}$ gives the relation every chemist memorizes:

$$ \pH + \pOH = \pKw = 14.00 \quad (25\,\degC). $$

In pure water the two ions are made in equal numbers, so $\conc{H^+} = \conc{OH^-} = \sqrt{\Kw} = 1.0\times10^{-7}\,\mathrm{M}$ and the pH is $7.00$. A solution is neutral when $\conc{H^+} = \conc{OH^-}$, acidic when hydronium wins, and basic when hydroxide wins.

pH 7 is not sacred

Neutrality means $\conc{H^+}=\conc{OH^-}$, which equals pH 7 only at $25\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$. Autoionization is endothermic, so warming water pushes the equilibrium right and raises $\Kw$. At $50\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$, $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{w}}\approx 13.26$, so neutral water has pH $\approx 6.63$ — still neutral, just not 7. §6Lewis: the widest circle

Also in 1923, G. N. Lewis offered the most general definition of all. A Lewis acid is an electron-pair acceptor; a Lewis base is an electron-pair donor. The base offers a lone pair; the acid has an empty orbital to receive it; together they form a new covalent bond. The textbook example involves no proton at all:

$$ \mathrm{BF_3} + \mathrm{NH_3} \;\longrightarrow\; \mathrm{F_3B{-}NH_3}, $$

where boron trifluoride (electron-deficient boron, empty orbital) accepts the nitrogen lone pair of ammonia. The Brønsted picture is a special case: a proton is just a particularly simple Lewis acid, an empty $1s$ orbital looking for a pair, and a Brønsted base is simply a Lewis base whose lone pair happens to grab a proton. The Lewis lens takes in metal ions, electron-poor carbons, and the catalytic surfaces of Chapter IX — territory the proton-centered view cannot reach. We will spend most of the book in the Brønsted world, because aqueous chemistry lives there, and return to the Lewis view in earnest in Chapter IX.

§7Which lens, and when

The three definitions are nested, not competing. Arrhenius is the narrowest (water, hydroxide); Brønsted–Lowry widens it to any proton transfer in any medium; Lewis widens it again to any electron-pair bond. For the quantitative aqueous chemistry of Chapters II–VIII — pH, titrations, buffers, speciation — the Brønsted picture is exactly the right tool, and we adopt it as the default. Keep the Lewis picture in your back pocket for when the proton is no longer the protagonist.

With water's self-reaction and $K_{\mathrm{w}}$ in hand, we have the stage and the lighting. The next chapter brings on the cast: how strong is a given acid, and how do we put a number on it?
