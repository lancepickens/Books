Chapter IX

Lewis Acids, Hardness, and Catalysis

Step back from the proton and the picture widens: an acid is anything hungry for an electron pair, and the question of which base it prefers turns out to be answerable with two numbers per partner.

Before we start

For eight chapters the protagonist was the proton. Now we return to the broadest definition from Chapter I — a Lewis acid accepts an electron pair, a Lewis base donates one — and ask the questions that the proton-centred view could not. How do we put a number on Lewis acidity when there is no $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$ to measure? Why does mercury reach for sulfur while aluminium reaches for oxygen and fluorine, even though both are "acids"? The answer is Pearson's hard–soft acid–base principle, and behind it sits a small piece of beautiful theory: two derivatives of the electronic energy that pin down how much an atom wants electrons and how stiffly it resists giving them up.

Those two derivatives — the chemical potential (minus the electronegativity) and the chemical hardness — come straight from density-functional theory, and from them we can predict the direction and amount of charge that flows when an acid meets a base. The same ideas explain catalysis: the Lewis-acid sites that crack petroleum inside zeolites, and the deliberately mismatched "frustrated" Lewis pairs that split $\mathrm{H_2}$ with no metal at all. Throughout, the hardness and electronegativity numbers are quoted in electronvolts — the natural unit for ionization energies and electron affinities — while reaction energies stay in kJ/mol as elsewhere in the book; everything is at $25\,\degC$.

§1The widest circle, revisited

Chapter I drew three nested definitions and promised to return to the widest. Here we are. A Lewis acid accepts an electron pair; a Lewis base donates one; together they form a new bond and the joined product is an adduct. Because both shared electrons originate on the base, the link is a dative bond (a coordinate bond). The proton is merely the simplest Lewis acid — an empty $1s$ orbital — so the entire Brønsted world of Chapters II–VIII is a special case sitting inside this one.

$$ \mathrm{BF_3} + \;:\!\mathrm{NH_3} \;\longrightarrow\; \mathrm{F_3B{-}NH_3}. $$

Boron in $\mathrm{BF_3}$ has only six valence electrons and an empty $2p$ orbital; the nitrogen lone pair of ammonia fills it. No proton moves at all. The same logic captures metal cations in water: a metal aquo ion such as $\mathrm{[Al(H_2O)_6]^{3+}}$ is a Lewis acid (the cation) saturated with six Lewis bases (the water oxygens). That this aquo ion is also a respectable Brønsted acid — Chapter VII's hydrolysis — is no coincidence: pulling electron density toward the metal weakens the $\mathrm{O{-}H}$ bonds, so Lewis acidity at the metal breeds Brønsted acidity at the ligand.

§2Putting a number on Lewis acidity

There is no single $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$ for a Lewis acid, because "strength" now depends on the partner. Chemists therefore use several operational scales, each fixing the base and measuring the response. Gutmann's acceptor number (AN) reads the $^{31}\mathrm{P}$ NMR shift of triethylphosphine oxide dissolved in the acid; the more the acid pulls on the phosphoryl oxygen, the larger the AN (hexane $\approx 0$, water $\approx 55$, $\mathrm{SbF_5}\approx 100$ on the normalized scale). The Childs scale does the analogous job with the $^1\mathrm{H}$ shift induced in crotonaldehyde.

For a thermodynamic measure, the fluoride ion affinity (FIA) — and its sibling the hydride ion affinity — quantify how strongly an acid $\mathrm{A}$ grabs the small, hard $\mathrm{F^-}$ in the gas phase:

$$ \mathrm{A} + \mathrm{F^-} \;\longrightarrow\; \mathrm{AF^-}, \qquad \mathrm{FIA} = -\Delta H. $$

On the FIA scale $\mathrm{SbF_5}$ is one of the strongest neutral Lewis acids known (which is why the superacids of Chapter VIII lean on it), and the antimony–fluorine adduct $\mathrm{SbF_6^-}$ is correspondingly inert. The lesson of having several scales at all is the deeper point: a Lewis acid does not have one strength but a preference. Ranking against $\mathrm{F^-}$ gives one order; ranking against a soft base like $\mathrm{R_3P}$ gives another. Explaining that reordering is exactly what the next sections do.

§3Hard and soft: Pearson's classification

In 1963 Ralph Pearson noticed that Lewis acids and bases sort cleanly into two families by the company they keep. He called them hard and soft. Hard species are small, of high charge, and barely polarizable: their bonding is dominated by electrostatics. Soft species are large, of low charge, and easily polarized: their bonding is dominated by orbital overlap, i.e. covalency.

A rough census

Hard acids: $\mathrm{H^+}$, $\mathrm{Li^+}$, $\mathrm{Na^+}$, $\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}$, $\mathrm{Al^{3+}}$, $\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}$, $\mathrm{BF_3}$. Soft acids: $\mathrm{Cu^+}$, $\mathrm{Ag^+}$, $\mathrm{Au^+}$, $\mathrm{Hg^{2+}}$, $\mathrm{Pd^{2+}}$, $\mathrm{Pt^{2+}}$. Hard bases: $\mathrm{F^-}$, $\mathrm{OH^-}$, $\mathrm{H_2O}$, $\mathrm{NH_3}$, $\mathrm{NO_3^-}$, $\mathrm{O}$-donors generally. Soft bases: $\mathrm{I^-}$, $\mathrm{CN^-}$, $\mathrm{CO}$, $\mathrm{R_3P}$, $\mathrm{R_2S}$, and $\mathrm{S}$-donors generally. Many species ($\mathrm{Fe^{2+}}$, $\mathrm{Pb^{2+}}$, $\mathrm{Br^-}$, $\mathrm{N_3^-}$) sit in between and are called borderline.

The empirical rule is disarmingly simple, and Pearson stated it as a maxim:

$$ \boxed{\;\text{hard acids prefer hard bases,}\quad\text{soft acids prefer soft bases.}\;} $$

It is not that a soft–soft bond is always the strongest absolutely — a bare proton (hard) binds almost anything fiercely. The principle is about selectivity: given a choice, each acid binds its own kind more strongly than a mismatched partner, and a matched pair is extra-stabilized beyond what charge or size alone would predict. The next sections turn "its own kind" into a measured quantity.

§4Consequences you can see

HSAB is one of those principles whose reach you only appreciate by counting what it explains. Mineralogy: hard cations ($\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}$, $\mathrm{Al^{3+}}$, $\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}$) occur as oxides, carbonates, and silicates — bound to hard $\mathrm{O}$-donors — while soft cations ($\mathrm{Cu^+}$, $\mathrm{Ag^+}$, $\mathrm{Hg^{2+}}$, $\mathrm{Pb^{2+}}$) occur as sulfide ores (chalcocite $\mathrm{Cu_2S}$, cinnabar $\mathrm{HgS}$, galena $\mathrm{PbS}$). The earth has been doing HSAB sorting for four billion years.

Solubility and stability: the soft $\mathrm{Ag^+}$ binds the soft halides $\mathrm{Br^-}$ and $\mathrm{I^-}$ far more strongly than the hard $\mathrm{F^-}$ — silver fluoride is freely soluble, silver iodide vanishingly so — exactly the reverse of the hard $\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}$, whose fluoride (fluorite) is the insoluble one. Biochemistry: the soft, thiophilic $\mathrm{Hg^{2+}}$ ignores the hard oxygen ligands that abound in a cell and seeks out the soft sulfur of cysteine thiol groups, jamming the proteins that depend on them — the molecular basis of mercury's toxicity, and the reason chelation antidotes (BAL, dimercaprol) present soft $\mathrm{S}$-donors to lure the metal away.

§5The conceptual-DFT basis: $\chi$, $\mu$, and $\eta$

Why two families? The frontier-orbital reading is that hard species have a large HOMO–LUMO gap (electrons held tight, charge-controlled bonding) and soft species a small gap (electrons easily shuffled, orbital-controlled bonding). Parr and Pearson made this exact in 1983 by reading reactivity off the curve of electronic energy $E$ versus electron number $N$. The first derivative is the chemical potential $\mu$, equal to minus the absolute electronegativity $\chi$. Approximating the curve by a finite difference through the cation, neutral, and anion gives Mulliken's formula:

$$ \chi=\frac{I+A}{2}, \qquad \mu=-\chi=-\frac{I+A}{2}=\left(\frac{\partial E}{\partial N}\right)_{v}. $$

Where

$I$
Ionization energy — energy to remove an electron from the neutral species, in eV here.
$A$
Electron affinity — energy released when the species gains an electron, in eV.
$\chi$
Absolute (Mulliken) electronegativity; large $\chi$ means a strong pull on electrons.
$\mu$
Electronic chemical potential, $=-\chi$; the slope of $E(N)$ at fixed external potential $v$ (the nuclei).

The second derivative measures how stiffly the energy curves — how hard it is to push or pull electrons. That is the chemical hardness $\eta$:

$$ \eta=\frac{I-A}{2}=\half\left(\frac{\partial^2 E}{\partial N^2}\right)_{v}, \qquad s=\frac{1}{\eta}. $$

Where

$\eta$
Chemical hardness, in eV. A wide gap between $I$ and $A$ (a wide HOMO–LUMO gap) means a large $\eta$ — a hard species.
$s$
Softness, the reciprocal $1/\eta$. (Some authors use $\sigma=1/(2\eta)$; we adopt $s=1/\eta$ throughout.)

So "hard" and "soft" stop being adjectives and become a measured number: a large $\eta$ is hard, a small $\eta$ is soft. Pearson's 1988 compilation gives, in eV, $\eta(\mathrm{Al^{3+}})\approx 45.8$, $\eta(\mathrm{Li^{+}})\approx 35.1$, $\eta(\mathrm{Mg^{2+}})\approx 32.5$, $\eta(\mathrm{Na^{+}})\approx 21.1$ for the hard acids, falling to $\eta(\mathrm{Zn^{2+}})\approx 10.8$, $\eta(\mathrm{Hg^{2+}})\approx 7.7$, $\eta(\mathrm{Ag^{+}})\approx 6.9$, $\eta(\mathrm{Cu^{+}})\approx 6.3$, $\eta(\mathrm{Au^{+}})\approx 5.7$ for the soft ones; and for the halide bases $\eta(\mathrm{F^-})\approx 7.0$ down to $\eta(\mathrm{I^-})\approx 3.7$. The proton is the limiting hard acid: it has no electrons, so $I-A$ is unbounded and $\eta(\mathrm{H^+})=\infty$.

§6Charge transfer on contact

When acid $\mathrm{A}$ and base $\mathrm{B}$ touch, electrons flow from the species of higher chemical potential (lower $\chi$, the base) to the one of lower chemical potential (higher $\chi$, the acid) until the two chemical potentials equalize — Sanderson's electronegativity-equalization idea, made quantitative. Expanding each energy to second order and equalizing $\mu$ gives the fractional charge that transfers:

$$ \Delta N=\frac{\chi_A-\chi_B}{2(\eta_A+\eta_B)}. $$

Where

$\Delta N$
The fractional number of electrons transferred from base to acid on contact (positive when the acid is the more electronegative).
$\chi_A,\chi_B$
Absolute electronegativities of acid and base; their difference is the driving force.
$\eta_A,\eta_B$
The two hardnesses; their sum in the denominator is the resistance to the flow.

Read the formula as Ohm's law for electrons: a potential difference ($\chi_A-\chi_B$) divided by a resistance (the summed hardness). Two soft partners have small $\eta$ values, so a small sum, so a large $\Delta N$ even when their electronegativities differ little — soft–soft pairings move a lot of charge, the hallmark of covalent bonding. Two hard partners barely transfer charge; they bind electrostatically instead, by point-charge attraction. This is why the matched pairings of §3 are favored, and it dovetails with the maximum-hardness principle: systems tend to arrange themselves to be as hard (as stable, large-gap) as possible, and a matched pair gets there.

An optional but useful complement is the empirical Drago–Wayland equation, which splits the adduct enthalpy into an electrostatic and a covalent term — the quantitative cousin of "hard = ionic, soft = covalent":

$$ -\Delta H = E_A E_B + C_A C_B. $$

Here $E$ measures electrostatic (hard) bonding capacity and $C$ measures covalent (soft) capacity, each acid and base carrying its own pair of parameters; a large product $C_A C_B$ flags a soft–soft match.

§7Acid catalysis: from zeolites to frustrated pairs

Lewis acidity is not a curiosity; it is how a vast slice of the chemical industry works. Solid acids. A zeolite is an aluminosilicate framework in which substituting $\mathrm{Al^{3+}}$ for $\mathrm{Si^{4+}}$ leaves a net negative charge balanced by a proton bridging the framework oxygens. That bridging $\mathrm{Si{-}OH{-}Al}$ is a strong Brønsted site — it is how zeolites crack and isomerize hydrocarbons in fluid catalytic cracking, the largest applied use of acid catalysis on earth. Dehydrate the framework, or leave a coordinatively unsaturated $\mathrm{Al}$ exposed, and you instead create a Lewis site that accepts electron density from the substrate. Most working oxide catalysts (alumina, silica–alumina, zeolites) carry both, and their interplay tunes selectivity. The HSAB ideas of §3–§6 are exactly what a chemist uses to match a hard or soft reactant to the right kind of site.

Frustrated Lewis pairs. The most striking modern twist deliberately prevents the adduct of §1 from forming. Take a very bulky Lewis acid (a fluorinated borane, $\mathrm{B(C_6F_5)_3}$) and a very bulky Lewis base (a hindered phosphine, $\mathrm{R_3P}$). Steric clash keeps them from quenching each other into a dative bond, so both remain reactive: a frustrated Lewis pair (FLP). Stephan and co-workers showed in 2006 that such a pair splits dihydrogen with no metal at all — the base grabs one proton and the acid the hydride in a heterolytic cleavage:

$$ \mathrm{R_3P} + \mathrm{H_2} + \mathrm{B(C_6F_5)_3} \;\longrightarrow\; \mathrm{R_3PH^+} + \mathrm{HB(C_6F_5)_3^-}. $$

The phosphine takes $\mathrm{H^+}$, the borane takes $\mathrm{H^-}$, and the once-inert $\mathrm{H{-}H}$ bond is broken across the frustrated pair — a feat long thought to require a transition metal. The reaction can be reversible, releasing $\mathrm{H_2}$ on warming, which turned FLPs into a platform for metal-free catalytic hydrogenation of imines, enamines, and more. It is a fitting place to end the Lewis story: the broadest definition of all, the same electron-pair handshake of Chapter I, now choreographed so precisely that two molecules which refuse to bond to each other instead tear apart the most stubborn bond in chemistry.

One chapter remains. We have measured acidity by titration, by $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$, by acceptor number and fluoride affinity, and by two derivatives of the energy. Chapter X asks how, given a structure and a computer, we can predict a $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$ before ever touching a flask.
