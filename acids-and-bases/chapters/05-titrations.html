Chapter V

Titrations and Indicators

A titration is a controlled proton transfer watched in real time: add base drop by drop, follow the pH, and the shape of the resulting curve confesses both how much acid was present and how strong it was.

Before we start

To titrate is to find out how much acid is in a sample by adding base until exactly enough has gone in to neutralize it, and reading off the volume that took. The instrument that tells you "exactly enough" is the pH: as base is added, the pH climbs, slowly at first, then through a near-vertical jump at the moment of completion, then slowly again. Plotting pH against added volume gives a titration curve, and learning to read that curve is the practical heart of acid–base chemistry.

This chapter builds those curves from first principles. We start with the easiest case — a strong acid neutralized by a strong base, pure counting of moles — then take on a weak acid, whose curve has four distinct regions and a memorable landmark: at the halfway point the pH equals the acid's $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$. Finally we collapse all the casework into a single exact equation that draws the entire curve, and we explain how indicators and Gran plots pin down the endpoint in the lab.

§1The titration experiment

The setup is simple and old. A measured volume of the analyte — say an acid of unknown concentration — sits in a flask. From a burette we deliver a titrant of known concentration, here a strong base such as $\mathrm{NaOH}$, a little at a time, stirring and recording the pH after each addition. As base is added it consumes acid; when the moles of added base exactly equal the moles of acid originally present, the reaction is complete. That volume is the equivalence point, $V_{\mathrm{eq}}$, and from it we compute the unknown:

$$ C_b\,V_{\mathrm{eq}} = C_a\,V_a \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad V_{\mathrm{eq}} = \frac{C_a\,V_a}{C_b}. $$

Where

$C_a$
Formal (total) concentration of the acid analyte, mol·L⁻¹.
$V_a$
Initial volume of analyte taken, in litres (or mL throughout, consistently).
$C_b$
Concentration of the strong-base titrant.
$V_{\mathrm{eq}}$
Volume of titrant at the equivalence point — the quantity the experiment measures.

The equivalence point is a fact of stoichiometry; it is not something an instrument reads off directly. What the lab actually observes is the endpoint: the moment a colour-changing indicator flips, or the moment a pH probe traces out its steepest rise. A good titration is one in which the endpoint and the equivalence point coincide to within the precision you need. Most of this chapter is about understanding the curve well enough to make them coincide.

§2Strong acid, strong base: pure bookkeeping

When both partners are strong, no equilibrium subtlety enters before the endpoint — the acid is fully dissociated, the base fully dissolved, and the only chemistry is $\mathrm{H^+}+\mathrm{OH^-}\to\mathrm{H_2O}$. So we simply count. After adding a volume $V_b$ of base to $V_a$ of acid, the leftover moles of $\mathrm{H^+}$ are $C_aV_a-C_bV_b$, diluted into the total volume $V_a+V_b$:

$$ \conc{H^+} = \frac{C_aV_a - C_bV_b}{V_a + V_b} \qquad (\text{before equivalence}). $$

Where

$C_aV_a$
Moles of $\mathrm{H^+}$ initially present (strong acid, fully dissociated).
$C_bV_b$
Moles of $\mathrm{OH^-}$ added so far, each of which removes one $\mathrm{H^+}$.
$V_a+V_b$
The total solution volume after the addition — the dilution denominator.

Past the equivalence point the base is in excess, and the same logic gives $\conc{OH^-}=(C_bV_b-C_aV_a)/(V_a+V_b)$, from which $\pH=\pKw-\pOH$. At equivalence itself the two cancel: all that remains is water plus the spectator salt ($\mathrm{Na^+}$, $\mathrm{Cl^-}$), neither of which touches a proton. The solution is simply neutral, so $\conc{H^+}=\conc{OH^-}=\sqrt{\Kw}$ and

$$ \pH_{\mathrm{eq}} = 7.00 \qquad (\text{strong–strong},\ 25\,\degC). $$

For $50.0\,\mathrm{mL}$ of $0.100\Msol$ $\mathrm{HCl}$ titrated with $0.100\Msol$ $\mathrm{NaOH}$, $V_{\mathrm{eq}}=50.0\,\mathrm{mL}$ and the equivalence pH is $7.00$. The characteristic feature is the jump: because $\conc{H^+}$ falls logarithmically, the last fraction of a millilitre before equivalence and the first after it sweep the pH through several units almost vertically. That near-discontinuity is what makes the endpoint sharp.

Why the jump is so steep

Right at equivalence the numerator $C_aV_a-C_bV_b$ passes through zero. A tiny excess of base — one drop, perhaps $0.05\,\mathrm{mL}$ — leaves a minute $\conc{OH^-}$, but "minute" on a logarithmic scale still means a large pH change. The curve's steepness is therefore not chemistry but arithmetic: dividing a number that crosses zero. The widget below lets you watch the jump sharpen as you raise the concentrations.

§3Weak acid, strong base: four regions

Replace the strong acid with a weak one and the curve acquires structure. A weak acid $\mathrm{HA}$ is only partly dissociated, so before any base is added the pH is set by its own equilibrium, not by its full concentration. The full titration of, say, $50.0\,\mathrm{mL}$ of $0.100\Msol$ acetic acid ($\pKa=4.76$) with $0.100\Msol$ $\mathrm{NaOH}$ moves through four regions.

1. Initial point. Only $\mathrm{HA}$ in water. From Chapter II's estimate, $\conc{H^+}\approx\sqrt{\Ka C_a}$, here $\sqrt{(1.75\times10^{-5})(0.100)}\approx1.3\times10^{-3}\Msol$, so $\pH\approx2.88$.

2. Buffer region. Each drop of base converts some $\mathrm{HA}$ into its conjugate base $\mathrm{A^-}$, so the flask now holds a mixture of both — a buffer. The pH is governed by the Henderson–Hasselbalch relation of Chapter IV:

$$ \pH = \pKa + \log_{10}\frac{\conc{A^-}}{\conc{HA}}. $$

At the half-equivalence point — exactly half the base needed for equivalence, here $25.0\,\mathrm{mL}$ — half the acid has been neutralized, so $\conc{A^-}=\conc{HA}$, the logarithm vanishes, and

$$ \pH = \pKa \qquad (\text{half-equivalence}). $$

This is the single most useful landmark on the whole curve: read the pH at the volume that is half of $V_{\mathrm{eq}}$ and you have measured the acid's $\pKa$ directly — here, $4.76$. The buffer region is also the flattest part of the curve, because here the solution resists pH change most strongly (the subject of Chapter IV).

3. Equivalence point. All the $\mathrm{HA}$ has become $\mathrm{A^-}$; the flask now holds a solution of pure acetate at the diluted concentration $0.0500\Msol$ (the original $0.100\Msol$ in $50\,\mathrm{mL}$, now spread through $100\,\mathrm{mL}$). Acetate is a weak base, so the equivalence point is basic, not neutral. Using $\Kb=\Kw/\Ka=10^{-14}/(1.75\times10^{-5})=5.7\times10^{-10}$ and the weak-base analogue of the §2 estimate,

$$ \pOH = \half\big(\pKb - \log_{10} C_{\mathrm{A^-}}\big), \qquad \pH = \pKw - \pOH. $$

Where

$\Kb$
Base constant of the conjugate base $\mathrm{A^-}$, equal to $\Kw/\Ka$ (Chapter II).
$C_{\mathrm{A^-}}$
Concentration of conjugate base at equivalence — the original acid moles divided by the total volume.
$\pOH$
$-\log_{10}\conc{OH^-}$; converted to pH through $\pH+\pOH=\pKw=14.00$.

For acetate at $0.0500\Msol$: $\pKb=14.00-4.76=9.24$, so $\pOH=\half(9.24-\log_{10}0.0500)=\half(9.24+1.30)=5.27$, giving $\pH_{\mathrm{eq}}\approx 8.72$. The equivalence point sits well above 7 — a fact that decides which indicator we may use.

4. Excess base. Beyond equivalence the added $\mathrm{OH^-}$ swamps the feeble basicity of acetate, and the pH is set by the excess strong base exactly as in §2: $\conc{OH^-}=(C_bV_b-C_aV_a)/(V_a+V_b)$. The two curves — strong-acid and weak-acid — merge here, since past equivalence neither remembers what acid it started from.

The weak-acid jump is smaller

Because the equivalence point of a weak acid is already basic and the buffer region holds the pre-equivalence pH high, the vertical jump spans fewer pH units than the strong–strong case. The weaker the acid (larger $\pKa$), the shorter and more rounded the jump, until for a very weak acid it vanishes into the rising baseline and the endpoint becomes impossible to see. Sharpness is the theme of §6.

§4One exact equation for the whole curve

The four-region story is pedagogically clear but it stitches together four approximations. There is a cleaner path. Instead of asking "what is the pH after I add this volume?" — which requires solving a polynomial at every step — we invert the question: what volume of base produces a given pH? That inversion is exact and elementary, and it draws the entire curve from a single formula.

Start from the conservation laws. Define the fraction of total acid present as conjugate base at a given $\conc{H^+}$,

$$ \alpha_{\mathrm{A^-}} = \frac{\Ka}{\Ka + \conc{H^+}}, $$

Where

$\alpha_{\mathrm{A^-}}$
Fraction of the acid that has shed its proton — runs from $0$ (all $\mathrm{HA}$) to $1$ (all $\mathrm{A^-}$). It is the speciation function of Chapter VI evaluated at the current pH.
$\conc{H^+}$
The chosen hydronium concentration, $10^{-\pH}$. We sweep this to trace the curve.

and the net excess of strong acid over strong base that the water must carry,

$$ \Delta = \conc{H^+} - \conc{OH^-} = \conc{H^+} - \frac{\Kw}{\conc{H^+}}. $$

Combining the charge balance, the mass balance on $\mathrm{A}$, and $\Kw$ — de Levie's proton condition approach — and solving for the titrant volume gives the master equation:

$$ \boxed{\,V_b = V_a\,\frac{C_a\,\alpha_{\mathrm{A^-}} - \Delta}{C_b + \Delta}\,}. $$

Where

$V_b$
Volume of strong-base titrant that produces the chosen $\conc{H^+}$.
$C_a\,\alpha_{\mathrm{A^-}}$
Concentration of conjugate base that must be created — i.e. the protons the base has removed from the acid.
$\Delta$
The free-proton excess (positive in acid, negative in base); it corrects for water's own ions and for any residual strong acidity.
$C_b+\Delta$
The denominator that maps "protons to remove" onto "volume of base", accounting for dilution by the titrant.

To draw the curve you sweep $\conc{H^+}$ from large (low pH) to small (high pH), compute $V_b$ at each step, and plot pH against $V_b$. Every region of §3 falls out of this one expression as a limit. It also handles the strong acid as a special case: a strong acid is fully dissociated at all pH, so $\alpha_{\mathrm{A^-}}=1$ everywhere and

$$ V_b = V_a\,\frac{C_a - \Delta}{C_b + \Delta} \qquad (\text{strong acid analyte}). $$

Check the obvious points. At the equivalence point all the acid is consumed and the solution is just $\mathrm{A^-}$ (or, for a strong acid, neutral salt), so $\Delta\to0$ and $C_a\alpha_{\mathrm{A^-}}\to C_a$, giving $V_b\to V_aC_a/C_b=V_{\mathrm{eq}}$ — the stoichiometric volume of §1, recovered exactly. The boxed equation is the engine behind the widget below, which sweeps pH and reads off volume.

Reading volume from pH, not pH from volume

The trick that makes the curve trivial is that pH appears nonlinearly when you solve for it from volume (a cubic in $\conc{H^+}$), but volume appears linearly when you solve for it from pH. So we choose pH freely and let the formula hand back the volume. The plotted curve looks identical either way; it is only the bookkeeping that becomes painless.

§5Indicators

Before pH meters, the endpoint was found by colour. An indicator is itself a weak acid, $\mathrm{HIn}$, whose two forms happen to absorb light differently:

$$ \mathrm{HIn} \;\eqm\; \mathrm{H^+} + \mathrm{In^-}, \qquad K_{\mathrm{In}} = \frac{\conc{H^+}\conc{In^-}}{\conc{HIn}}. $$

Because it is a weak acid, it obeys the same Henderson–Hasselbalch logic as everything else:

$$ \pH = \mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{In}} + \log_{10}\frac{\conc{In^-}}{\conc{HIn}}. $$

Where

$\mathrm{HIn},\ \mathrm{In^-}$
The acid (one colour) and conjugate base (another colour) forms of the indicator dye.
$\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{In}}$
$-\log_{10}K_{\mathrm{In}}$ — the pH at which the two coloured forms are present in equal amounts.

The eye reads the dominant colour. The two forms are roughly equally visible when their ratio lies between about $1\!:\!10$ and $10\!:\!1$, i.e. when $\log_{10}(\conc{In^-}/\conc{HIn})$ runs from $-1$ to $+1$. So the visible colour change spans a transition range

$$ \pH \approx \mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{In}} \pm 1. $$

The art is to pick an indicator whose transition range brackets the equivalence-point pH, so that the colour flips inside the vertical jump. For our acetic-acid titration, with $\pH_{\mathrm{eq}}\approx8.72$, phenolphthalein ($\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{In}}\approx9.4$, range $8.0$–$10.0$) is the standard choice: colourless in acid, pink as the pH sweeps up through the endpoint. Methyl orange (range $3.1$–$4.4$) would flip far too early here, but is perfectly suited to a strong-acid titration, whose jump straddles pH 7 and passes through that lower range. Choosing the wrong indicator is the classic source of titration error.

§6What makes an endpoint detectable

An endpoint is usable only if the pH changes fast enough at equivalence that a drop of titrant produces an unmistakable signal. Two things govern that steepness.

Concentration. The size of the jump grows with how concentrated both solutions are. Dilute everything tenfold and the leftover and excess ion concentrations near equivalence shrink tenfold too, flattening the jump. Below roughly $10^{-3}\Msol$ the jump for a weak acid all but disappears into the baseline set by water itself.

Acid strength. For a weak acid the jump shrinks as $\Ka$ falls. Quantitatively, the pH change brackets the equivalence point between roughly the buffer pH (near $\pKa$) below and the strong-base region above; the weaker the acid, the higher its pre-equivalence pH starts and the smaller the gap left to jump. A useful rule of thumb is that a sharp endpoint requires roughly

$$ C_a\,\Ka \gtrsim 10^{-8}\,, $$

below which the equivalence-point pH and the buffer pH have closed in on each other and no clear inflection survives. This is why very weak acids (phenol, $\pKa\approx10$) cannot be titrated cleanly in water — water's own basicity levels the signal, just as it levels strong acids in Chapter II. The widget makes this concrete: slide $\pKa$ upward and watch the jump melt away.

§7Gran plots and the derivative endpoint

When the jump is shallow, or when you want the equivalence point without measuring near it (where electrode response is sluggish), two numerical methods beat eyeballing the curve.

The derivative method notes that the inflection point — the steepest part of the curve — is where $\mathrm{d(pH)}/\mathrm{d}V$ peaks, equivalently where $\mathrm{d^2(pH)}/\mathrm{d}V^2$ crosses zero. Plotting the first derivative turns the search for a vertical region into the search for a sharp maximum, which the eye and the computer both locate well.

The Gran plot (Gran, 1952) is subtler and more robust. Before the equivalence point of a strong acid, $\conc{H^+}=(C_aV_a-C_bV_b)/(V_a+V_b)$, so the product $(V_a+V_b)\,\conc{H^+}$ is a linear function of $V_b$ that hits zero exactly at $V_{\mathrm{eq}}$. Since $\conc{H^+}=10^{-\pH}$, one plots

$$ (V_a + V_b)\,10^{-\pH} \quad\text{versus}\quad V_b, $$

Where

$(V_a+V_b)\,10^{-\pH}$
The Gran function before equivalence; proportional to the moles of $\mathrm{H^+}$ remaining, hence linear in $V_b$.
$V_b$
Titrant volume added — the horizontal axis of the Gran plot.

and extrapolates the straight line to the volume axis; the intercept is $V_{\mathrm{eq}}$. (A simpler $V_b\,10^{-\pH}$ versus $V_b$ works similarly when dilution is slight.) The beauty of Gran's construction is that it uses data taken away from the endpoint — where measurements are stable and the indicator-free probe behaves linearly — to pin the endpoint precisely, sidestepping the very region that is hardest to measure. The same idea, applied past equivalence, locates $V_{\mathrm{eq}}$ from the excess-base branch, and a matching weak-acid Gran function exists for the conjugate-base region.

With the curve fully in hand — its shape, its landmarks, its master equation, and the tools to read its endpoint — we are ready to follow what happens when an acid carries more than one proton. Each proton produces its own buffer region and its own equivalence point, and the speciation that results is the subject of the next chapter.
