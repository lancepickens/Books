Chapter III

Equilibrium in Water

Every pH problem is the same question — where do all the charges and all the atoms have to balance? — and the famous shortcuts are just that question answered lazily.

Before we start

In Chapter II we estimated the pH of a weak acid with a single tidy formula, $[\mathrm{H}^+]\approx\sqrt{K_{\mathrm{a}}C}$. That formula is a guess that happens to be good most of the time. This chapter explains where it comes from, exactly how good it is, and — more usefully — when it quietly fails. The trick is to stop reaching for a memorized formula and instead write down two statements that are always true: atoms are conserved (mass balance) and charge is conserved (charge balance). Combine them with the equilibrium constants and the answer falls out, with no approximation at all.

Done honestly, that combination gives a cubic equation for the hydrogen-ion concentration. We will derive it, solve it numerically in the widget, and then peel away terms one at a time to recover the simple shortcuts — seeing precisely which term you are allowed to drop, and when. Along the way the awkward cases that break the shortcut announce themselves: solutions so dilute or so weak that water's own ions matter, salts that make water basic or acidic on their own, and the strange "in-between" salts whose pH does not depend on how much you dissolve at all.

§1The systematic approach: mass and charge balance

A solution of a weak acid is not just $\mathrm{HA}$ and $\mathrm{A}^-$. It also contains $\mathrm{H}^+$ and $\mathrm{OH}^-$ from the water, and whatever cations came with the acid. To find the pH exactly we need as many independent equations as there are unknown concentrations. Two of them are not chemistry at all — they are bookkeeping that must hold in every aqueous solution.

The first is mass balance (or material balance): the total amount of the acid you dissolved, the analytical concentration $C$, is still there, just split between its two forms.

$$ C = \conc{HA} + \conc{A^-}. $$

Where

$C$
Analytical (formal) concentration of the acid in $\mathrm{mol\,L^{-1}}$ — a known input, fixed when you weigh out the acid.
$\conc{HA}$
Equilibrium concentration of the undissociated acid (an unknown).
$\conc{A^-}$
Equilibrium concentration of the conjugate base (an unknown).

The second is charge balance, the statement that the solution carries no net charge. Summing every cation against every anion (each weighted by the magnitude of its charge) for a pure weak acid in water gives

$$ \conc{H^+} = \conc{A^-} + \conc{OH^-}. $$

Where

$\conc{H^+}$
Hydrogen-ion (hydronium) concentration — the only cation present, and the quantity we ultimately want.
$\conc{A^-}$
The acid's anion.
$\conc{OH^-}$
Hydroxide, always present from the water.

If a spectator cation is present — say sodium, from sodium acetate — it joins the left side. For a salt $\mathrm{MA}$ dissolving alongside the acid,

$$ \conc{H^+} + \conc{M^+} = \conc{A^-} + \conc{OH^-}, $$

and $\conc{M^+}$ is known (it equals the salt's formal concentration, since a spectator ion does not react). Mass balance, charge balance, the equilibrium $K_{\mathrm{a}}$, and $K_{\mathrm{w}}$ together give four equations in the four unknowns $\conc{HA}$, $\conc{A^-}$, $\conc{H^+}$, $\conc{OH^-}$. The system is closed; everything else in this chapter is algebra.

§2The proton condition

Charge balance and mass balance can be combined into a single, especially intuitive statement called the proton condition (or proton balance). The idea: pick a reference level — the species you actually put in the beaker, in the form you put them in — and then tally everything that has gained a proton against everything that has lost one. Because protons are only shuffled around, never created, the two tallies must match.

For a weak acid the reference level is $\mathrm{HA}$ and $\mathrm{H_2O}$ — the things present before any reaction. Relative to that level, $\mathrm{H_3O^+}$ is water that has gained a proton, while $\mathrm{A}^-$ is $\mathrm{HA}$ that has lost one and $\mathrm{OH}^-$ is water that has lost one. Proton gains equal proton losses:

$$ \conc{H^+} = \conc{A^-} + \conc{OH^-}. $$

This is identical to the charge balance above — which is no accident, and a good sanity check. For this simple system the two routes coincide; in messier systems (salts, ampholytes) the proton condition is often the quickest way to the right equation, because it sidesteps the spectator ions entirely.

Why the reference level matters

The proton condition for a salt of the acid, say sodium acetate, uses a different reference level: $\mathrm{A}^-$ and $\mathrm{H_2O}$, because acetate is what you dissolved. Now $\mathrm{HA}$ is acetate that has gained a proton, so the balance reads $\conc{HA}+\conc{H^+}=\conc{OH^-}$. Same solution chemistry, different starting form, different proton condition — and it correctly predicts a basic solution, since the right side must exceed $\conc{H^+}$.

§3The exact equation for a weak monoprotic acid

Now we turn the four equations into one. Start from the charge (proton) balance and replace each species using the equilibria. From $K_{\mathrm{a}} = \conc{H^+}\conc{A^-}/\conc{HA}$ and the mass balance $\conc{HA}=C-\conc{A^-}$ we get $\conc{A^-} = K_{\mathrm{a}}\conc{HA}/h$ with $h\equiv\conc{H^+}$, and from $K_{\mathrm{w}}$ we get $\conc{OH^-}=K_{\mathrm{w}}/h$. Substituting into $h = \conc{A^-}+\conc{OH^-}$ and clearing denominators yields a cubic in $h$:

$$ h^3 + \Ka\,h^2 - (\Ka C + \Kw)\,h - \Ka \Kw = 0. $$

Where

$h$
$\equiv\conc{H^+}$, the single unknown; the physically meaningful root is the unique positive one.
$\Ka$
Acid dissociation constant (dimensionless, standard state $1\,\mathrm{M}$).
$C$
Analytical concentration of the acid.
$\Kw$
Ion product of water, $1.0\times10^{-14}$.

This is the exact answer for any monoprotic weak acid at any concentration: nothing has been dropped. (The same cubic, with the leading and trailing terms inspected, also covers strong acids in the limit $K_{\mathrm{a}}\to\infty$ and very dilute solutions where $K_{\mathrm{w}}$ matters.) A cubic has no convenient closed form worth memorizing, so we solve it numerically — by bisection, exactly as the widget does. The point of writing the cubic is not to solve it by hand; it is to know what the shortcuts are approximating.

One positive root

Because $h$ is a concentration it must be positive, and Descartes' rule of signs (one sign change in the coefficients $+,+,-,-$) guarantees exactly one positive root. So "solve the cubic" really means "find the one root between $0$ and roughly $C$" — a job bisection does flawlessly.

§4The approximation hierarchy

Now peel the cubic apart. The two terms with $K_{\mathrm{w}}$ are the contribution of water's own ions. In any solution that is even mildly acidic, $\conc{OH^-}=K_{\mathrm{w}}/h$ is tiny compared with $\conc{A^-}$, so dropping the $K_{\mathrm{w}}$ terms is the first and safest simplification. The proton balance collapses to $\conc{H^+}=\conc{A^-}$, and the cubic becomes a quadratic:

$$ \conc{H^+} = \sqrt{\Ka\,(C - \conc{H^+})} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \conc{H^+} = \frac{-\Ka + \sqrt{\Ka^2 + 4\,\Ka C}}{2}. $$

The square-root inside keeps the $-\conc{H^+}$ correction: it acknowledges that the acid you have left, $C-\conc{H^+}$, is a little less than $C$ because some has dissociated. Keeping that term is necessary whenever the acid is appreciably dissociated.

The final, most aggressive step assumes dissociation is slight, $\conc{H^+}\ll C$, so $C-\conc{H^+}\approx C$. That recovers the Chapter II shortcut:

$$ \conc{H^+} \approx \sqrt{\Ka C}, \qquad \pH \approx \half\!\left(\pKa - \log_{10} C\right). $$

When is that last leap legitimate? The discarded quantity is $\conc{H^+}$ relative to $C$. The convention is the 5% rule: the approximation is acceptable if the fraction dissociated

$$ \alpha = \frac{\conc{A^-}}{C} = \frac{\Ka}{\Ka + \conc{H^+}} \;\le\; 0.05. $$

Where

$\alpha$
Fraction of the acid that has dissociated; runs from $0$ (no dissociation) to $1$ (complete).
$\Ka,\,\conc{H^+}$
As above; note $\alpha$ rises as the solution is diluted, because $\conc{H^+}$ falls while $\Ka$ stays fixed.

For $0.10\,\mathrm{M}$ acetic acid ($\pKa = 4.76$, $\Ka = 1.75\times10^{-5}$), the simple formula gives $\conc{H^+}=\sqrt{1.75\times10^{-5}\times0.10}=1.32\times10^{-3}\,\mathrm{M}$, i.e. $\pH\approx 2.88$, and $\alpha\approx 1.3\%$ — comfortably under 5%, so the shortcut is excellent. The quadratic gives $\conc{H^+}=1.31\times10^{-3}\,\mathrm{M}$, a difference in the fourth figure. But dilute the same acid to $1\times10^{-4}\,\mathrm{M}$ and $\alpha$ climbs past 30%: now the $-\conc{H^+}$ term is mandatory, and soon even the quadratic is not enough.

§5Very dilute or very weak acids: water fights back

The quadratic still assumed water's ions were negligible. That assumption fails when the acid is so dilute or so weak that the $\mathrm{H}^+$ it supplies is comparable to the $10^{-7}\,\mathrm{M}$ that water supplies on its own. Then you cannot drop the $K_{\mathrm{w}}$ terms, and the pH bends back toward 7 — a weak acid can never push the pH above neutral.

The cleanest illustration is a strong acid at vanishing concentration, where $\mathrm{HA}$ is fully dissociated so $\conc{A^-}=C$ exactly and the only equilibrium left is water's. Charge balance $\conc{H^+}=C+\conc{OH^-}=C+K_{\mathrm{w}}/h$ becomes

$$ h^2 - C\,h - \Kw = 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad h = \frac{C + \sqrt{C^2 + 4\Kw}}{2}. $$

Put in $C = 1\times10^{-7}\,\mathrm{M}$ of $\mathrm{HCl}$: $h = \big(10^{-7} + \sqrt{10^{-14}+4\times10^{-14}}\big)/2 = 1.62\times10^{-7}\,\mathrm{M}$, so $\pH = 6.79$ — distinctly acidic, but not the pH $7.00$ the naive $-\log_{10}C$ would (absurdly) give, and certainly not the pH $7$ you would get by forgetting the acid entirely. Water's own $\mathrm{H}^+$ adds to the acid's. The widget lets you sweep $C$ downward and watch any acid's curve peel away from the straight $\half(\pKa-\log_{10}C)$ line and level off near 7.

The pH-8 trap

If a careless calculation ever returns a pH above 7 for an acid (or below 7 for a base), the $K_{\mathrm{w}}$ terms were dropped illegitimately. The exact equation never makes that mistake: as $C\to 0$ its positive root approaches $\sqrt{K_{\mathrm{w}}}=10^{-7}\,\mathrm{M}$, pH 7, from the acidic side.

§6Weak bases and salts of weak acids

Dissolve sodium acetate and the solution turns basic. The sodium is a spectator; the acetate is the conjugate base of a weak acid, and it takes a proton from water — the reaction called hydrolysis:

$$ \mathrm{A^-} + \mathrm{H_2O} \;\eqm\; \mathrm{HA} + \mathrm{OH^-}, \qquad \Kb = \frac{\Kw}{\Ka}. $$

Where

$\Kb$
Base constant of $\mathrm{A}^-$, fixed by the conjugate relationship $K_{\mathrm{a}}K_{\mathrm{b}}=K_{\mathrm{w}}$ from Chapter II.
$\conc{OH^-}$
Hydroxide produced by hydrolysis — now the dominant ion.

The algebra mirrors the weak acid exactly, with $\mathrm{OH}^-$ playing the role $\mathrm{H}^+$ played before. The analogous shortcut, valid under the same 5% condition, is

$$ \conc{OH^-} \approx \sqrt{\Kb\,C}, \qquad \pOH \approx \half\!\left(\pKb - \log_{10} C\right), \qquad \pH = \pKw - \pOH. $$

For $0.10\,\mathrm{M}$ sodium acetate: $\Kb = K_{\mathrm{w}}/K_{\mathrm{a}} = 10^{-14}/1.75\times10^{-5} = 5.7\times10^{-10}$, so $\conc{OH^-} = \sqrt{5.7\times10^{-10}\times0.10} = 7.5\times10^{-6}\,\mathrm{M}$, giving $\pOH = 5.12$ and $\pH = 8.88$ — mildly basic, as the conjugate base of a weak acid should be. The mirror case is a salt like ammonium chloride: $\mathrm{NH_4^+}$ is the conjugate acid of the weak base ammonia, so it hydrolyses to give an acidic solution, $\conc{H^+}\approx\sqrt{K_{\mathrm{a}}C}$ with $K_{\mathrm{a}}=K_{\mathrm{w}}/K_{\mathrm{b}}$. In the widget, choosing "weak base" solves the same exact balance for $\mathrm{OH}^-$ and reports the pH.

§7Amphiprotic salts

The most surprising case is an amphiprotic salt — the middle species of a diprotic system, such as sodium bicarbonate ($\mathrm{HCO_3^-}$) or sodium dihydrogen phosphate ($\mathrm{H_2PO_4^-}$). Bicarbonate can lose a proton (acting as the acid of the pair $\mathrm{HCO_3^-}/\mathrm{CO_3^{2-}}$, constant $K_{\mathrm{a2}}$) or gain one (acting as the base of the pair $\mathrm{H_2CO_3}/\mathrm{HCO_3^-}$, constant $K_{\mathrm{a1}}$). Writing the proton condition with $\mathrm{HCO_3^-}$ as the reference level and applying the usual simplifications, the two competing tendencies nearly cancel and the result is remarkably clean:

$$ \conc{H^+} \approx \sqrt{K_{\mathrm{a}1} K_{\mathrm{a}2}} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \pH \approx \half\!\left(\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}1} + \mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}2}\right). $$

Where

$K_{\mathrm{a}1},\,K_{\mathrm{a}2}$
The first and second dissociation constants of the parent diprotic acid; the amphiprotic ion sits between them.
$\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}1},\,\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}2}$
Their negative logs; their average is the pH.

The astonishing feature is that $C$ has dropped out: to this approximation the pH of an amphiprotic salt does not depend on its concentration. For sodium bicarbonate, with $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}1} = 6.35$ and $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}2} = 10.33$ for carbonic acid, $\pH \approx \half(6.35 + 10.33) = 8.34$ — close to the value measured for a baking-soda solution across a wide range of concentrations. (The exact treatment restores a weak concentration dependence and corrections from $K_{\mathrm{w}}$ and $K_{\mathrm{a1}}$; the formula above is the standard first approximation, and it is the one to reach for.)

Where the formula comes from

For the ampholyte $\mathrm{HA}^-$ the full result is $\conc{H^+}=\sqrt{\dfrac{K_{\mathrm{a1}}(K_{\mathrm{a2}}C + K_{\mathrm{w}})}{K_{\mathrm{a1}}+C}}$. When $C\gg K_{\mathrm{a1}}$ and $K_{\mathrm{a2}}C\gg K_{\mathrm{w}}$ — usually true — the $C$'s cancel and the $K_{\mathrm{w}}$ drops, leaving $\conc{H^+}\approx\sqrt{K_{\mathrm{a1}}K_{\mathrm{a2}}}$. The cancellation of $C$ is exactly the surprising concentration-independence.
