A Primer

Acids and Bases

The chemistry of a single proton — where it sits, how tightly it is held, and how we move it — in ten chapters, with something to play with in each.

10 chapters · in browser · self-contained

  1. I Three Lenses on Acidity Arrhenius, Brønsted–Lowry, and Lewis — conjugate pairs, the hydronium ion, and the autoionization of water.
  2. II The pH Scale and the Strength of Acids pH and pOH, the equilibrium constants $K_{\mathrm{a}}$ and $K_{\mathrm{b}}$, the $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$ ladder, and the leveling effect of water.
  3. III Equilibrium in Water Mass and charge balance, the proton condition, ICE tables, and when the textbook approximations are safe to make.
  4. IV Buffers The Henderson–Hasselbalch equation, buffer capacity, and the bicarbonate buffer that keeps your blood at pH 7.4.
  5. V Titrations and Indicators Titration curves derived exactly, the equivalence and half-equivalence points, indicators, and Gran plots.
  6. VI Polyprotic Acids and Speciation Stepwise dissociation, distribution (alpha) functions, log-concentration diagrams, and the carbonate system.
  7. VII The Molecular Origins of Acidity What sets a $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$: bond strength, electronegativity, induction, resonance, solvation, and the Hammett equation.
  8. VIII Beyond Water: Solvents and Superacids Autoprotolysis in other solvents, the differentiating effect, the Hammett acidity function $H_0$, and the superacids.
  9. IX Lewis Acids, Hardness, and Catalysis Electron-pair acceptors, hard and soft acids and bases, frontier orbitals, and acid catalysis from zeolites to FLPs.
  10. X Computing pKa Thermodynamic cycles, implicit solvation, the proton's solvation free energy, and machine-learned $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$ prediction.

Conventions used throughout: concentrations $[\mathrm{X}]$ are in mol·L⁻¹ (M), referred to the standard state $c^{\circ} = 1\,\mathrm{M}$, so equilibrium constants are dimensionless. Temperature is $25\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ ($298.15\,\mathrm{K}$) unless noted, where $K_{\mathrm{w}} = 1.0\times10^{-14}$ and $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{w}} = 14.00$. We write $\mathrm{pH} = -\log_{10} a_{\mathrm{H}^+} \approx -\log_{10}[\mathrm{H}^+]$, taking activities equal to concentrations in dilute solution (flagged where it matters). $\mathrm{H}^+$ and $\mathrm{H_3O^+}$ both denote the hydrated proton. $\log$ means $\log_{10}$; $\ln$ is natural. Energies are in kJ·mol⁻¹, with $\Delta G^{\circ} = -RT\ln K$ and $2.303\,RT = 5.71\,\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$. Citations follow Harris (Quantitative Chemical Analysis) and Atkins for aqueous equilibria, Anslyn–Dougherty for $\mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{a}}$ trends, and Housecroft–Sharpe for Lewis and inorganic acidity unless noted.
